What makes quantum computing different?

Most computers today are classical. They process information using bits, which are either 0 or 1.

Quantum computers use qubits. A qubit can be:
- 0
- 1
- both at the same time (this is called superposition)

That single difference changes how computation works.

Three core ideas (no maths required)

1. Superposition — many possibilities at once
A classical bit is like a coin lying flat: heads or tails.
A qubit is like a coin spinning in the air: it represents both until you look at it.

This means a quantum computer can explore many possible solutions simultaneously, rather than one at a time.

2. Entanglement — linked behaviour
Qubits can become entangled, meaning their states are connected.

If two qubits are entangled, changing one instantly affects the other, even if they are far apart.
This allows coordinated computation across multiple qubits in ways classical systems cannot replicate.

3. Interference — amplifying the right answer
Quantum systems can combine probabilities so that:
- wrong answers cancel out
- correct answers become more likely

Think of it as tuning a signal: the computer nudges the system so the correct solution "stands out" when measured.

What problems does this help with?

Quantum computers are not faster at everything. They are specialised.

They are promising for problems where possibilities grow exponentially, such as:
- Chemistry and materials: simulating molecules (e.g. drug discovery)
- Optimisation: logistics, scheduling, energy grids
- Cryptography: breaking or creating new encryption methods

For everyday tasks (email, spreadsheets), classical computers remain more efficient.

A useful mental model

A classical computer:

tries one path at a time, very quickly

A quantum computer:

explores many paths at once, then uses interference to favour the best one

Current reality
- Quantum computers exist, but are early-stage (often called "NISQ" — noisy intermediate-scale quantum)
- They are fragile: qubits are easily disrupted by noise and temperature
- Practical, large-scale advantage is still limited to specific experimental cases

Key limitation to be aware of

The "parallelism" idea is often overstated.
You do not directly read all possible answers — measurement gives one result, so algorithms must be carefully designed to make that result meaningful.
