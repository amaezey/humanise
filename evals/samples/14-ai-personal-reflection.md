I spent three years building a startup that failed, and I learned more from that failure than from anything else in my career.

The idea was simple: a platform that connected freelance designers with small businesses that couldn't afford agency rates. We called it DesignBridge. The pitch was clean, the market was real, and the early traction was encouraging. We signed up 200 designers in the first month and had 40 paying clients by month three.

What went wrong

The short answer is that we built the wrong thing for the right market. The longer answer is more instructive.

We assumed that small businesses wanted a marketplace — a place to browse portfolios, compare prices, and hire designers on a project basis. What they actually wanted was a relationship. They didn't want to evaluate five designers for every project. They wanted one designer who understood their brand, their voice, and their constraints. They wanted continuity, not choice.

We discovered this through a painful series of conversations with churning customers. "The platform works fine," they'd tell us. "We just don't need it anymore. We found someone we like and now we work with them directly."

We weren't being disrupted by a competitor. We were being disrupted by the relationships we'd facilitated.

What I'd do differently

I've thought about this constantly since we shut down, and three things stand out.

First, I would have talked to customers before building anything. We spent four months building a marketplace platform based on assumptions we never tested. Four months of engineering, design, and infrastructure for a product whose core premise was wrong. If we'd spent two weeks doing customer interviews first, we'd have built something entirely different.

Second, I would have charged from day one. Our free tier attracted users who weren't serious about the problem we were solving. The clients who paid from the start were the ones who gave us the most useful feedback and stayed the longest. Free users optimise for convenience; paying users optimise for value.

Third, I would have been more honest with myself about the metrics. Our dashboard showed growth: more signups, more projects posted, more messages sent. But underneath those numbers, repeat usage was declining. The users who mattered — the ones who'd found their designer — stopped coming back. I saw this in the data but I didn't want to see it, because the top-line numbers still looked good.

What failure taught me

The most important lesson wasn't about product-market fit or pricing strategy or customer development methodology. It was about intellectual honesty.

Building a startup is an exercise in motivated reasoning. You want it to work, so you find evidence that it's working. You dismiss the signals that suggest otherwise. You tell yourself the story you need to hear to keep going.

That instinct is necessary — you can't build anything hard without some irrational optimism. But it becomes dangerous when it prevents you from seeing what's actually happening. The founders who succeed aren't the ones who never fail. They're the ones who can hold optimism and honesty in the same hand, who can believe in what they're building while remaining genuinely curious about whether it's working.

I haven't started another company yet. I might. But when I do, I'll build the smallest possible version first, charge real money from day one, and pay more attention to the users who leave than the ones who arrive.
