1 Write a 1200-word essay for a wellness publication about the relationship
  between sleep and grief. Personal but research-aware.
The hour grief comes looking

Grief has its own clock. It does not respect the ordinary architecture of a day: work, meals, messages, errands, sleep. In the first weeks after a loss, night can stop being a place of rest and become a second room where the dead feel closer, the mind gets louder, and the body refuses the simple mercy of switching off.

This is one of the crueller parts of grief. The person most in need of rest is often the least able to get it.

Research supports what many grieving people already know in their bones: bereavement and sleep disturbance are closely linked. A systematic review found a high prevalence of sleep problems after bereavement, with stronger grief often associated with greater sleep difficulty. It also found higher rates of sleep disturbance among people experiencing complicated or prolonged grief, particularly where depression is also present.

That matters because sleep is not just a pause from consciousness. It is an active biological process that affects memory, emotional regulation, attention, immune function and the body’s capacity to recover.   When grief disrupts sleep, it does not simply make us tired. It can change the texture of grief itself. Everything becomes harder to metabolise. Small decisions feel punitive. Social contact becomes more effortful. The ordinary world can seem too bright, too fast, too indifferent.

After a death, insomnia is not always dramatic. Sometimes it is a clean, brutal wakefulness at 3 am. Sometimes it is the inability to fall asleep because the mind keeps replaying the last conversation, the hospital room, the phone call, the thing said badly, the thing never said. Sometimes sleep comes easily but breaks apart before dawn. Sometimes the body sleeps for ten hours and still wakes exhausted.

There is a particular loneliness to being awake while everyone else is sleeping. During the day, grief may be held in place by tasks. There are forms to complete, people to update, meals to make, children to collect, work to return to too soon. At night, the scaffolding comes down. The mind has no meeting to attend, no polite sentence to finish. It begins its inventory.

This is why advice about “good sleep habits” can land badly with grieving people. A fixed bedtime, less caffeine, a dark room and no phone in bed are useful. Most adults need around 7 to 9 hours of sleep each night, and regular routines can support sleep quality.   But grief is not poor time management. It is not caused by bad lighting or an overused screen. A person who has lost someone may already know exactly what sleep hygiene requires and still be unable to obey it.

The better question is not “How do I force myself to sleep?” but “How do I make the night less hostile?”

That might mean lowering the stakes. If sleep does not come, rest still counts. Lying in the dark with one hand on the chest, listening to something familiar, moving to the couch for a while, or making tea without turning the kitchen into daylight can help the body remain in a quieter state. The goal is not to win against grief. It is to reduce the secondary panic that says, “Now I am grieving and failing at sleep.”

There is also a difference between remembering and ruminating, though they can feel identical at night. Remembering may hurt, but it has movement in it: a face, a phrase, a scene, a tenderness. Rumination loops. It prosecutes. It asks the same unanswerable question until the body becomes alert again. Writing can help, not because it solves anything, but because it gives the mind somewhere to put the loop. A notebook beside the bed can become a holding place for the thought that insists it must be dealt with at 2.17 am.

Dreams add another layer. Grieving people often report dreams in which the dead return with startling ordinariness. They are in the kitchen. They are wearing the old jumper. They have not died after all. Waking from these dreams can feel like losing the person again before breakfast. A dream can be comforting, but it can also make the day harder. Wellness culture often wants to interpret every dream as healing. Some dreams are not healing. Some are just the sleeping mind trying to process an impossible absence.

The research field is still developing. One review noted that, despite the obvious connection between bereavement and sleep, the literature has been relatively limited compared with related areas such as depression and trauma.   That limitation is important. It means we should be careful about neat claims. Poor sleep may intensify grief, and intense grief may disrupt sleep. The relationship can move in both directions. Recent work continues to examine this bidirectional pattern, especially in prolonged grief disorder.

For some people, sleep gradually returns as the nervous system begins to trust the world again. The first full night can feel almost disloyal. There is a strange guilt in waking rested. Grief can make rest feel like abandonment, as if vigilance were a form of love. But exhaustion is not devotion. Staying awake does not keep the person closer. It only keeps the body in distress.

For others, sleep problems persist and become their own condition. That is when support matters. Acute grief can include sadness, tearfulness and insomnia, and it may not require clinical treatment. But prolonged grief disorder describes grief that remains intense and disabling over time, preventing a person from adapting to the loss.   Persistent insomnia, especially when paired with depression, anxiety, intrusive thoughts or inability to function, deserves professional attention. This is not weakness. It is a health issue sitting inside a human loss.

The most compassionate approach to sleep in grief is therefore practical but not simplistic. Keep the room cool and dark. Limit alcohol, which can make sleep more fragmented even if it initially makes you drowsy. Be careful with late caffeine. Use a routine, not as a command, but as a signal to the body.   Let mornings carry some light and movement, even if both feel offensive at first. Tell someone if nights are becoming frightening.

And be wary of anyone who turns sleep into a moral achievement. Grief already gives people enough to feel guilty about. The aim is not perfect sleep. The aim is enough safety for the body to stand down, even briefly.

Over time, sleep can become one of the ways grief changes shape. Not disappears. Not resolves neatly. But changes. The bed becomes less like a witness stand. The dark becomes less accusatory. The first thought on waking is not always the loss. Some mornings, there is a second or two before memory returns. Later, there may be mornings when memory returns gently.

Sleep does not cure grief. It gives the grieving body a place to repair while the heart does harder work than anyone else can see.

   I realised my parents weren't perfect. First person, reflective. For a literary

The first crack in the porcelain

I was twenty-four when I realised my parents were not perfect. Not in a dramatic way. There was no slammed door, no revelation pulled from a drawer, no confession delivered across a kitchen table under a bad light. It happened in the ordinary weather of a Tuesday evening, while my father stood in the driveway holding a garden hose that had split near the nozzle, and my mother sat inside at the dining table, crying over an electricity bill.

The house looked exactly as it always had. The same brick path. The same agapanthus along the fence. The same bowl by the front door where keys and coins and old receipts collected in a minor domestic archaeology. Everything was intact, which is perhaps why the moment unsettled me so deeply. The setting had not changed. Only my sight had.

I had come home for dinner because I was tired and underfed and pretending not to be. My twenties had begun with the false confidence of someone who mistakes motion for direction. I had a job with an email signature, a rent payment that frightened me every fortnight, and a set of opinions about how life should be managed. I was old enough to see my parents’ habits as limitations, but not yet old enough to understand the labour that had made my own certainty possible.

Until then, I had carried an image of them that was almost architectural. They were the frame around everything. My father knew where the torch was, how to reverse a trailer, what to do when the hot water went cold. My mother remembered birthdays, medical histories, the names of teachers I had not thought about in years. Between them, they seemed to possess the entire operating manual for being alive.

That evening, the manual failed.

The hose burst while my father was watering the lemon tree. I heard the sound before I saw it, a frantic metallic hiss, then his short exhale of irritation. He turned the tap off too hard and the handle kept spinning loosely in his hand. He stared at it for a second, as if it had betrayed him personally.

Inside, my mother had opened the electricity bill and gone quiet in a way that made the room change temperature. She smoothed the paper with both hands, then folded it, unfolded it, and pressed her fingers to her mouth. When she looked up, she seemed not older exactly, but exposed. Her face had lost the composure I associated with her. She looked like someone who had reached the end of a calculation and found there was still more to pay.

My first reaction was annoyance. It shames me now, but it is true. I wanted them to handle it. I wanted the hose fixed, the bill absorbed, dinner started, the world returned to its proper scale. I had come home to be looked after. Instead, I found two people standing inside their own limits.

“Is it that bad?” I asked.

My mother said, “It’s fine.”

This was the first lie I recognised not as deception, but as reflex. She said it quickly, with the competence of long practice. Fine meant she had no answer yet. Fine meant she was trying to protect me from an anxiety I was already old enough to see. Fine meant she did not know how to say: yes, actually, this is difficult.

My father came in from the driveway with wet cuffs and a look of forced calm. He picked up the bill, scanned it, and made a small sound through his nose. Then he said something about calling them tomorrow. My mother said she had already called. He asked what they said. She said they said what they always said.

There was no argument. That almost made it worse. I had grown up thinking the great dangers were noise, rupture, visible unhappiness. I did not yet understand the quieter forms of strain: the spreadsheet, the postponed appointment, the envelope left unopened for two days, the adult conversation conducted in low voices after children have gone to bed.

I watched them speak past each other, each trying not to add to the other’s fear. My father wanted a practical problem because practical problems gave him a role. My mother wanted acknowledgement because acknowledgement, for her, was a kind of relief. Neither of them could give the other what they needed in that moment. Not because they lacked love. Because they lacked range.

That was the crack.

For years I had mistaken my parents’ strengths for completeness. My father’s competence had hidden his terror of helplessness. My mother’s care had hidden her resentment at having to notice everything. I had thought their flaws were irritations, things they could fix if they tried harder: his stubbornness, her worrying, his silence, her sharpness when tired. But sitting at that dining table, with the bill between them and water still dripping from my father’s trouser hem, I saw something more difficult. Their flaws were not decorative faults on otherwise finished people. They were part of the machinery. They were adaptations, old defences, compromises made so long ago they had become character.

I did not become kinder immediately. Realisation rarely improves us on contact. In fact, I think I became briefly worse. There is a particular cruelty available to people in their twenties, especially those who have just learned enough language to diagnose everyone else. I could name family dynamics, emotional avoidance, gendered labour. I could see the patterns clearly and felt, therefore, superior to them. I confused analysis with wisdom.

I wanted my parents to have read the books I had read, to have had the conversations I was having, to become fluent in a language they had never been taught. I wanted my father to say, “I feel frightened when I cannot fix things.” I wanted my mother to say, “I am exhausted by being the person who anticipates consequences.” Instead, he went back outside with a wrench, and she started peeling potatoes with more force than necessary.

That was how they knew how to continue.

Dinner was late. The potatoes were undercooked in the centre. Nobody mentioned the bill again. My father made a joke about the hose, and my mother gave him a look that was not quite amusement and not quite forgiveness. I remember watching them across the table and feeling a grief I could not explain. Nothing terrible had happened, and yet something had been lost.

What I lost was not love. It was exemption.

Children exempt their parents from personhood because they need them to be weatherproof. Even when we rebel against them, we often rebel against monuments. We accuse them of being controlling, embarrassing, old-fashioned, unreasonable, without fully understanding that these are not fixed properties. They are behaviours emerging from fear, fatigue, history, class, disappointment, biology, marriage, money, work, and the thousand small humiliations of adult life.

To see a parent as imperfect is one thing. To see them as contingent is another. They could have been otherwise. They were made, not issued.

After that night, memories rearranged themselves. My father sitting in the car after work before coming inside was no longer simply odd. It was a man taking three minutes of silence because the house would need him the second he opened the door. My mother checking the oven twice before we left for holidays was not just anxiety. It was the burden of being blamed, or blaming herself, if something went wrong. Their caution, their rules, their occasional emotional illiteracy: all of it had roots.

This did not absolve them of everything. That distinction mattered then, and matters more now. Understanding is not the same as excusing. Some wounds remain wounds even after you learn who held the knife and why their hand was shaking. But the moral geometry of my family changed. There were fewer clean lines. Less prosecution. More evidence.

In the years that followed, I noticed other things. My father sometimes did not know the answer. My mother sometimes made herself indispensable and then resented being needed. They both misremembered events in ways that protected them. They both gave advice shaped more by fear than by truth. They both loved me imperfectly, which is to say they loved me with the instruments they had.

That night in my twenties did not make me stop needing them. It made needing them more complicated. I could no longer collapse into the old fantasy that somewhere, behind the visible surface of ordinary life, my parents were still in charge. The adults were not elsewhere. There was no hidden committee. No one was coming with the complete manual.

There was only us, improvising.

For a while, that frightened me. Later, it became a form of mercy. If my parents were not perfect, then perhaps I did not have to become perfect before I could be decent. Perhaps adulthood was not a threshold crossed by competent people, but a room everyone enters unfinished. You learn where the torch is. You forget the thing you promised to remember. You pay the bill late. You say the wrong thing. You mend what can be mended and carry what cannot.

I think often of my father in the driveway, holding the broken hose as water ran across the concrete. I think of my mother at the table, pressing the bill flat with both hands. At twenty-four, I thought I was witnessing their failure. Now I think I was witnessing something closer to the truth: two people, tired and limited, trying to keep the house running anyway.

That was the moment they became smaller.

It was also the moment they became real.

Why The year of magical thinking should be reread every decade

Joan Didion’s The year of magical thinking is often described as a grief memoir, which is accurate but insufficient. It is also a book about evidence, memory, control, marriage, medical language, social performance, and the collapse of ordinary time. Its subject is the year after the sudden death of Didion’s husband, John Gregory Dunne, while their daughter, Quintana, was gravely ill. But its deeper achievement is that it changes shape as the reader ages. Read in one decade, it is a devastating account of bereavement. Read in another, it becomes a study of how fragile the structures of adult life really are. Later again, it reads as an anatomy of denial, a critique of competence, and a warning about the stories we tell ourselves to survive.

That is why it deserves to be reread every decade. Not because its meaning changes, exactly, but because the reader’s defences do.

The book begins with a sentence that now feels almost proverbial: life changes fast. Didion does not stage grief as an emotional revelation. She stages it as administrative shock. One evening in December 2003, after visiting Quintana in hospital, Didion and Dunne return to their New York apartment. They sit down to dinner. He has a drink. He says something ordinary. Then he is dead. The horror of the scene lies in its lack of drama. There is no warning, no moral preparation, no cinematic pause. Didion’s account is precise because precision is the only instrument she still trusts.

That precision matters. She remembers the fire, the table, the salad, the ambulance, the hospital, the paperwork. She reconstructs the event not only as a grieving widow but as a reporter. She seeks the timeline. She wants the medical explanation. She reads the records. She returns to the details because details imply sequence, and sequence implies cause. Cause, in turn, suggests that reality might still be governed by rules.

This is one reason the book rewards rereading. In early adulthood, Didion’s forensic attention can look like strength. She knows what to do. She calls the right people. She signs forms. She keeps functioning. A decade later, the same composure reads differently. It becomes recognisable as shock, even as a kind of magical thinking in itself: the belief that if events can be accurately ordered, they might somehow become less final.

The title’s “magical thinking” is not ornamental. It names the irrational logic by which grief tries to reverse the irreversible. Didion knows John is dead. She has seen the body. She has received the medical facts. Yet she cannot give away his shoes because he will need them when he returns. This is the book’s most famous example because it is so small and so devastating. It is not an extravagant delusion. It is domestic, practical, almost reasonable. A living man needs shoes. The mind accepts death abstractly while refusing its implications at the level of household management.

That scene lands differently across a life. At twenty-five, it may seem like an elegant symbol. At thirty-five or forty-five, after enough losses, it reads less like symbolism than documentary fact. Grief does not always announce itself as despair. It can appear as a logistical hesitation: keep the shoes, preserve the room, do not cancel the account, do not move the glass. The dead remain embedded in systems designed for the living.

Didion’s great cultural importance lies partly in her refusal to sentimentalise this. She does not turn grief into moral improvement. She does not claim loss made her wiser, kinder, or more authentic. The book is bracing because it rejects consolation as a literary obligation. She is not interested in uplift. She is interested in what the mind does under pressure.

That refusal feels more valuable now, in a culture saturated with therapeutic scripts. Contemporary public grief often arrives already shaped for display: tribute posts, anniversary captions, curated vulnerability, the language of healing and closure. Didion offers something more austere and more useful. She shows grief as repetitive, self-interrupting, intellectually humiliating. She circles the same dates, the same medical facts, the same memories. She catches herself bargaining with reality. She notices how often she searches for the mistake in the system, the overlooked sign, the moment at which things might have gone otherwise.

The “vortex effect” is central to this. Certain memories pull her out of the present and into an earlier life with John: Los Angeles, Malibu, Honolulu, working routines, family rituals, professional habits, private jokes. These are not decorative flashbacks. They show how marriage creates a shared archive so extensive that almost anything can trigger it. A place is not just a place. A hotel, a road, a hospital, a line from a novel, a weather pattern can become an opening into the lost life.

Reread every decade, this aspect of the book becomes more consequential. The first time, the reader may focus on the death. Later, the book’s real subject may appear to be marriage: not romance as an event, but intimacy as accumulated reference. Didion and Dunne’s marriage was also a working partnership, an editorial relationship, a long conversation. His absence is therefore not only emotional. It is cognitive. The person who helped her verify reality is gone.

That is one of the book’s sharpest insights. Grief is not only missing someone. It is losing a witness. John had been present for the formation of Didion’s adult life. Without him, her memories become unstable, not because they are false but because the person who could confirm their texture has disappeared. This is why the book is so exacting about memory. Didion does not trust it, but she cannot stop depending on it.

The book also deserves periodic rereading because it changes alongside our relationship to institutions. Hospitals occupy much of its emotional terrain. Quintana’s illness means Didion is navigating intensive care, diagnoses, specialist language, medical uncertainty and bureaucratic routines at the same time as she is absorbing John’s death. She learns the language of sepsis, pneumonia, intracranial pressure, induced coma. She becomes fluent in the vocabulary of crisis because fluency offers a thin form of control.

The medical scenes are not merely background. They expose the limits of expertise. Doctors know things Didion does not know, but they do not know everything she needs to know. They can explain mechanisms, not meanings. They can describe risk, not guarantee outcome. For readers who have spent more time with hospitals, ageing parents, chronic illness, disability systems or end-of-life care, these sections become more disturbing with each decade. The book understands that institutional language can be both necessary and unbearable. It gives names to events while failing to make them liveable.

Didion’s style is inseparable from this argument. Her sentences are clean but not simple. The repetitions, returns and abrupt transitions enact the experience they describe. She writes as someone trying to create order while documenting the failure of order. The result is a prose style that feels controlled and destabilised at once. That tension is why the book still matters to cultural readers, not only to readers seeking a book about grief. It is a formal achievement: a work in which structure becomes evidence.

There is also a harder reason to reread it. Each decade brings the reader closer to the book’s facts. Parents age. Partners become mortal in less theoretical ways. Friends die. Children become vulnerable adults. The body becomes less obedient. The systems that once seemed peripheral become central: hospitals, wills, emergency contacts, death certificates, intensive care units, insurance, memory itself. Didion’s book waits for the reader at each threshold.

It is tempting to call The year of magical thinking timeless, but that word can flatten it. The book is intensely situated: in New York, in a particular marriage, in a particular class world, in the medical and literary culture of the early 2000s. Its durability comes not from universality in the abstract, but from the accuracy with which Didion records a specific consciousness under extreme strain. She does not speak for everyone. She speaks exactly enough that others can recognise themselves.

To reread the book every decade is to submit to a test: what did you miss last time because you had not yet needed to know it? At one age, the unforgettable image is the shoes. At another, it is the dinner table. Later, it may be the hospital corridor, the medical record, the sudden unreliability of memory, or the terrible fact that ordinary life continues to look ordinary until it does not.

Didion’s achievement is not that she makes grief intelligible. She shows why it resists intelligence. She shows the mind attempting to negotiate with death through syntax, research, manners, memory and routine. She shows the bargain failing. Then she keeps looking.

That is why the book should not be left on the shelf as a classic once read and respectfully admired. It should be returned to, not for comfort, but for calibration. Every decade, it will measure something different: what has been lost, what is now feared, what can no longer be controlled, and what forms of magical thinking still remain.

4 Write a 1300-word reflective piece for a tech blog about why I've stopped using
  productivity apps. Personal, slightly contrarian, with practical observations.
Why I stopped using productivity apps

I used to believe the right productivity app would eventually fix the problem.

Not the obvious problem, which was that I had too much to do. The subtler problem: that I did not have a clean enough system for holding it all. Tasks lived in too many places. Notes were scattered. Ideas arrived while walking, showering, driving, scrolling, sitting in meetings, half-listening to myself promise something I knew I would forget unless I captured it immediately.

So I tried apps.

Task managers. Notes apps. Whiteboards. Kanban boards. Habit trackers. Calendar overlays. Focus timers. Personal knowledge management tools. Read-it-later apps. Browser extensions. Automations. Dashboards. Apps that promised simplicity. Apps that promised depth. Apps that promised to become the second brain I apparently needed because the first one was being throttled by work, life, tabs, notifications and vague dread.

For a while, each new tool worked. That is the trick of productivity software: the first week always feels like progress. You are not yet maintaining the system. You are imagining the person you will become once the system is complete.

You make categories. You tag things. You build a dashboard. You watch someone on YouTube explain their frictionless weekly review and think, yes, this is the missing layer. The app becomes a little model of a better self: calmer, more intentional, less reactive, never surprised by a deadline, never leaving a receipt in a tote bag or a document in the wrong folder.

Then reality resumes.

The task manager fills with stale intentions. The notes app becomes a storage unit for things you were once excited to think about. The habit tracker quietly records your failure until you stop opening it. The dashboard becomes another surface to check. The automation breaks. The tags proliferate. The system that was meant to reduce cognitive load becomes a small administrative job with no salary and no end date.

I did not stop using productivity apps because they are useless. Some are very good. I stopped because I finally admitted that I was using them to avoid a more uncomfortable question: what actually deserves my attention?

Most productivity apps are built around capture. Capture the task. Capture the idea. Capture the meeting note. Capture the article. Capture the insight. Capture the thing someone mentioned in passing that might matter later. Capture everything, because storage is cheap and forgetting feels irresponsible.

But capture is not the same as commitment.

A captured task still needs a decision. A saved article still needs time. A note still needs synthesis. A list still needs priority. Every item preserved for later becomes a tiny claim on the future. The app does not remove that claim. It just makes it look orderly.

That was the first practical observation: I was not overwhelmed because I lacked a reliable capture system. I was overwhelmed because I was capturing too much and deciding too little.

There is a particular relief in writing something down. It feels like moving it out of the mind. But if the thing matters, it returns. If it does not return, perhaps it did not matter enough. I do not mean this as a universal rule. Some work genuinely requires rigorous tracking. But in my own life, I had confused documentation with discernment. I kept preserving things to avoid having to choose.

The second observation was that productivity apps reward tidying, not doing.

This is not a moral failing. It is a design pattern. Apps have interfaces. Interfaces invite interaction. You can drag, sort, nest, colour, schedule, reschedule, link, archive, review and reframe. These actions feel constructive because they are concrete. They produce visible change. The list is cleaner. The board is aligned. The note has metadata. The project has a template.

Meanwhile, the hard thing remains untouched.

Writing the difficult email. Reading the dense document. Making the decision. Having the conversation. Starting the draft. Closing the loop.

Productivity theatre is still theatre even when the stage is beautifully designed.

The more elaborate my system became, the easier it was to spend the first hour of a workday preparing to work. I was not procrastinating in the usual sense. I was doing something adjacent to useful. That made it harder to challenge. It looked disciplined from the outside and felt responsible from the inside. But the practical outcome was the same: I had spent energy managing representations of work instead of doing the work itself.

The third observation was that most apps assume the future is more stable than it is.

A task entered on Monday can feel precise: draft proposal, review deck, call supplier, write brief. By Thursday, the context has changed. The meeting moved. Someone sent new information. The urgent thing became irrelevant. The small thing became political. The neat sequence collapsed.

Productivity apps tend to treat this as a planning issue. Build better workflows. Add dependencies. Use recurring reviews. Reprioritise.

Sometimes that helps. But often the problem is not that the plan needs maintenance. The problem is that the plan was a fiction. Useful, perhaps, but still a fiction.

A weekly plan can create orientation. It cannot eliminate volatility. When I tried to force volatile work into rigid systems, I generated more maintenance than clarity. My app was always asking me to reconcile yesterday’s assumptions with today’s conditions. Eventually, that reconciliation became its own workload.

The fourth observation was more personal: productivity apps made me more attentive to failure than momentum.

A completed task disappears. An overdue task glows.

This is rational from a software perspective. Systems need to show what still requires action. But psychologically, it meant I spent a lot of time looking at evidence of what I had not done. Even a productive day ended with the app reminding me of the residue. The list was never empty because the list was not a measure of effort. It was a measure of everything that could be imagined, requested, deferred or worried about.

That distinction matters.

A to-do list can be endless because possibility is endless. Capacity is not. Treating the list as the source of truth gives the list too much authority. It starts to feel as though the problem is personal inefficiency rather than structural excess.

This is where my view has become slightly contrarian: I think many productivity systems individualise what are often workload, priority and boundary problems.

If work is badly scoped, a better task manager will not fix it. If a team has no clear decision rights, a better notes system will not fix it. If every channel is treated as urgent, a focus app will not fix it. If you are trying to do too many things because saying no has social or professional costs, a habit tracker will not save you.

The app may help you survive the chaos. It may not help you question it.

That is not a reason to reject tools altogether. It is a reason to stop asking tools to solve organisational and human problems they can only display.

Since stepping away from productivity apps, my system is less impressive and more honest.

I use a calendar for commitments that involve time or other people. I keep a short written list for the day or week. I keep notes where they are useful, not where they satisfy an architecture. I do not try to capture every idea. I do not build dashboards for my life. I do not track habits unless there is a specific reason. I let some things be forgotten.

The last part is harder than it sounds.

There is a small arrogance in believing every thought should be preserved. There is also fear. Fear that forgetting means losing value. Fear that an uncaptured idea will prove important later. Fear that an untracked task will expose you as careless.

But memory has its own filtering function. So does recurrence. If something matters, it often reappears through consequence, conversation, discomfort or need. Not always. But often enough that I no longer treat total capture as a virtue.

My current approach is deliberately plain.

Each morning, I ask what would make the day meaningfully complete. Not ideal. Not maximally productive. Complete. Usually the answer is one to three things. Sometimes it is one difficult thing and several minor obligations. Sometimes it is simply staying ahead of a deadline without making the rest of the week worse.

I write those things down.

If new tasks arrive, I decide whether they belong today, later or nowhere. “Later” is not a magic category anymore. It has to earn its place. If I cannot imagine when I would do it, or why it matters, I try not to store it as a moral debt.

For reading and ideas, I have become more ruthless. I save less. I finish less. I trust skimming more. Not every useful article needs to become a note. Not every note needs to become an insight. Not every insight needs to be operationalised.

This has changed how I think about attention. I no longer see it as something to optimise through tooling. I see it as something to spend.

That framing is less glamorous, but more practical. Spending attention implies trade-offs. It makes waste visible. It also makes rest legitimate. You cannot spend continuously without depletion. No app can negotiate that physics on your behalf.

The irony is that stopping productivity apps has probably made me more productive, but not in the quantified, dashboard-friendly sense. I finish more important things because fewer trivial things get promoted into the system. I feel less compelled to maintain a perfect inventory of obligations. I notice sooner when the issue is not organisation but overcommitment. I have fewer places to look and fewer ways to pretend I am working.

The cost is that the system is not elegant. It does not scale infinitely. It does not produce satisfying analytics. It depends on judgement, which is inconvenient and cannot be fully automated.

But that is also the point.

A good tool should support judgement, not replace it. My problem was that I kept outsourcing judgement to systems that could only organise inputs. They could tell me what I had entered, tagged, dated and deferred. They could not tell me what mattered, what to abandon, what to renegotiate, or what kind of day I was actually capable of having.

So I stopped looking for the app that would make me a better version of myself.

I needed less infrastructure and more refusal. Less capture and more choice. Fewer lists and a clearer sense of what did not belong on them.

That is harder to package as software. It is also why it works.

The long weekend city

Lisbon does not reveal itself in a straight line. It rises, slips away, doubles back, and reappears from an angle you were not expecting. A long weekend there is enough time to understand very little, but to feel a great deal: the weight of the hills in your calves, the gloss of worn stone underfoot, the way light gathers on tiles and water until the whole city seems to be reflecting itself.

I arrived with the usual vague promises people make to themselves before travel. I would wander. I would not over-plan. I would eat properly, sleep enough, look up from my phone. Lisbon immediately made most of this feel unnecessary, because the city has its own sense of direction and seems faintly amused by yours. Streets that appear modest on a map turn into staircases. Alleys bend into courtyards. A quiet residential slope suddenly opens onto the Tagus, broad and pale and almost too bright to look at.

The first thing I noticed was the texture. Lisbon is not smooth. Its beauty is chipped, sun-struck, uneven. The pavements are patterned with small black and white stones, polished by weather and shoes into something treacherous and lovely. The buildings wear tiles like fabric: blue, green, yellow, cream, some precise and geometric, others floral and faded. In another city, these surfaces might feel decorative. In Lisbon, they feel practical, historical and intimate all at once, as though the walls themselves have learned to manage heat, salt air and time.

The light is the second thing. It does not simply illuminate Lisbon; it edits it. In the morning, the city feels washed clean, all soft facades and open shutters. By late afternoon, the sun hardens and deepens every surface. Windows flare. Laundry becomes flags. Shadows collect under balconies. The river turns from silver to tin to gold, depending on where you stand and how tired you are.

I spent much of the first day in Alfama, though “in” is the wrong word for a place that resists being entered neatly. Alfama is more like a sequence of encounters: a woman shaking a rug over a railing, a cat asleep beside a doorway, the smell of grilled sardines rising from somewhere below, a square no bigger than a room where older men sat in permanent committee. There were tourists, of course. I was one of them. But the neighbourhood still seemed to operate according to local rhythms, and part of the pleasure was knowing you could only ever be passing through.

At one lookout, the city spread beneath me in layers of terracotta, church towers and river haze. People took photographs in the familiar choreography of travel: stand here, turn slightly, pretend not to pose. I took my own, then put the phone away because the view did not improve by being captured. What stayed with me was not the panorama, but the sound around it: tram bells, low conversation, a busker’s guitar, gulls cutting through everything with their harsh, comic authority.

Lisbon’s trams are almost too picturesque for their own good. The yellow carriages, especially the famous old ones, look like props until one appears at speed on a narrow street and reminds you it is still a working machine. They rattle, lean and complain uphill, packed with bodies and bags and private discomfort. Watching one squeeze through a gap between parked cars and tiled walls, I understood why so many images of Lisbon show transport in motion. The city is not built for ease. Its charm depends partly on friction.

That friction is also cultural. Lisbon is often sold as gentle, affordable, sunlit and melancholy, a European capital softened for visitors. It is all of these things in moments, but it is also a city under pressure. In certain streets, the signs of tourism and investment are hard to miss: apartments converted to short stays, menus translated into five languages, shops selling the same ceramics, sardine tins and linen shirts in increasingly curated arrangements. The beauty is real, but so is the packaging of it.

This does not ruin the city. It complicates the visitor’s pleasure, which is probably useful. To love a place briefly is easy. To notice the conditions that make that brief love possible is harder. I found myself thinking about how travel often asks cities to perform their essence on demand. Lisbon, with its hills and azulejos and fado drifting from restaurant doors, is particularly vulnerable to being reduced to atmosphere. Yet the more interesting city was not the postcard one. It was the city doing ordinary things around the postcard: schoolchildren dragging bags over cobbles, tradesmen smoking beside vans, office workers eating alone at counters, residents navigating around people like me.

Food made this tension more forgiving. Not because meals solved anything, but because they brought me back to appetite and attention. One morning began with coffee and a pastel de nata eaten standing up. The custard was warm, blistered on top, soft enough to collapse but not quite surrender. Pastry shattered over my fingers. I had intended to compare bakeries, as if pleasure required a ranking system, then immediately lost interest in the project. The first tart was enough.

Later there was bacalhau, salty and dense, served in a room where the tablecloths were paper and the waiter seemed both impatient and kind. There was vinho verde, cold and faintly sharp. There were oranges stacked in a market, cuttlefish on ice, pyramids of tinned fish arranged with the precision of jewellery. There was bread that arrived without ceremony and disappeared the same way. Lisbon is not a city where I wanted elaborate food. I wanted things grilled, poured, sliced, passed across a counter.

By the second day, I had stopped pretending not to mind the hills. They changed the experience of time. Distances that looked small became bodily negotiations. You did not simply go somewhere; you climbed towards it, descended from it, paused halfway and reconsidered your character. The reward was that every climb altered the city. Lisbon seen from below is enclosed and granular. Lisbon seen from above is luminous and theatrical. The same street could feel domestic in the morning and cinematic by dusk.

In Bairro Alto, the city shifted again. During the day, its shutters and graffiti gave it a slightly hungover air, as though it were conserving energy. By evening, the lanes filled with people drinking in doorways, smoking on corners, spilling from bars too small to hold them. The sound was dense and multilingual. Bottles clinked. Someone laughed too loudly. Someone else sang badly and with conviction. I thought I would stay out longer than I did, but Lisbon had made me pleasantly tired, the sort of tired that feels earned rather than imposed.

The most moving part of the weekend was not a monument, although Lisbon has plenty of them. It was an hour near the river, watching the city loosen into evening. The Tagus is central to Lisbon’s imagination, not only as scenery but as memory: departure, trade, empire, longing, loss. Portugal’s maritime history is often presented in grand, polished forms, but standing by the water, what I felt most strongly was ambiguity. Rivers and harbours carry myths, but they also carry consequences. The same openness that made Lisbon expansive also made it complicit in histories that cannot be admired cleanly.

Perhaps that is why the city’s melancholy does not feel like a simple aesthetic. The Portuguese word saudade is too often handed to visitors as a convenient key, a way to explain the emotional weather of the place. I distrust words that travel brochures teach too quickly. Still, there is something in Lisbon that feels oriented towards absence: in fado’s dramatic ache, in the gaps between buildings, in the late light on empty balconies, in the sense that the river is always suggesting elsewhere.

On the final morning, I walked without much ambition. This is the best state in which to meet a city briefly. Ambition turns travel into acquisition: see this, eat that, photograph that, prove you were there. Lisbon rewarded the opposite. A tiled doorway. A bakery queue. A tram line shining in the road. A jacaranda beginning to purple the street. A woman leaning from a window to speak to someone below with the full force of her lungs.

A long weekend is not enough time to know Lisbon. It may be enough time to learn how it asks to be noticed. Not as a checklist of viewpoints, custard tarts and yellow trams, though those have their place, but as a city of gradients: steep and social, bright and worn, hospitable and strained, intimate and exposed. It gives generously, but not simply.

When I left, the city was still rising behind me, stacked against the sky. I had the feeling Lisbon often gives, of having missed things that were not exactly hidden. That may be its best argument for return. Not that there is more to do, though there is, but that there is more to notice once the obvious beauty has done its work and fallen away.

In praise of slow work

The first time I noticed how badly speed had colonised my attention, I was not at work. I was making soup.

It was an ordinary soup, the kind that begins with onions softening in oil. I had chopped them badly, because I was thinking about something else. I kept checking my phone between stirs, as if the onions might resent my full attention. The recipe said to cook them slowly until translucent, but I had turned the heat too high. They browned at the edges before they had properly softened. The soup was fine, in the way many rushed things are fine: edible, competent, unmemorable.

That is the problem with speed. It often produces something acceptable enough to pass. It rarely produces something with depth.

Creative work is not soup, but it is also not unrelated to soup. It depends on timing, heat, patience and the willingness to let materials change in their own way. A sentence needs time to reveal whether it is alive or merely polished. An idea needs time to gather contradiction around it. A painting, a poem, an essay, a design, a film, a piece of music: all of these are made not only through activity, but through waiting. To work slowly is not to work lazily. It is to allow the work to become more intelligent than the first impulse that produced it.

We live in a culture that treats speed as a moral quality. The fast worker is presumed to be disciplined, responsive, valuable. The slow worker must explain herself. Speed has become a proxy for competence, as if the shortest interval between request and output were the clearest measure of skill. This logic has always been present in industrial life, but it has intensified in creative work because creative work is now so often forced to behave like content.

Content must be regular. Content must be optimised. Content must be legible to platforms, calendars, audiences, metrics and clients. Content does not ripen; it is published. Its success is measured by circulation, not by what it does to the person who made it or the person who encounters it months later. The content economy has trained us to confuse production with expression, and expression with visibility. It asks not “What is this worth saying?” but “Can this be shipped by Thursday?”

The uncomfortable truth is that creative work can now be made very quickly. Tools have become astonishingly efficient. Drafts can be generated, edited, templated, scheduled and distributed at a pace that would once have seemed absurd. The question is no longer whether we can make more, faster. We plainly can. The more serious question is whether speed is making our work thinner.

In my own work, the answer is often yes. The fastest drafts tend to carry the residue of whatever language is most available: professional clichés, inherited structures, familiar rhythms, thoughts that sound correct because they have already been said too many times. Speed rewards the phrase nearest to hand. It rewards the idea that already knows how to present itself. It favours fluency over accuracy and polish over discovery.

Slowness, by contrast, is where embarrassment begins. That is one reason we avoid it. To sit with a draft for longer than efficiency requires is to notice how much of it is false. Not factually false, necessarily, but emotionally false, intellectually lazy, rhetorically inflated. Slowness exposes borrowed conviction. It shows where an argument is gesturing instead of thinking. It reveals the sentence that sounds beautiful but means very little.

This is why slowness is not merely an aesthetic preference. It is an ethical practice. When work is slow, it has more opportunities to become accountable to reality. It can absorb doubt. It can admit complexity. It can resist the easy shape that makes an idea pleasing but untrue.

The demand for speed is often defended as pragmatism. Deadlines are real. Money is real. Attention is scarce. Nobody gets to spend a decade carving a paragraph while the world burns. This is true, but it is also incomplete. The argument for slower creative work is not an argument against deadlines, discipline or publication. It is an argument against allowing urgency to become the default atmosphere in which everything is made.

Some work must be fast. A protest sign, a news response, a campaign, an emergency communication: these have their own dignity and necessity. Speed can be appropriate. It can even be beautiful when it carries force, clarity and purpose. But speed should be a chosen condition, not the permanent climate of creative life. When everything is urgent, nothing has the chance to become substantial.

There is a difference between urgency and momentum. Momentum can sustain work over time. Urgency consumes it. Momentum says: return tomorrow, and the next day, and the next. Urgency says: extract whatever is available now. Much contemporary creative work is built on extraction: from attention, from personal experience, from grief, from outrage, from identity, from the half-formed private life of the maker. The result is often work that feels immediate but not considered, revealing but not transformed.

The personal essay, in particular, has suffered from this. At its best, it is one of the most exacting forms: a mind meeting its own experience with discipline, suspicion and grace. At its worst, it becomes a rapid conversion machine, turning pain into narrative before the pain has been understood. Slowness matters here because experience is not insight. To have lived something is not yet to know what it means. Meaning arrives later, often inconveniently, after the first version has hardened into something too neat.

Some of the most important creative labour happens when nothing visible is being produced. Walking, reading badly, reading again, staring out a window, abandoning a structure, noticing irritation, changing one word and then changing it back. These are not romantic luxuries. They are part of the work. Yet they are difficult to defend in systems that recognise only outputs. A notebook full of fragments does not look productive. Neither does silence.

But silence is where the work often starts to resist us. It is where we stop performing productivity and begin listening for what the thing actually requires. A rushed work tends to obey its maker too quickly. A slower work begins to make demands of its own. It refuses the first ending. It asks for a different form. It reveals that the central image is not central at all. It exposes the fact that the argument is not about what we claimed it was about.

This is the part of creativity that cannot be made efficient without being damaged. There are tasks around creative work that can and should be streamlined. Administration, formatting, version control, transcription, research management: let these be as efficient as possible. But the core act of making meaning cannot be treated as a logistics problem. It is closer to cultivation than manufacture.

To defend slowness is also to defend taste. Taste is not simply preference; it is the capacity to perceive difference. It develops through exposure, attention and revision. A culture of speed weakens taste because it reduces the time available for comparison. We no longer ask whether the work is good. We ask whether it is ready. But ready and good are not the same thing. Ready means it can leave us. Good means it can survive leaving us.

Slower work is not guaranteed to be better. There is plenty of dull, overworked art. Time alone does not confer depth. A sentence can be laboured into lifelessness. A project can hide in refinement because its maker is afraid of judgement. Slowness can become avoidance, just as speed can become compulsion. The point is not to worship delay. The point is to recover duration as a legitimate creative condition.

The better question is not “How fast can this be done?” but “What kind of time does this work need?” Some pieces need the voltage of immediacy. Some need months of composting. Some need to be drafted quickly and revised slowly. Some need to be abandoned until the maker has become someone else. Creative maturity lies partly in learning the difference.

I have begun to distrust work that arrives too cleanly. Not always, but often. Cleanliness can signal craft, but it can also signal that nothing difficult has happened yet. The work has not been interrupted by a better thought. It has not had to give up its cleverness. It has not been made strange to itself. The pieces that matter most to me, as both writer and reader, usually contain some evidence of pressure: not mess, exactly, but density. They feel as if the maker stayed long enough for the obvious thing to break.

That is what slowness gives us: the chance for the obvious thing to break.

In an age that prizes speed, slow creative work will increasingly look inefficient, even indulgent. It may also become one of the few ways to make work that is not merely a reflection of the conditions that produced it. Speed gives us more of the present. Slowness gives us a chance to argue with it.

The onions, I have learned, need lower heat. They need time to collapse into sweetness before anything else is added. You can hurry them, and you will still get dinner. But you will not get the same soup. Creative work is like that. The difference is not always visible at first. It arrives later, in the depth, in the aftertaste, in what remains.

7Write a 1300-word essay for a craft magazine about taking up bread-making during
   a difficult year. Personal, sensory, reflective.

The slow work of bread

The year I took up bread-making was not the year I became a better person. That would be too neat a claim, and bread does not deserve to be burdened with that kind of symbolism. It was simply the year in which many of the ordinary structures of my life thinned out or broke, and I needed something small, repetitive and physical enough to keep me company.

At first, I did not think of it as craft. I thought of it as dinner.

Flour, water, salt, yeast. The list looked almost offensively modest. I had spent much of that year overwhelmed by things I could not simplify: illness in the family, work that expanded to fill every waking hour, the quiet dread of unanswered messages, the particular exhaustion that comes from making decisions when none of the options feel good. Bread offered the illusion of containment. Four ingredients. A bowl. A bench. A loaf tin if I felt cautious; a tray if I felt brave.

The first dough was a shaggy, reluctant thing. I mixed it with a wooden spoon until the flour disappeared into damp clumps, then turned it out onto the bench and pressed my hands into it with more force than skill. It stuck to my fingers, to the heel of my palm, to the laminate. I added more flour, then too much flour, then water, then regretted everything. The dough resisted and sagged. It seemed impossible that this pale paste could become the thing I knew as bread: domed, fragrant, structured, cuttable.

Still, there was relief in its refusal to be hurried. Dough does not care about panic. It responds to pressure, temperature, time. It has no interest in your mood except as it passes through your hands.

In those first weeks, I made dense loaves with tight crumbs and pale crusts. I cut into them too soon because patience was a quality I admired in theory. Steam rushed out, the knife dragged through the gummy centre, and I ate the evidence with butter anyway. Even failed bread has uses. Toast forgives a lot. Soup forgives more.

Slowly, I learnt to read the dough rather than the recipe. This was the first real lesson. Recipes tell you to knead for eight minutes, prove until doubled, bake until golden. They are useful, but they are also blunt instruments. My kitchen was cold in the mornings and hot by late afternoon. Some days the dough rose quickly, blooming against the sides of the bowl like it had urgent business elsewhere. Other days it sat there, sulking under a damp tea towel, while I checked it every twenty minutes as if my attention could substitute for warmth.

I began to notice texture. A dough that had seemed sticky at first would become smooth and elastic if I left it alone for ten minutes. The surface would tighten under my hands. The bench scraper became less of a rescue tool and more of an extension of my palm. I learnt the feeling of gluten developing, the subtle change from tearing to stretching, from paste to fabric. When the dough was ready, it had a kind of liveliness to it. Not life exactly, but potential.

There was pleasure in the verbs: mixing, folding, resting, shaping. They were plain, domestic words. They asked for attention but not performance. I could be tired and still fold dough. I could be sad and still weigh flour. I could be angry and still wash a bowl.

The kitchen began to hold a rhythm that the rest of the year lacked. Morning: feed the starter or measure yeast, depending on how ambitious I felt. Midday: fold the dough, feel it strengthen. Afternoon: shape, dust, cover. Evening: bake. The smell arrived before the loaf was ready, filling the flat with warmth and a faint sweetness that made the rooms feel occupied in a different way. Toasted grain, caramel, steam, the slight mineral edge of salt. It made the day legible. Something had begun as powder and water. Something had changed.

The sourdough starter came later, when I had acquired enough false confidence to invite a new form of responsibility into the house. I mixed flour and water in a jar and waited for signs. At first, nothing. Then a few bubbles on the surface, a sour smell, a rise and collapse that felt both dramatic and faintly disgusting. It had the temperament of a pet and the hygiene requirements of a small lab experiment.

I nearly threw it out twice. Once because it smelt like overripe fruit left in a warm car. Once because it separated into a layer of grey liquid that made me question every online guide I had read. But it recovered. Starters, I learnt, are less fragile than they appear. Neglect them for a day and they may sulk. Feed them and they return.

That idea was more comforting than I wanted to admit.

The first sourdough loaf was not beautiful. It spread wider than it rose, and the score I made across the top healed over in the oven like a wound refusing to open. But the crust crackled as it cooled. I stood beside it in the kitchen and listened. The sound was tiny and astonishing, a dry clicking as the surface contracted. It was the first time all year I had made something that spoke back.

Bread-making did not fix the difficult year. It did not make grief tidy or work manageable or uncertainty noble. There is a danger in romanticising craft as therapy, as if the right handmade practice can redeem whatever is happening around it. I do not believe that. There were days when the bread failed because I failed it. I forgot a fold. I let the dough overproof while dealing with a phone call. I misread the oven. I cried in the kitchen and left the flour open. Craft does not remove the conditions of your life. It gives your hands somewhere to put them.

That was enough.

Over time, bread changed the way I understood attention. I had thought attention meant focus: the narrow beam, the closed door, the completed task. Bread required a different kind. It asked me to return, repeatedly, without staring. To observe without controlling. To act, then leave things alone. This was harder than it sounds. Much of that year had trained me into vigilance. I checked phones, inboxes, symptoms, budgets, weather, news. Bread asked for watchfulness instead. Is the dough relaxed? Has it risen enough? Does it spring back slowly when pressed? Is the crust dark enough, or only beige enough to appease anxiety?

The loaf improved when I stopped trying to dominate it. That sentence is almost too convenient, but it is true.

I became attached to the material details. The soft drag of flour across the bench. The satisfying slap of wet dough during slap-and-folds. The blistered crust of a loaf baked in a preheated pot. The warm, almost animal smell when I lifted the lid and steam hit my face. The way a serrated knife entered a cooled loaf with resistance, then surrender. The first slice was always diagnostic. Open crumb or tight. Even bake or gummy line. Enough salt or not quite. But the second slice was for eating, and it was better not to overthink it.

Butter mattered. So did jam. So did the immediate, practical joy of tearing bread by hand while standing at the bench, even though I owned plates and had been raised better.

By winter, I was making bread for other people. Not as gifts with ribbons and labels, but as small deliveries. A loaf left on a doorstep. A still-warm round wrapped in a tea towel and carried to a friend. Bread has weight. It is not symbolic when it is in your hands. It is dense, warm, perishable. It says: here is something made today, for today, and you should eat it before it goes stale.

That perishability became part of its value. So much of the year felt suspended, as if we were all waiting for life to resume its proper shape. Bread refused suspension. It rose, baked, cooled, hardened, moulded if ignored. It belonged to time. It made demands. Cut me. Share me. Freeze me. Toast me tomorrow. Do not pretend I can be kept forever.

There is a moral clarity in that.

I still bake bread, though less urgently now. The habit has loosened into something steadier. There are weeks when I buy a loaf and feel no guilt. There are weekends when I make focaccia because olive oil pooling in dimples is one of the more persuasive arguments for staying alive. There are mornings when I feed the starter and think, with mild surprise, that it has outlasted the crisis that produced it.

What remains is not mastery. I am still capable of making an unimpressive loaf. What remains is a relationship with a process that cannot be completed by force of will. Bread-making taught me to respect the interval between effort and result. To measure, but also to feel. To accept that some forms of care are repetitive, practical and unglamorous. To understand that transformation is not always dramatic. Sometimes it is flour taking water. Sometimes it is dough becoming elastic under pressure. Sometimes it is a loaf cooling on the bench while the difficult year continues outside the kitchen, and for a few minutes the room smells warm, and the work of the day has made itself visible.

  aimed at general readers of a science magazine. Engaging but accurate, with
  concrete examples.
The forest beneath the forest

Walk through a forest after rain and it is tempting to think the important action is above you: leaves catching light, trunks widening year by year, birds moving between branches. But much of the forest’s metabolism is below the path. Under the litter layer, roots are threaded through with fungal filaments so fine that a teaspoon of soil can hold an intricate living mesh. These fungi are not decoration on tree roots. They are part of how forests feed, recover, compete and store carbon.

The relationship is called mycorrhiza, from the Greek for “fungus-root”. In a mycorrhizal association, a fungus colonises a plant’s root system and extends outward into the soil as microscopic filaments called hyphae. The plant supplies carbon-rich compounds made through photosynthesis. The fungus, in return, helps the plant acquire nutrients, especially phosphorus and nitrogen, and often water and trace elements from beyond the reach of the roots themselves. Mycorrhizal fungi form nutritional symbioses with more than 80% of plant species, and their underground networks can represent a substantial share of living microbial biomass in soils.

For a tree, this is not a minor upgrade. Roots are thick, expensive structures compared with fungal hyphae. A root hair may explore the soil immediately around it; a fungal filament can slip into smaller pores, cross tiny gaps, and spread through patches of decomposing leaf litter or mineral soil. The fungus effectively gives the tree a larger, finer and more chemically versatile absorbing surface. Some ectomycorrhizal fungi, common on many pines, birches, oaks, beeches and eucalypts, form a sheath around root tips and grow between root cells. Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi, found across a very large range of plants, enter root cells and form tiny branched structures where exchange occurs. The architecture differs, but the underlying bargain is similar: carbon flows from plant to fungus; soil-derived resources flow from fungus to plant.

The bargain is also dynamic. Trees do not simply hand over sugar to any fungus in the neighbourhood. Fungi vary in what they can provide, where they forage, and how much carbon they demand. Some are better at scavenging inorganic phosphorus; others can help mobilise nitrogen from organic matter. Recent reviews of ectomycorrhizal forests emphasise that nitrogen transfer is highly variable, depending on fungal identity, soil conditions and the form in which nitrogen is held.   The result is less like a pipe and more like a shifting set of trade relationships. A tree growing in poor soil may invest heavily in fungal partners; a tree in fertilised or nitrogen-rich conditions may invest differently because the return on that carbon has changed.

This is where the popular phrase “wood-wide web” enters the story, and where accuracy matters. A common mycorrhizal network is not just “fungi in the soil”. It means a physical fungal connection linking the roots of two or more plants. Such connections can, in principle, move carbon, nutrients, water or chemical signals between plants. But proving that this happens in intact forests is difficult. Researchers have to separate transfer through fungal hyphae from other routes: roots leaking compounds into soil, microbes consuming and re-releasing them, water moving dissolved chemicals, or plants responding to airborne signals. A 2023 review in Nature Ecology & Evolution argued that several strong public claims about common mycorrhizal networks in forests, including that they are widespread, that they reliably improve seedling performance, and that mature trees preferentially send resources to offspring, are not yet supported by enough field evidence.

That does not make the networks imaginary. It means they are harder to demonstrate than the metaphor suggests. One of the classic experiments was published in 1997 by Suzanne Simard and colleagues. Working with paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings in British Columbia, the researchers used carbon isotopes to trace movement between trees associated with ectomycorrhizal fungi. They found bidirectional carbon transfer between the two species, with a net gain by Douglas-fir under the conditions studied.   The experiment became famous because it suggested that forest plants were not merely competing as isolated individuals. Their roots and fungi could be part of a shared below-ground system.

The more careful conclusion is narrower and more interesting. Carbon can move between plants in some experimental settings. Fungal pathways may contribute. But a detected transfer is not the same as proof that a mature tree is deliberately feeding a seedling, or that the transfer meaningfully changes survival in all forests. Later reviews have found mixed evidence on whether access to common mycorrhizal networks improves, harms or has no effect on seedlings. In some cases, being connected may give a seedling access to nutrients or water. In others, the seedling may lose carbon to fungi, face stronger below-ground competition, or gain no measurable advantage. Forest life is not a morality play about generosity. It is an ecological economy with uneven terms.

The “communication” claim needs the same discipline. Plants certainly detect and respond to chemical cues. When leaves are attacked, they can alter defensive chemistry; when neighbours are damaged, nearby plants may respond. Some experiments suggest that mycorrhizal networks can mediate stress-related signalling. In one study, damaged Douglas-fir was reported to transfer carbon and defence-related signals to neighbouring ponderosa pine through ectomycorrhizal networks.   But this is not conversation in the human sense. There is no evidence of intention, language or conscious warning. A more accurate analogy is a biochemical alarm system: changes in one organism can alter the chemical environment of connected organisms, which may then shift their own physiology.

Even the fungal partner should not be treated as a passive cable. The fungus is alive, metabolically active and under selection to acquire carbon and reproduce. It may allocate nutrients towards hosts that supply more carbon, proliferate in nutrient-rich patches, or reduce exchange when conditions change. Some mycorrhizal relationships look mutualistic; others drift towards parasitism when one partner receives less than it gives. The same fungus that helps one tree may compete with another for nitrogen. The same network that appears to connect plants may also allow fungi to manage their own resources across multiple hosts.

The physical structure of these networks changes how soil works. Hyphae bind particles into aggregates, move nutrients across small distances, and leave behind fungal residues when they die. Those residues can become part of longer-lived soil organic matter, especially when microbial remains bind to minerals. But mycorrhizal fungi do not simply “store carbon” in a straightforward way. They receive plant carbon, build tissue, respire carbon dioxide, interact with decomposer microbes and, in some cases, stimulate decomposition as they mine organic matter for nutrients. Recent forest carbon research has found strong links between fungal community composition and soil carbon patterns, but it also shows why simple claims are risky: different fungi can push carbon cycling in different directions.

A concrete example is a conifer forest growing on nutrient-poor soil. The trees may allocate a large share of photosynthetic carbon below ground because nutrients, not light, are the limiting constraint. Ectomycorrhizal fungi form sheaths around root tips, extend hyphae into the soil, and use enzymes and organic acids to access forms of nitrogen or phosphorus the tree cannot easily reach alone. The visible mushrooms that appear after rain are only the fruiting bodies. Most of the organism is the mycelium threaded through roots and soil. When forestry machinery compacts soil, when topsoil is removed, or when a clear-fell changes temperature and moisture, the living conditions for this mycelium can change abruptly.

Another example is forest regeneration after disturbance. Seedlings do not enter a neutral underground world. They germinate into soil already occupied by fungi, roots, bacteria and decaying organic matter. In some settings, existing mycorrhizal fungi can help young trees establish by rapidly colonising their roots. In others, the existing fungal community may favour some tree species over others, shaping which seedlings survive. This is one reason forest recovery is not just a matter of planting stems in the ground. The below-ground community can influence whether those stems become a functioning forest.

The story also matters for climate and conservation. Mycorrhizal fungi are involved in plant productivity, nutrient cycling and soil carbon dynamics at large scales. A 2025 Nature study mapping global mycorrhizal fungal richness reported that mycorrhizal fungi form extensive underground networks and that many biodiversity hotspots for these fungi fall outside protected areas.   That does not mean protecting fungi is a simple climate solution. It means forest policy that ignores below-ground biodiversity is missing part of the system it is trying to manage.

The best way to understand mycorrhizal networks is to abandon both extremes. They are not magical forest internet cables through which wise old trees send care packages to their children. Nor are they a debunked fantasy. Mycorrhizal fungi are real, ancient and ecologically consequential. They help trees acquire nutrients, alter competition, influence seedling establishment, build and break down soil carbon, and sometimes connect plants in ways that allow materials or signals to move between them.

The forest beneath the forest is therefore not a hidden utopia. It is infrastructure, marketplace, digestive system and battleground at once. Its exchanges are chemical rather than sentimental; its networks are patchy, changing and difficult to measure. That makes them less tidy than the “wood-wide web” metaphor, but more impressive. A forest is not just a collection of trees. It is a layered community of organisms negotiating matter and energy above and below ground, with fungi doing much of the work where light cannot reach.

Why Stoicism refuses to stay ancient

Every generation rediscovers Stoicism because every generation eventually discovers the same humiliation: most of life is not under personal control. The scenery changes. Athenian city-states give way to empires; religious wars give way to nation-states; industrial uncertainty gives way to algorithmic work, climate dread and political volatility. The problem remains recognisable. People want a way to live without waiting for the world to become stable first. Stoicism keeps returning because it offers a severe, portable answer: govern the judgement, practise virtue, accept the limits of power, and do the next necessary thing.

That answer is old enough to seem permanent and practical enough to survive fashion. Stoicism was founded in Athens around 300 BCE by Zeno of Citium and took its name from the Stoa Poikile, the painted porch where its early teachers gathered. It emerged in the Hellenistic world, after the older political scale of the Greek city-state had been overtaken by larger imperial systems. From the beginning, then, Stoicism was not a philosophy of secure citizens in a settled republic. It was a philosophy for people trying to live ethically inside conditions they did not command. Its core claim was not that suffering is imaginary or that emotion should be amputated. It was that human freedom begins in the disciplined distinction between what belongs to us and what does not.

This is why Stoicism travels so well across class and circumstance. Its Roman exemplars are almost too symbolically convenient: Seneca, the wealthy statesman; Epictetus, formerly enslaved; Marcus Aurelius, emperor. Britannica notes the striking range of its major Roman proponents: “both a slave, Epictetus … and an emperor, Marcus Aurelius”. The philosophy can be read as a manual for the powerless and as a discipline for the powerful. That elasticity is central to its appeal. The powerless find in it a way to preserve agency when external choices are brutally constrained. The powerful find in it a warning against appetite, vanity and fear. The same doctrine can teach endurance in one context and restraint in another.

Its rediscovery is also helped by form. Stoicism survives not primarily as a technical system of ancient logic and physics, but as exercises, aphorisms, letters, meditations and handbooks. That matters. Philosophies embedded in dense metaphysics often need institutions to transmit them. Stoicism can be carried in a pocket, quoted in a crisis, pasted above a desk, taught in a prison, or repackaged into a newsletter. This portability makes it vulnerable to simplification, but it also explains its persistence. A generation does not have to inherit the whole ancient system to find a usable Stoic practice.

The Renaissance did exactly this. When Stoicism reappeared in early modern Europe, it was not restored as a museum piece. It was adapted. Late Renaissance Neo-Stoicism tried to revive ancient Stoic ethics in a form acceptable to Christian readers, modifying or rejecting parts of ancient Stoic physics and determinism. Justus Lipsius became the key figure in this revival; his De constantia, published in 1584, argued that Christians could draw on Stoic resources during troubled times while marking the limits of what Christian doctrine could accept. This was rediscovery as translation. Stoicism returned because Europe needed a vocabulary for constancy under disorder, but it had to be made theologically survivable.

That pattern keeps repeating: each age edits Stoicism to suit its anxieties. In the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, it could be made to serve ideals of republican self-command, civic virtue and masculine restraint. In the twentieth century, it found new credibility through psychology and war. Albert Ellis, who developed rational emotive behaviour therapy, drew on the Stoic idea that distress is shaped by judgement rather than events alone; contemporary scholarship continues to identify Stoic ideas as part of the western lineage behind cognitive and mindfulness-based therapies.

Military culture found another version. James Stockdale, a US naval officer and prisoner of war in North Vietnam, later wrote and lectured on Epictetus, describing Stoicism as tested under extreme captivity. The Hoover Institution’s account of Stockdale records that he was held as the senior naval prisoner of war in Hanoi for eight years, tortured repeatedly, placed in leg irons for two years and solitary confinement for four. In that setting, Stoicism was not a lifestyle brand. It was a harsh technology of moral survival: distinguish what can be controlled, refuse spiritual surrender, and maintain duty under coercion.

The contemporary revival is both more democratic and more compromised. On one side, it is genuinely popular philosophy. Modern Stoicism projects such as Stoic Week have turned ancient exercises into public experiments; by 2022, organisers reported that about 38,600 people had signed up across a decade and more than 20,000 had contributed data. On another side, the revival has been absorbed into self-optimisation culture: podcasts, productivity routines, executive coaching, gym discipline, start-up mythology and social media quotation tiles. Ryan Holiday’s Daily Stoic empire has helped bring Stoicism to a mass audience through books, a website, newsletter, podcast and Instagram presence; The Guardian described his audience as including athletes, politicians and tech chief executives.

This is not automatically a corruption. Popularisation is often how old ideas remain socially alive. The problem is selective inheritance. Contemporary Stoicism frequently keeps emotional regulation and discards ethics. It likes discipline, but not always justice; resilience, but not always obligation; serenity, but not always solidarity. Ancient Stoicism was not merely a way to become unbothered. It was a demanding account of how to live according to reason and virtue in a shared cosmos. The modern version is weakest when it becomes a technique for tolerating bad systems rather than judging them. A worker told to “focus on what you can control” may be receiving useful psychological advice, or a subtle instruction to accept exploitation. The difference is political.

This is why the Silicon Valley phase of Stoicism has attracted both fascination and suspicion. Wired noted in 2017 that Stoicism had found unexpected popularity in Silicon Valley and discussed its appeal for figures such as Tim Ferriss, while also warning that the philosophy could be misappropriated when detached from its ethical dimensions. The criticism lands because Stoicism can be made to sound dangerously convenient for winners: accept volatility, master discomfort, convert obstacles into advantage, and do not dwell on external injustice. In that form it becomes less a philosophy than a managerial temperament.

Yet the critique can be too easy. Stoicism also helps explain why people inside systems resist rather than collapse. Susan Fowler, whose account of harassment at Uber helped expose the company’s culture, has been discussed in precisely this context: Stoicism gave her a framework for persisting through fear and uncertainty, not for staying silent. The moral question is not whether Stoicism teaches acceptance. It does. The question is what it teaches people to accept. Fate, mortality, insult, discomfort and uncertainty are one category. Cowardice, injustice and cruelty are another. A serious Stoicism does not confuse the two.

The deeper reason Stoicism keeps being rediscovered is that it solves a cultural contradiction. Modern societies preach control while multiplying experiences of powerlessness. People are told to optimise their bodies, careers, attention, finances, sleep, influence and emotional lives, while living under forces too large for individual command: markets, platforms, bureaucracies, pandemics, wars, climate systems, housing prices, institutional decay. Stoicism cuts through that contradiction by narrowing the field of command. It says: stop pretending the whole world is your project. Your character is.

That message is bracing because it is both modest and demanding. It refuses grandiosity. It also removes many excuses. If reputation is not fully yours, then neither praise nor humiliation should govern conduct. If death is inevitable, then fear of it should not waste life. If anger begins in judgement, then outrage is not simply evidence of moral seriousness. If wealth and status are unstable, then they cannot be the centre of a good life. These claims remain culturally abrasive. They offend consumer capitalism, therapeutic indulgence and political performance in equal measure.

Stoicism returns, then, not because people keep misunderstanding antiquity, but because antiquity keeps offering a discipline for recurring human conditions. Its revivals are never innocent. The Roman court, the Christian Renaissance, modern psychotherapy, military captivity, Silicon Valley and Instagram have each taken different things from it. Some have deepened it; some have flattened it. But the pattern is clear. Whenever a culture becomes noisy, unstable and overconfident about control, Stoicism reappears with its old rebuke: the world is not yours to command, but your response is not nothing.

That is why it will be rediscovered again. Not as a complete answer, and not always in admirable forms. Stoicism is too easily converted into hardness, passivity or elite self-congratulation. But its central provocation remains difficult to exhaust. It asks people to stop confusing comfort with freedom, reaction with judgement, and success with virtue. Each generation believes its crises are unprecedented. Stoicism survives by insisting that the moral test inside them is not.

 Write a 1300-word essay for a design publication about what I've learned from

What junior designers taught me about design

The first thing I learned from mentoring junior designers is that most design advice is too abstract.

We tell them to be curious, think strategically, advocate for users, communicate clearly and become comfortable with ambiguity. None of that is wrong, but it does not tell a designer what to do when a product manager questions a research sample or a developer asks whether an error message should be a toast, an inline alert or a modal.

Mentoring junior designers has forced me to translate judgement back into teachable moves. That has changed how I think about design, not just how I explain it.

Early in my career, I thought seniority meant seeing the answer faster. I now think it means showing your working without taking the pen out of someone else’s hand.

A common example is the first research synthesis session after fieldwork. A junior designer will often arrive with a board full of quotes, colour-coded notes and tentative clusters. They can usually identify what people said. The harder move is deciding what matters. I remember working with a junior designer on research for a public information service. Participants repeatedly said the content was “hard to find”. The first synthesis read this as a navigation problem. The proposed response was to simplify the menu and promote the most common pages.

That was plausible, but incomplete. When we went back through the evidence together, a more precise pattern emerged. People were finding pages. They did not trust that they had found the current, authoritative version of the guidance. Some were checking three pages, downloading old PDFs and then using Google to confirm what the site itself should have made clear. The issue was not only findability. It was confidence. That distinction changed the design response. It moved the work from “make the menu shorter” to “make currency, ownership and task relevance visible at the point of need”.

The lesson for the junior designer was not that their first interpretation was wrong. It was that synthesis is not tidying. It is judgement under constraint. The lesson for me was that I had started using the word “insight” too casually. An insight is not a theme with a better name. It is a shift in understanding that changes what the team should do next.

Mentoring has also taught me that juniors often struggle less with design craft than with the social conditions around it. The harder problem is knowing what to do when the room rewards premature certainty.

I once watched a junior designer present three concept options to senior stakeholders. The options were thoughtful, but the discussion quickly collapsed into preference. One executive liked the version with fewer words. Another wanted more reassurance. A third asked whether “users even read”. The junior designer responded by defending each screen in turn. The more they defended, the more the room treated the work as opinion.

Afterwards, we worked through a different framing. Instead of presenting concepts as alternatives to be liked or disliked, they could have presented them as hypotheses: “This option tests whether people need reassurance before they start”; “this option tests whether eligibility needs to be resolved before account creation”; “this option tests whether plain-language sequencing reduces calls for help”. The screens did not change much. The conversation did. The work became a vehicle for decision-making rather than a set of artefacts awaiting approval.

That experience reminded me that design maturity is not just better artefacts. It is better control of the conversation around artefacts. Junior designers need to learn how to make work discussable: name the decision required, separate evidence from interpretation, and make trade-offs visible before the loudest person in the room defines them.

Confidence is often misdiagnosed. Some junior designers look confident because they talk readily and produce polished work. Others look uncertain because they pause, qualify their language and ask many questions. The second group is often closer to good design judgement than they appear.

One designer I mentored rarely spoke first in critiques. When I reviewed their notes, they had identified risks others had missed: assumptions about literacy, edge cases in eligibility rules, inconsistent terminology, and moments where the service asked users to provide information the organisation already held. Their problem was not a lack of insight. It was a lack of structure for entering the conversation.

Before critique, they began writing three sentences: the decision they wanted feedback on, the constraint they were working within, and the risk they were worried about. That small scaffold changed how they participated. Instead of asking, “What do you think?”, the team had something concrete to engage with.

This has made me wary of advice that tells juniors to “speak up more”. Sometimes they do need to speak up. Often the environment needs better mechanisms for listening. Mentoring is also about noticing when the room is producing the behaviour we then attribute to the individual.

Juniors also need exposure to the boring parts of design, because that is where design becomes real. Design publishing still over-indexes on the visible moments: the workshop, the prototype, the launch, the elegant case study. Much of the actual work sits elsewhere: the content spreadsheet with 1,700 rows, the naming convention no one wants to resolve, the accessibility review that finds the same pattern failing in six different templates.

One junior designer once told me they were disappointed to be spending so much time on content governance rather than “proper UX”. We were working on a service with hundreds of pages, multiple publishers and inconsistent ownership. The interface problems were real, but they were symptoms of a deeper operating problem. Pages decayed because no one knew who owned them, when they should be reviewed, or what standard they needed to meet. We mapped the publishing workflow, identified where accountability disappeared, and designed a review model that matched the organisation’s capacity. It did more for the user experience than another round of page templates would have done.

Craft and context cannot be separated. A button label, a service blueprint, a consent form, a search result and a policy summary all carry organisational assumptions. Junior designers can be taught patterns, heuristics and methods, but they also need to learn how to read the system around the artefact. Who benefits from ambiguity? Who is carrying risk? Which constraint is real?

Junior designers do not need mythology. They do not need to be told that design will transform everything if only people would listen. They need a more durable and honest account of the work. Design is powerful, but it is not magic. It is a disciplined way of making decisions under uncertainty, constraint and consequence. It asks for imagination, but also for evidence. It requires empathy, but also precision.

The best junior designers I have worked with are not the ones who arrive with perfect instincts. They are the ones who can stay with complexity without becoming vague. They can hear difficult feedback without collapsing into obedience. They can care about users without turning every user quote into a command. They can make something concrete, test it, learn from it and change their mind.

Mentoring them has made me less interested in the performance of expertise. It has made me more interested in how expertise is built, shared and tested. It has reminded me that design judgement is not a private talent. It is a practice formed through examples, critique, repetition, language and context.

Every time a junior designer asks, “Why would you do it that way?”, there is a choice. You can give them a rule, or you can reopen the reasoning. The second option takes longer. It is also where the real mentoring happens.

What I have learned, in the end, is that mentoring junior designers is one of the ways senior practice stays honest. It prevents fluency from hardening into dogma. It turns instinct back into explanation. It forces the work to become legible, not only to clients or stakeholders, but to the people who will carry the discipline forward.
