1. Servant Hedonism: My Life Philosophy

One Sunday afternoon, stretched out on the couch with a book, it struck me that I had been living by a philosophy without ever naming it.

It isn’t elaborate. It doesn’t rest on an all-encompassing theory of history. It offers no checklist of rules or algorithm for decision-making.

Yet it has proven reliable. In periods when everything felt unstable or overwhelming, it gave me orientation.

I call it Servant Hedonism — serving yourself by serving others.

It brings together two concepts that are usually framed as opposites: service and pleasure.

Begin with “servant.”

By this I mean something close to Servant Leadership: the idea that life is best approached through steady, unglamorous generosity — a commitment to be useful where possible, as often as possible.

When I’ve faced difficult choices, this orientation has repeatedly pointed toward the most meaningful outcomes. Its strength lies in its simplicity. Most of the time, it is obvious where help is needed. And opportunities to contribute are never scarce.

To serve is to adopt a humble stance toward existence. You arise from a web of relationships and conditions, and you will return to it. You are responsible for sustaining yourself. But that sustenance can come through contribution rather than extraction.

Put differently, you are not a self-contained unit. You are part of a lineage and a community that precedes you and will outlast you. During your brief interval here, you can add something of value.

There are pragmatic advantages as well. Service generates positive-sum exchanges. It builds trust. It aligns people around shared efforts.

Even so, the most compelling reason to serve is simpler: it feels good.

This brings us to “hedonism,” which matters even more.

Being of service is the most deeply satisfying state I know. Few experiences compare to offering your attention or skill to someone and witnessing the moment something shifts for them. In a culture saturated with self-absorption, it feels like a rare privilege.

But service alone can harden into self-denial. The insistence on being useful can slip into martyrdom or quiet burnout. A rigid focus on contribution can obscure one’s own worth.

As adrienne maree brown observes, “You cannot create freedom for others through your own bondage. You cannot empower others through your own demoralization. You cannot create a fulfilling life for others by draining your own of its color. You are a seed, and that is not how seeds work.”

Without pleasure, service becomes another discipline of control.

Service and pleasure are frequently treated as competing commitments, as though attention to one diminishes the other. As though enjoyment necessarily undermines generosity.

I reject that framing.

The two can reinforce each other. Asking how to contribute shapes you into someone you respect. Allowing yourself enjoyment — including your imperfections — increases your capacity to give.

At its core, Servant Hedonism is an ethic of self-regard.

It means counting yourself among those who deserve care. It means refusing to accept chronic depletion as a moral badge. It means asking, as brown does, “What is happening and why did I decide to endure it?”

Charles Eisenstein writes, “Pleasure is the feeling we get from satisfying a need. The more powerful the need, the greater the pleasure. To follow this principle requires, first, accepting that our needs are valid and even beautiful. And not just our needs, but our desires as well, coming as they do from unmet needs.”

Once you collapse the boundary between serving and enjoying, it becomes difficult to distinguish whose needs are being met. The binary between giver and receiver softens.

There is a contagious quality to this approach. When people experience safety, belonging, intimacy and connection, they tend to extend the same outward. The impulse to serve emerges naturally.

The word “hedonism” unsettles some. It suggests indulgence without restraint, excess at others’ expense. I retain it deliberately.

Your own well-being must remain central. If your life is treated purely as an instrument for some external objective, then human life itself ceases to be the highest value. And when that hierarchy shifts, it becomes easy to justify using others as means.

Servant Hedonism draws people in not through guilt or obligation but through visible vitality. Joy is persuasive. People gravitate toward what appears alive.

There is no need for coercion. Pleasure exerts its own pull. It sustains motion without constant force.

Pleasure can function as an organising principle for a life that generates more than it consumes. It can shape activism, institutions and economies. It may be among the few principles that does not exhaust itself over time.

Adopting this stance is not effortless. At first it can be uncomfortable.

There is a fear of losing discipline, of sliding into unrestrained self-indulgence. Many of us have internalised the belief that suffering carries moral weight, while enjoyment is suspect. We imagine that we must first eliminate all personal lack before we can justify generosity. But needs proliferate.

What steadies me is a simple reminder: you are allowed to inhabit the future you are trying to build. You do not need to sacrifice yourself to make it real. Your freedom and satisfaction are not obstacles to collective flourishing; they are part of it.

Workshops as Portals

And how to create clarity in consulting work

Selling consulting work is structurally awkward. There’s an inherent inside–outside tension: how much can be known about a client and their problem before a proposal is written?

The task, in practice, is to find a way to move from the outside in. To get close enough to the work to understand it properly—without already being embedded.

A workshop does that.

Over the past few years, the default response to new enquiries has been to propose a workshop first. It has consistently led to stronger engagements and longer retention.

“Workshop” is a broad term. Here, it refers specifically to sessions designed to produce clarity—a way to step inside the client’s context and establish a basis for ongoing work.

The key shift is to stop treating the workshop as a vehicle for solving problems. Instead, treat it as a working session that sharpens understanding.

Trying to resolve something like brand strategy in a single day is unrealistic. Establishing clarity around it is not.

And clarity has economic value. Structured correctly, these sessions can be priced accordingly.

This piece covers:

* Why clarity matters to both sides
* How to structure workshops for outcomes rather than experience
* Conversation prompts that support productive sessions
* Why pricing should not be discounted (e.g. $4,000/day)
* Examples drawn from practice

First, a framing device.

In media res

“In media res” means starting in the middle of the action. It’s a common narrative technique—begin with movement, then fill in context.

It works because people tolerate exposition once they have a sense of momentum.

Consulting follows a similar pattern. Both parties want early evidence of what working together actually feels like.

From the client’s perspective:

* Can this person address our specific issues?
* Do they demonstrate competence?
* Will our ways of working align?
* Are they reliable?
* How do I justify this internally?

From the consultant’s perspective:

* Is there sufficient scope here?
* What are the underlying problems?
* What are the team dynamics?
* Will this relationship work?

This is the core tension. Proposals are expected before sufficient context exists, and client problem statements are often incomplete or inaccurate.

At the same time, the process typically begins with pricing and contractual discussion—before either party has enough signal.

The question becomes: how to approximate in media res early, without committing to a full engagement?

Selling portal workshops

One practical approach is to respond to initial conversations with a defined workshop—a “portal” into the organisation.

A limited, contained piece of work is usually easier to approve than a retainer or large programme. It provides access to real context without requiring full commitment.

Notably, the scope is deliberately loose. These sessions are not heavily programmed. They function better as exploratory working sessions than tightly scripted workshops.

Jam sessions

“Workshop” carries assumptions—facilitation plans, structured activities, predefined outputs.

In this model, the format is closer to a collaborative session than a facilitated sequence. Preparation might range from a minimal agenda to a short framing deck, but excessive structure tends to reduce effectiveness.

In practice, the session behaves more like a jam session: adaptive, conversational, and responsive.

The objective is to surface enough context to answer key unknowns:

* Establish working rapport
* Signal attentiveness and listening
* Identify the real problems—and whether budget exists to address them
* Demonstrate capability
* Observe team dynamics
* Understand context (what, who, why now)
* Surface blind spots

These are not trivial. They require space for divergence and follow-up questioning.

Group size matters. More than five or six participants reduces signal quality. Larger groups introduce performance behaviour and suppress dissent. Smaller groups tend to produce more accurate information.

Conversation prompts for running an effective jam session

Unstructured does not mean unprepared. The session still requires deliberate framing.

Useful prompts and techniques include:

* Start by restating the situation and objectives. Test for inconsistencies and push for specificity.
* Operate iteratively rather than linearly. Follow threads rather than progressing through a fixed sequence.
* Prioritise simple questions over clever ones. For example: “Why wouldn’t we approach this the same way as the previous project?” or “If the problem and solution are clear, what role is needed here?”
* Use hypotheticals to expose assumptions. “If a senior hire were already in place, what would they prioritise?”
* Be direct. “Do you have the capability to execute this?” or “What is preventing approval?”
* Resolve small, contained issues where appropriate to build credibility.
* Avoid resolving large issues prematurely. Early “solutions” are often contextually incomplete or already rejected internally.
* Reframe problems explicitly to test understanding.
* Maintain a visible list of discrete issues—this becomes input for subsequent scoping.
* Allow silence. Delays in response often indicate real complexity rather than disengagement.

Engineer for outcomes not optimism

The aim is not a positive experience. It is a productive one.

This distinction matters. Effective sessions often feel demanding. They challenge assumptions and expose gaps.

Clients tend to value sessions that produce movement—even if uncomfortable—over sessions that are smooth but inconsequential.

Define the outcomes required in advance and drive towards them.

Workshops for selling open-ended retainers

Post-workshop, several outcomes typically emerge:

* Established working relationship
* Shared experience of collaboration
* Improved understanding of context and constraints
* Initial framing of the problem space

One additional effect is less obvious:

Detailed statements of work become easier to agree.

Without context, both parties attempt to specify future work precisely, which leads to friction. After a workshop, shared understanding reduces this need.

Scopes can remain lightweight. For example:

* On-site support for audience development, two days per week

The reduction in specification overhead is significant.

$4,000 days — a note on pricing

There is a temptation to discount these sessions, given their role in securing longer-term work.

This is counterproductive.

The workshop sets the pricing anchor. Lowering the rate here makes it difficult to increase it later.

A common approach is to price at approximately double the standard day rate. For example, a $2,000 day rate becomes a $4,000 workshop.

This remains a relatively small commitment for the client while signalling value. It can be justified through preparation, synthesis, and follow-up.

A common failure mode is overcompensating for price by over-structuring the session. This reduces effectiveness.

The key point: the session is not solving the problem.

It is producing clarity.

Four examples from practice

High-value, low-structure

A two-day workshop was agreed following a single call and a one-page outline. The agenda was minimal.

This led directly to a multi-year retainer.

Agenda displacement

In one session, asking participants to describe the project independently revealed significant misalignment.

The planned agenda was abandoned. The session focused entirely on reconciling differing interpretations.

The result was clarity on internal issues, rather than progress on the nominal topic. A retainer did not follow, but the outcome was still correct.

Productive silence

A session characterised by long pauses initially appeared unproductive.

Subsequent discussion revealed a fundamental gap in market understanding. This reframed the entire engagement toward research.

Boundary ambiguity

In another case, language intended to signal partnership blurred perceived boundaries.

This created minor confusion but also reinforced a collaborative stance. The trade-off was acceptable.

Conclusion

There are multiple ways to reduce the inside–outside gap in consulting. Workshops are one reliable mechanism.

The underlying principle is consistent: clarity enables effective work.

Design entry points that create context quickly—whether through workshops or other formats. Use them to test working relationships and surface real problems before committing to larger engagements.

And price accordingly. Clarity is not incidental; it is the basis of all subsequent work.

Construct a way in, and use it.

First Men and Original Sins

Faith and the American Space Program

Kendrick Oliver. To Touch the Face of God: The Sacred, the Profane, and the American Space Program, 1957–1975. Johns Hopkins, 2013.

Catherine L. Newell. Destined for the Stars: Faith, Future, and America’s Final Frontier. Pittsburgh, 2019.

First Man. Directed by Damien Chazelle. Universal/Amblin/DreamWorks, 2018.

⸻

“Profane” is a revealing word. Its roots point to what lies outside—or more precisely, in front of (pro)—the temple (fanum). How, then, should spaceflight be understood in these terms? Is Earth the sacred centre, with outer space as its exterior? Or are the heavens themselves the temple, leaving humanity confined to a merely preliminary, secular threshold? Does the sense of awe associated with space exploration arise from a fundamentally religious instinct? Or should Apollo be read as the victory of resolutely secular science, engineering, and material capability?

Kendrick Oliver addresses these questions in To Touch the Face of God, a detailed social history of the American space program. He resists simplistic attempts to project the personal religious beliefs of astronauts and engineers directly onto Apollo. Even so, he demonstrates how thoroughly the program was infused with a providential, broadly Protestant sensibility. His analysis weighs the merits and limitations of treating spaceflight as a quasi-religious undertaking. In particular, he shows how the program intersected with national debates about church–state separation, sharpened after the 1963 U.S. Supreme Court ruling against compulsory school prayer.

A striking example came in December 1968, when the crew of Apollo 8, orbiting the moon, broadcast a message to “all the people back on earth” before reading aloud from Genesis 1. Oliver documents the scale of the response. The Christian Century described itself as “struck dumb,” while flight director Gene Kranz later recalled feeling “the presence of creation and the Creator” as he listened.

The moment was not universally welcomed. Madalyn Murray O’Hair, a central figure in the earlier school prayer case, protested the broadcast and gathered tens of thousands of supporters. Her formal objections had limited institutional impact—NASA acknowledged them but moved on—but they reflected a broader strand of opposition to Apollo.

Other critics focused on cost. The program was attacked as a misuse of public funds that could instead address urban decline or the Vietnam War. Enthusiasm waned over time. After the symbolic impact of Armstrong’s first step, public interest declined sharply through the 1970s. NASA struggled to justify continued missions once the initial objective had been achieved. The question became unavoidable: having reached the moon, what purpose remained?

Oliver suggests an alternative path. He notes that between 1968 and 1975 NASA received more than eight million letters and signatures defending astronauts’ right to religious expression in space—far exceeding correspondence on any other topic. In his view, this indicated an untapped constituency. Framing space exploration in explicitly religious terms—“going for God”—might have renewed public engagement. He implies that such a reframing could still hold relevance.

This raises a gap in Oliver’s account: the role of science fiction. Public disengagement was not with space itself but with the routine character of later Apollo missions. What mission control valued—predictability and reliability—translated into diminished spectacle. Yet in 1975, the same year as the final Apollo-Soyuz mission, George Lucas was preparing Star Wars, released in 1977. The film restored a sense of wonder and excitement to space, albeit in fictional form. Audiences accepted the substitute. In effect, cinematic space displaced real space: a more immediate, less demanding version of exploration.

The scale of this shift is measurable. The Apollo program cost roughly $25.4 billion in 1960s dollars (around $100 billion today). By contrast, the acquisition and expansion of the Star Wars franchise alone has generated tens of billions in revenue. Since the late twentieth century, expenditure on fictional representations of space has plausibly exceeded that on actual lunar exploration. This is not an argument against science fiction, but it indicates a cultural preference for mediated experience over physical achievement.

Science fiction also carries a spiritual dimension. The Force in Star Wars functions as a quasi-religious system, shaping a dedicated global fan base. The term “fan” itself shares linguistic roots with temple imagery. Large-scale, organised fandom exhibits behaviours not unlike religious communities. The persistence of such engagement suggests that the appeal of space exploration may partly reside in its capacity to host spiritual narratives.

One difficulty for real-world spaceflight is theological mismatch. The Christian God is not a specialised sky deity in the manner of Zeus or Uranus. Rather, the divine is conceived as omnipresent, not confined to a particular domain. By contrast, space exploration—particularly lunar ascent—resembles older mythic encounters with sky gods. Science fiction, more flexible in its cosmology, supplies new forms of cosmic divinity, from Lovecraftian entities to the Force itself. It may be filling a conceptual gap left by traditional theology.

⸻

Catherine Newell approaches related themes in Destined for the Stars, focusing on artist Chesley Bonestell. She traces how mid-twentieth-century American culture popularised space exploration through imagery that combined scientific plausibility with aesthetic clarity. This work, she argues, drew on earlier traditions of frontier painting, particularly the Hudson River School, linking outer space to longstanding narratives of expansion and destiny.

Newell details Bonestell’s career, including his collaborations with Willy Ley and his contributions to the film Destination Moon. However, the later sections shift toward Wernher von Braun, whose influence on the space program becomes central.

Her portrayal of von Braun is descriptive rather than evaluative. While acknowledging controversy, she emphasises his technical achievements and personal religiosity. This treatment risks minimising significant ethical issues. Von Braun’s involvement with the SS and the V-2 rocket program—linked to forced labour and mass casualties—is documented, though not foregrounded in her account.

A more balanced interpretation would treat von Braun as embodying both innovation and moral compromise. This duality is not incidental but integral to the history of Apollo. If the program is framed in religious terms, then its origins in wartime technology complicate any narrative of pure progress. The analogy to original sin is not incidental: a foundational achievement entangled with prior wrongdoing.

Newell returns, in conclusion, to Bonestell’s imagery as a source of renewed purpose. She argues that the aesthetic and spiritual qualities of his work could support a future revival of space exploration. The optimism is clear, though it sits uneasily alongside the historical realities that enabled the technology itself.

⸻

First Man (2018), directed by Damien Chazelle and starring Ryan Gosling and Clare Foy, offers a different perspective. The film underperformed commercially despite critical regard, likely due to its restrained tone. It avoids spectacle in favour of psychological focus.

Based on James Hansen’s biography, the film reconstructs the technical environment of Apollo while concentrating on Neil Armstrong’s internal life. The narrative centres on grief—specifically, the death of his daughter Karen—and its long-term effects. The lunar landing becomes less a national triumph than a personal endpoint.

Chazelle’s direction emphasises confinement and tension. Flight sequences are rendered as physically disorienting and hazardous, contrasting with the more expansive imagery typical of space films. Domestic scenes are subdued, depicting emotional restraint rather than overt drama. The result is a study of isolation rather than heroism.

The film’s closing gesture—Armstrong leaving a memento in a lunar crater—is speculative but thematically consistent. It frames the moon not as an object of conquest but as a site of release. In this sense, First Man engages the same question as Oliver and Newell: what constitutes the spiritual dimension of space exploration?

Oliver links this dimension to charisma and collective belief. First Man instead suggests a quieter interpretation. Grace, in this context, is not grandeur but relief—something encountered in stillness rather than spectacle. The film implies that the significance of the space race lies not only in technological achievement but in its capacity to hold human vulnerability.

If Apollo represents both aspiration and compromise, then its meaning may lie in that tension. The journey outward is inseparable from what is carried within: ambition, grief, and unresolved moral inheritance. Space exploration, in this reading, is neither purely sacred nor purely profane, but a convergence of both.

How MDMA therapy changed my life

I underwent MDMA-assisted therapy, and it profoundly transformed my life.

I had two sessions, one at the start of September and another at the beginning of October.

In terms of personal growth, it’s easily among the three most significant experiences I’ve ever had.

I’m sharing this because I genuinely believe it has the potential to help many people, yet awareness remains limited.

In this article, I’ll discuss my reasons for choosing MDMA therapy, what the experience entailed, its impact on me, and where to find further information.

This isn’t intended to persuade you to try MDMA therapy. However, I’m not neutral—I genuinely think MDMA therapy has profound therapeutic potential and will likely become central to mental health treatment in the future.

Disclaimer

It’s crucial to acknowledge:

MDMA is currently illegal in the United States.

By engaging in therapeutic MDMA use under professional supervision, I knowingly broke the law.

To protect those involved, all names related to my MDMA therapy are pseudonyms—except my own.

Yes, there’s a risk in openly writing about this under my real identity, but I’ve accepted this risk for two primary reasons:

1. MDMA therapy is nearing completion of Stage Three clinical trials and is projected to become legally available for prescribed therapeutic use within approximately four years. Authorities typically aren’t pursuing therapeutic users.
2. More importantly, I’ve always openly shared my experiences, especially when I believe the message could profoundly impact or even save lives. If there’s a legal risk involved, I’m willing to accept it.

1. Why I chose MDMA therapy

Simply put, I chose MDMA therapy because traditional methods had failed to adequately address lingering trauma.

To provide context, my therapeutic history includes:

* Four years of intensive psychoanalysis (four sessions weekly), which initially greatly benefited me, helping me understand and manage my emotions. But after four years, its effectiveness waned.
* Meditation followed, which initially aided me significantly. However, after two years, progress stalled—likely due to my lack of sustained discipline.
* Numerous other methods (yoga, CBT, EMDR) proved ineffective despite empirical support.

I then encountered a shaman—not advocating for ayahuasca—but offering to help directly. Skeptical at first, I experienced noticeable benefits from her unconventional approach. After a year, however, this method also ceased to progress my healing.

Despite extensive therapeutic exploration, a deep emotional blockage remained untouched. Recognising my unresolved trauma and emotional barriers, I sought advice from a network of trusted individuals knowledgeable about therapeutic psychedelics.

MDMA emerged consistently as the recommended choice due to its targeted effectiveness for trauma treatment. Unlike hallucinogens such as LSD or ayahuasca, MDMA’s primary therapeutic function is precisely to facilitate trauma processing. Early research indicates MDMA achieves remission in 70–85% of individuals with treatment-resistant PTSD.

Given this compelling evidence, personal testimonies, and my stalled therapeutic progress, I decided MDMA was the next logical step.

2. Finding my MDMA therapist

I was fortunate.

My publishing company, Scribe, produced a book titled Trust, Surrender, Receive by “Anne Other,” detailing MDMA therapy cases. A trusted employee profoundly benefited from Anne’s MDMA sessions, inspiring me to book my own.

3. Preparing for my first MDMA session

Anne conducted an initial call, confirming medication safety (no SSRIs allowed), understanding my motives, and providing simple session instructions:

* Minimal breakfast, arriving by 10 am.
* Initial 125mg dose, potentially followed by a 75mg dose after an hour.
* Wearing an eyeshade, listening to calming music, and embracing whatever emotional responses surfaced.
* No active therapeutic dialogue unless requested, favouring introspective processing.

4. Experiencing the first session

After ingesting the initial dose, I initially slept briefly. After waking and taking the second dose, the experience began profoundly:

Describing MDMA to someone inexperienced with it is challenging, but fundamentally, it felt like experiencing pure, unconditional love—not directed toward an external object or person, but an overwhelming internal state.

I repeatedly distracted myself with intellectual discussions rather than fully surrendering initially. Recognising this, I later allowed the medicine to trigger powerful emotional and physical (somatic) releases, especially in my legs, manifesting as involuntary shaking and deep breaths.

While MDMA intensely stimulates emotional processing, it’s uniquely manageable, allowing controlled modulation of intensity.

5. The aftermath of my first session

Post-session, I continued releasing stored trauma physically. Observations included significantly reduced aggression, less emotional reactivity, and notable improvements in relationships, as colleagues and loved ones noticed my positive emotional shifts.

This release created unexpected feelings of emptiness—like mourning the loss of familiar emotional burdens. It revealed deep unconscious reliance on anger as a coping mechanism, providing power, motivation, and control, even with its negative aspects.

6. The second session

I chose a home setting for my second session, supervised by my wife, roughly one month later. Anxiety emerged beforehand, manifesting as irritability.

This session involved deeper somatic releases—full-body movements—and emotional introspection about relationships, purpose, and past confrontations, highlighting past emotional isolation.

7. Aftermath of the second session

Post-session insights included recognising how suppressed pain had diminished my capacity for joy, playfulness, and openness. MDMA helped me understand the necessity of transitioning from a defensive warrior mentality toward becoming a constructive leader, building positively rather than defensively reacting to past hurt.

These realisations led to enhanced emotional detachment from reactive identities, fostering greater inner peace and resilience.

8. What’s next

Given my experiences, I anticipate regular future sessions, possibly annually, and considering exploration with other psychedelics. The increasing interest and private discussion within influential circles underscores a widespread therapeutic demand.

MDMA and similar treatments represent an emerging wave in mental health care, likely expanding significantly in coming years.

Belonging is a superpower – patterns for decentralised organising

If you’re in a co‑op, collective, or any non‑hierarchical team with open and inclusive values — and you’re aiming for genuinely horizontal ways of working — this is for you. It’s a write‑up of a session run by the founders of Loomio, where they shared distilled, repeatable patterns for collaborating well. The focus was on what makes decentralised organising reliable in practice, not just in principle.

You’ve probably heard of Loomio — a simple, effective decision‑making tool that grew out of Enspiral. What tends to get less attention is the set of principles the Loomio founders use in their own organisation: small design choices that make decentralised work less fragile. The workshop was essentially “the operating system” behind the software.

Rich and Nati delivered the workshop as part of their grand tour, and it’s obvious they’ve been living this for years. The tone was pragmatic and grounded, with the kind of clarity that comes from iterating in real teams, not just theorising.

The session was hosted at the always‑stimulating Newspeak House, with about 30 of us split into small groups. Rich opened by warning it would be dense. Four slides and around 20 minutes later, I was trying to keep pace with the number of usable ideas. None of the patterns are magical on their own — the value is in how clearly they’re expressed and how deliberately they’re practised. I went in expecting familiar advice and came out with sharper language for things many groups only sense vaguely.

Take pattern 1: “Intentionally produce counter-culture”. Many groups already sense this matters, but the phrase that landed was: “belonging is a superpower”. It’s something teams often claim but don’t design for. When everyone experiences real belonging, people step up, share responsibility, and stretch what the group can do. When newcomers can’t find a way in — or long‑timers hoard context, status or access — belonging shrinks. Unwritten rules start to dominate: who gets invited to the important conversations, whose questions get answered, and who is treated as “credible”. Once belonging slips, coordination gets harder and effectiveness goes with it. This theme came back later (see below), but the other patterns deserve attention too.

Pattern 2, “Systematically distribute care labour”, was about making support visible and shared rather than leaving it to the same few people. Rich described a model where each person is someone’s steward (offering care and attention) and also a stewardee (receiving it). It’s basic relationship infrastructure, but formalising it makes neglect harder. It also stops care work becoming an invisible job for the same conscientious people, and builds trust through consistent, low‑stakes contact.

Pattern 3 was “Make explicit norms and boundaries”. Remove guesswork: be clear about purpose, how decisions happen, what behaviours are acceptable, and what is out of bounds. Vagueness invites competing interpretations, and those differences surface later as friction. In groups that prize openness, boundaries can feel unfriendly — but clear boundaries often make participation safer because people know what to expect.

Pattern 4 tackled the thing “flat” groups often avoid naming: “Keep talking about power”. Rich was direct that imbalances are inevitable. Some people accumulate influence — for example, those who speak confidently in public settings often earn more attention and therefore more authority. The point isn’t to pretend this doesn’t happen, but to surface it and share it: say who has power in which contexts, rotate roles, step back on purpose, and invite others to step forward.

By then my attention was wobbling — it was past 11am and I’d only had a small cup of tea. The tag‑team dynamic helped: Nati took over, got everyone stretching, and reset the room’s energy.

Pattern 5, “Make decisions asynchronously”, is so straightforward it’s surprising how often it’s ignored. The distinction was useful: meetings are great for connection, trust and sense‑making; computers are great for supporting better decisions. Asynchronous processes can be more inclusive and less intrusive — timely without forcing urgency. It’s also what Loomio was built for.

Pattern 6 named a common headache and turned it into a clear prompt: “Agree how you use tech”. Rich suggested most team toolsets can be organised into a “holy trinity” for group work: one tool for real‑time communication (chat), one space for asynchronous discussion (email or a forum), and one place for stable reference content (a wiki or document store). The trick is agreeing what each tool is for, and what it is not for. If a group sticks to that agreement, coordination improves and the “where should this go?” noise drops. Without it, decisions scatter across channels and the team ends up re‑litigating issues because nobody can find the current position.

Pattern 7 was “Using rhythm to cut information overload”. Create predictable cycles for sharing updates, asking for help, and staying aligned. The key is matching cadence to purpose: longer rhythms for deeper reflection (for example, quarterly reviews that get people out of routine and reconnect them — creating “peak oxytocin” moments), and shorter cycles for week‑to‑week planning, review and celebration. Well‑chosen rhythms give people permission to focus, because updates and requests for help have an agreed place to land each cycle.

Pattern 8 pulled the whole approach together: “Generate new patterns together”. Loomio’s culture clearly draws on iterative development — build a way of working, test it, learn, adjust, repeat. Regular review and collective reflection aren’t “nice to have”; they’re a core capability. Continuous, participatory change can feel slower, but it’s often what keeps a decentralised group coherent over time.

For the remainder of the workshop we worked in groups: choose a real challenge, describe a “desired future”, and identify practical next steps. Our group chose onboarding — specifically, how to help everyone (especially newcomers) gain the “superpower of belonging”. We mapped where people typically get stuck and then generated actions that could make entry smoother and status less concentrated. The “Next steps” section of our worksheet captures what we generated; hopefully some of it is useful in your own context.

A final note of thanks to Rich and Nati for making the event happen and for delivering a well‑structured, thoughtfully designed workshop with timely content. Better decentralised practice means more capable teams, and more capable teams can deliver more positive change. Their upcoming workshops are worth recommending to anyone working in a cooperative or horizontal model.
