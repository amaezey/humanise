# In praise of slow work

The first time I noticed how badly speed had colonised my attention, I was not at work. I was making soup.

It was an ordinary soup, the kind that begins with onions softening in oil. I had chopped them badly, because I was thinking about something else. I kept checking my phone between stirs, as if the onions might resent my full attention. The recipe said to cook them slowly until translucent, but I had turned the heat too high. They browned at the edges before they had properly softened. The soup was fine, in the way many rushed things are fine: edible, competent, unmemorable.

That is the problem with speed. It often produces something acceptable enough to pass. It rarely produces something with depth.

Creative work is not soup, but it is also not unrelated to soup. It depends on timing, heat, patience and the willingness to let materials change in their own way. A sentence needs time to reveal whether it is alive or merely polished. An idea needs time to gather contradiction around it. A painting, a poem, an essay, a design, a film, a piece of music: all of these are made not only through activity, but through waiting. To work slowly is not to work lazily. It is to allow the work to become more intelligent than the first impulse that produced it.

We live in a culture that treats speed as a moral quality. The fast worker is presumed to be disciplined, responsive, valuable. The slow worker must explain herself. Speed has become a proxy for competence, as if the shortest interval between request and output were the clearest measure of skill. This logic has always been present in industrial life, but it has intensified in creative work because creative work is now so often forced to behave like content.

Content must be regular. Content must be optimised. Content must be legible to platforms, calendars, audiences, metrics and clients. Content does not ripen; it is published. Its success is measured by circulation, not by what it does to the person who made it or the person who encounters it months later. The content economy has trained us to confuse production with expression, and expression with visibility. It asks not “What is this worth saying?” but “Can this be shipped by Thursday?”

The uncomfortable truth is that creative work can now be made very quickly. Tools have become astonishingly efficient. Drafts can be generated, edited, templated, scheduled and distributed at a pace that would once have seemed absurd. The question is no longer whether we can make more, faster. We plainly can. The more serious question is whether speed is making our work thinner.

In my own work, the answer is often yes. The fastest drafts tend to carry the residue of whatever language is most available: professional clichés, inherited structures, familiar rhythms, thoughts that sound correct because they have already been said too many times. Speed rewards the phrase nearest to hand. It rewards the idea that already knows how to present itself. It favours fluency over accuracy and polish over discovery.

Slowness, by contrast, is where embarrassment begins. That is one reason we avoid it. To sit with a draft for longer than efficiency requires is to notice how much of it is false. Not factually false, necessarily, but emotionally false, intellectually lazy, rhetorically inflated. Slowness exposes borrowed conviction. It shows where an argument is gesturing instead of thinking. It reveals the sentence that sounds beautiful but means very little.

This is why slowness is not merely an aesthetic preference. It is an ethical practice. When work is slow, it has more opportunities to become accountable to reality. It can absorb doubt. It can admit complexity. It can resist the easy shape that makes an idea pleasing but untrue.

The demand for speed is often defended as pragmatism. Deadlines are real. Money is real. Attention is scarce. Nobody gets to spend a decade carving a paragraph while the world burns. This is true, but it is also incomplete. The argument for slower creative work is not an argument against deadlines, discipline or publication. It is an argument against allowing urgency to become the default atmosphere in which everything is made.

Some work must be fast. A protest sign, a news response, a campaign, an emergency communication: these have their own dignity and necessity. Speed can be appropriate. It can even be beautiful when it carries force, clarity and purpose. But speed should be a chosen condition, not the permanent climate of creative life. When everything is urgent, nothing has the chance to become substantial.

There is a difference between urgency and momentum. Momentum can sustain work over time. Urgency consumes it. Momentum says: return tomorrow, and the next day, and the next. Urgency says: extract whatever is available now. Much contemporary creative work is built on extraction: from attention, from personal experience, from grief, from outrage, from identity, from the half-formed private life of the maker. The result is often work that feels immediate but not considered, revealing but not transformed.

The personal essay, in particular, has suffered from this. At its best, it is one of the most exacting forms: a mind meeting its own experience with discipline, suspicion and grace. At its worst, it becomes a rapid conversion machine, turning pain into narrative before the pain has been understood. Slowness matters here because experience is not insight. To have lived something is not yet to know what it means. Meaning arrives later, often inconveniently, after the first version has hardened into something too neat.

Some of the most important creative labour happens when nothing visible is being produced. Walking, reading badly, reading again, staring out a window, abandoning a structure, noticing irritation, changing one word and then changing it back. These are not romantic luxuries. They are part of the work. Yet they are difficult to defend in systems that recognise only outputs. A notebook full of fragments does not look productive. Neither does silence.

But silence is where the work often starts to resist us. It is where we stop performing productivity and begin listening for what the thing actually requires. A rushed work tends to obey its maker too quickly. A slower work begins to make demands of its own. It refuses the first ending. It asks for a different form. It reveals that the central image is not central at all. It exposes the fact that the argument is not about what we claimed it was about.

This is the part of creativity that cannot be made efficient without being damaged. There are tasks around creative work that can and should be streamlined. Administration, formatting, version control, transcription, research management: let these be as efficient as possible. But the core act of making meaning cannot be treated as a logistics problem. It is closer to cultivation than manufacture.

To defend slowness is also to defend taste. Taste is not simply preference; it is the capacity to perceive difference. It develops through exposure, attention and revision. A culture of speed weakens taste because it reduces the time available for comparison. We no longer ask whether the work is good. We ask whether it is ready. But ready and good are not the same thing. Ready means it can leave us. Good means it can survive leaving us.

Slower work is not guaranteed to be better. There is plenty of dull, overworked art. Time alone does not confer depth. A sentence can be laboured into lifelessness. A project can hide in refinement because its maker is afraid of judgement. Slowness can become avoidance, just as speed can become compulsion. The point is not to worship delay. The point is to recover duration as a legitimate creative condition.

The better question is not “How fast can this be done?” but “What kind of time does this work need?” Some pieces need the voltage of immediacy. Some need months of composting. Some need to be drafted quickly and revised slowly. Some need to be abandoned until the maker has become someone else. Creative maturity lies partly in learning the difference.

I have begun to distrust work that arrives too cleanly. Not always, but often. Cleanliness can signal craft, but it can also signal that nothing difficult has happened yet. The work has not been interrupted by a better thought. It has not had to give up its cleverness. It has not been made strange to itself. The pieces that matter most to me, as both writer and reader, usually contain some evidence of pressure: not mess, exactly, but density. They feel as if the maker stayed long enough for the obvious thing to break.

That is what slowness gives us: the chance for the obvious thing to break.

In an age that prizes speed, slow creative work will increasingly look inefficient, even indulgent. It may also become one of the few ways to make work that is not merely a reflection of the conditions that produced it. Speed gives us more of the present. Slowness gives us a chance to argue with it.

The onions, I have learned, need lower heat. They need time to collapse into sweetness before anything else is added. You can hurry them, and you will still get dinner. But you will not get the same soup. Creative work is like that. The difference is not always visible at first. It arrives later, in the depth, in the aftertaste, in what remains.
