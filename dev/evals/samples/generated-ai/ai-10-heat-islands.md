# What Are Urban Heat Islands?

Urban heat islands are areas in cities that become noticeably warmer than nearby rural or less developed areas. This happens because cities are built with materials and layouts that trap heat. Roads, roofs, car parks, concrete walls, and dense buildings absorb sunlight during the day and release the stored heat slowly, especially after sunset. As a result, a city can remain hot even when surrounding areas begin to cool down.

The effect is not just a small comfort issue. Heat islands can make heatwaves more dangerous, increase energy demand, worsen air quality, and place extra stress on people who are already vulnerable. As climate change raises average temperatures and makes extreme heat more likely in many regions, the heat island effect becomes a bigger public health and planning concern.

One reason cities heat up is the amount of dark, hard surface. Asphalt and many roofing materials absorb a lot of solar energy. A shaded patch of grass and a black road can feel completely different on the same afternoon because they handle sunlight differently. Natural surfaces often cool the air through evaporation and plant processes, while sealed surfaces tend to store heat.

The loss of vegetation is another major factor. Trees provide shade, but they also cool the air through transpiration, which is the process of releasing water vapor from leaves. Parks, gardens, street trees, and green corridors can reduce local temperatures. In neighborhoods with few trees, people are more exposed to direct sun, buildings heat up faster, and outdoor areas become less usable.

City shape matters too. Tall buildings and narrow streets can create urban canyons. These can trap heat and reduce airflow. Sometimes buildings shade the street, which can help during the day, but the same dense layout can also hold warm air at night. Traffic, air conditioning units, industry, and other human activities add more heat into the environment. These sources may seem small individually, but across a whole city they contribute to the overall temperature.

Heat islands are often strongest at night. Rural areas may cool quickly after sunset because open land releases heat more easily. Urban areas, however, continue releasing heat from roads and buildings. This is a serious problem during heatwaves because nighttime cooling gives the body a chance to recover. If nights remain hot, the risk of heat stress rises, especially for older adults, young children, outdoor workers, people without air conditioning, and people with certain medical conditions.

The impacts are not evenly shared. Lower-income neighborhoods often have fewer trees, more industrial land, more traffic exposure, and more heat-absorbing surfaces. Residents may also have less access to cooling, good insulation, or safe public spaces. This means heat islands are also an equity issue. Two people in the same city can experience very different heat risks depending on where they live.

Urban heat islands can also increase energy use. When buildings get hotter, people use more air conditioning. This raises electricity demand, which can strain the grid during extreme heat. If the electricity comes from fossil fuels, increased cooling can also contribute more greenhouse gas emissions. Air conditioning can protect health, but relying only on cooling inside buildings does not solve the outdoor heat problem.

There are several ways cities can reduce heat islands. Planting and maintaining trees is one of the most common strategies, but it needs planning. Trees must be suitable for the local climate, placed where shade is needed, and cared for during dry periods. Green roofs and walls can also help in some settings, although they are not a substitute for street-level shade in places where people walk and wait.

Cool roofs and cool pavements are another approach. These use lighter-colored or reflective materials to absorb less heat. They can lower surface temperatures and reduce building cooling needs. However, they must be chosen carefully so they do not create glare or shift heat onto pedestrians. Urban design often works best when several strategies are combined rather than relying on one solution.

Cities can also make heat planning part of everyday decisions. This includes protecting mature trees, designing shaded bus stops and footpaths, reducing unnecessary paved areas, creating parks in heat-exposed neighborhoods, and using heat maps to identify the worst affected places. Emergency planning matters too. During heatwaves, cities need cooling centers, public communication, welfare checks, and support for people who cannot easily escape the heat.

Urban heat islands show how climate risk is shaped by design. Heat is not only a weather problem. It is also a land use, housing, transport, and infrastructure problem. The good news is that many solutions are already known. Cooler cities are possible when heat is treated as a central planning issue rather than an afterthought.
