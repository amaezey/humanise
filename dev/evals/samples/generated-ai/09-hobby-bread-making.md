# The slow work of bread

The year I took up bread-making was not the year I became a better person. That would be too neat a claim, and bread does not deserve to be burdened with that kind of symbolism. It was simply the year in which many of the ordinary structures of my life thinned out or broke, and I needed something small, repetitive and physical enough to keep me company.

At first, I did not think of it as craft. I thought of it as dinner.

Flour, water, salt, yeast. The list looked almost offensively modest. I had spent much of that year overwhelmed by things I could not simplify: illness in the family, work that expanded to fill every waking hour, the quiet dread of unanswered messages, the particular exhaustion that comes from making decisions when none of the options feel good. Bread offered the illusion of containment. Four ingredients. A bowl. A bench. A loaf tin if I felt cautious; a tray if I felt brave.

The first dough was a shaggy, reluctant thing. I mixed it with a wooden spoon until the flour disappeared into damp clumps, then turned it out onto the bench and pressed my hands into it with more force than skill. It stuck to my fingers, to the heel of my palm, to the laminate. I added more flour, then too much flour, then water, then regretted everything. The dough resisted and sagged. It seemed impossible that this pale paste could become the thing I knew as bread: domed, fragrant, structured, cuttable.

Still, there was relief in its refusal to be hurried. Dough does not care about panic. It responds to pressure, temperature, time. It has no interest in your mood except as it passes through your hands.

In those first weeks, I made dense loaves with tight crumbs and pale crusts. I cut into them too soon because patience was a quality I admired in theory. Steam rushed out, the knife dragged through the gummy centre, and I ate the evidence with butter anyway. Even failed bread has uses. Toast forgives a lot. Soup forgives more.

Slowly, I learnt to read the dough rather than the recipe. This was the first real lesson. Recipes tell you to knead for eight minutes, prove until doubled, bake until golden. They are useful, but they are also blunt instruments. My kitchen was cold in the mornings and hot by late afternoon. Some days the dough rose quickly, blooming against the sides of the bowl like it had urgent business elsewhere. Other days it sat there, sulking under a damp tea towel, while I checked it every twenty minutes as if my attention could substitute for warmth.

I began to notice texture. A dough that had seemed sticky at first would become smooth and elastic if I left it alone for ten minutes. The surface would tighten under my hands. The bench scraper became less of a rescue tool and more of an extension of my palm. I learnt the feeling of gluten developing, the subtle change from tearing to stretching, from paste to fabric. When the dough was ready, it had a kind of liveliness to it. Not life exactly, but potential.

There was pleasure in the verbs: mixing, folding, resting, shaping. They were plain, domestic words. They asked for attention but not performance. I could be tired and still fold dough. I could be sad and still weigh flour. I could be angry and still wash a bowl.

The kitchen began to hold a rhythm that the rest of the year lacked. Morning: feed the starter or measure yeast, depending on how ambitious I felt. Midday: fold the dough, feel it strengthen. Afternoon: shape, dust, cover. Evening: bake. The smell arrived before the loaf was ready, filling the flat with warmth and a faint sweetness that made the rooms feel occupied in a different way. Toasted grain, caramel, steam, the slight mineral edge of salt. It made the day legible. Something had begun as powder and water. Something had changed.

The sourdough starter came later, when I had acquired enough false confidence to invite a new form of responsibility into the house. I mixed flour and water in a jar and waited for signs. At first, nothing. Then a few bubbles on the surface, a sour smell, a rise and collapse that felt both dramatic and faintly disgusting. It had the temperament of a pet and the hygiene requirements of a small lab experiment.

I nearly threw it out twice. Once because it smelt like overripe fruit left in a warm car. Once because it separated into a layer of grey liquid that made me question every online guide I had read. But it recovered. Starters, I learnt, are less fragile than they appear. Neglect them for a day and they may sulk. Feed them and they return.

That idea was more comforting than I wanted to admit.

The first sourdough loaf was not beautiful. It spread wider than it rose, and the score I made across the top healed over in the oven like a wound refusing to open. But the crust crackled as it cooled. I stood beside it in the kitchen and listened. The sound was tiny and astonishing, a dry clicking as the surface contracted. It was the first time all year I had made something that spoke back.

Bread-making did not fix the difficult year. It did not make grief tidy or work manageable or uncertainty noble. There is a danger in romanticising craft as therapy, as if the right handmade practice can redeem whatever is happening around it. I do not believe that. There were days when the bread failed because I failed it. I forgot a fold. I let the dough overproof while dealing with a phone call. I misread the oven. I cried in the kitchen and left the flour open. Craft does not remove the conditions of your life. It gives your hands somewhere to put them.

That was enough.

Over time, bread changed the way I understood attention. I had thought attention meant focus: the narrow beam, the closed door, the completed task. Bread required a different kind. It asked me to return, repeatedly, without staring. To observe without controlling. To act, then leave things alone. This was harder than it sounds. Much of that year had trained me into vigilance. I checked phones, inboxes, symptoms, budgets, weather, news. Bread asked for watchfulness instead. Is the dough relaxed? Has it risen enough? Does it spring back slowly when pressed? Is the crust dark enough, or only beige enough to appease anxiety?

The loaf improved when I stopped trying to dominate it. That sentence is almost too convenient, but it is true.

I became attached to the material details. The soft drag of flour across the bench. The satisfying slap of wet dough during slap-and-folds. The blistered crust of a loaf baked in a preheated pot. The warm, almost animal smell when I lifted the lid and steam hit my face. The way a serrated knife entered a cooled loaf with resistance, then surrender. The first slice was always diagnostic. Open crumb or tight. Even bake or gummy line. Enough salt or not quite. But the second slice was for eating, and it was better not to overthink it.

Butter mattered. So did jam. So did the immediate, practical joy of tearing bread by hand while standing at the bench, even though I owned plates and had been raised better.

By winter, I was making bread for other people. Not as gifts with ribbons and labels, but as small deliveries. A loaf left on a doorstep. A still-warm round wrapped in a tea towel and carried to a friend. Bread has weight. It is not symbolic when it is in your hands. It is dense, warm, perishable. It says: here is something made today, for today, and you should eat it before it goes stale.

That perishability became part of its value. So much of the year felt suspended, as if we were all waiting for life to resume its proper shape. Bread refused suspension. It rose, baked, cooled, hardened, moulded if ignored. It belonged to time. It made demands. Cut me. Share me. Freeze me. Toast me tomorrow. Do not pretend I can be kept forever.

There is a moral clarity in that.

I still bake bread, though less urgently now. The habit has loosened into something steadier. There are weeks when I buy a loaf and feel no guilt. There are weekends when I make focaccia because olive oil pooling in dimples is one of the more persuasive arguments for staying alive. There are mornings when I feed the starter and think, with mild surprise, that it has outlasted the crisis that produced it.

What remains is not mastery. I am still capable of making an unimpressive loaf. What remains is a relationship with a process that cannot be completed by force of will. Bread-making taught me to respect the interval between effort and result. To measure, but also to feel. To accept that some forms of care are repetitive, practical and unglamorous. To understand that transformation is not always dramatic. Sometimes it is flour taking water. Sometimes it is dough becoming elastic under pressure. Sometimes it is a loaf cooling on the bench while the difficult year continues outside the kitchen, and for a few minutes the room smells warm, and the work of the day has made itself visible.
