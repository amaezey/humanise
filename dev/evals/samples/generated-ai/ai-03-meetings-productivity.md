# How to Make Meetings Less Wasteful

Meetings are one of the most common complaints in modern work, and also one of the hardest things to get rid of. Most teams need to talk sometimes. People need to make decisions, share context, solve problems, and notice when work is drifting. The problem is not that meetings exist. The problem is that too many meetings are scheduled without a clear reason, run without structure, and leave people unsure what changed.

A good meeting should earn the time it takes. If six people attend a thirty-minute meeting, that is three hours of working time. This does not mean every meeting must be severe or perfectly optimized, but it does mean the organizer should be able to explain why the conversation is worth that cost.

The first step is deciding whether a meeting is needed at all. Status updates often work better as written notes. Simple approvals can happen in a comment thread. Announcements can be recorded or sent in a short message. Meetings are most useful when there is uncertainty, disagreement, urgency, or a need for shared judgment. If the goal is only to transmit information, a meeting may be the slowest format.

Once a meeting is clearly needed, it should have a purpose that is more specific than "sync" or "discuss." A useful purpose sounds like: choose between two launch dates, identify the cause of a customer issue, review the first draft of a plan, or decide what work will be cut from the sprint. The more concrete the purpose, the easier it is for people to prepare and the easier it is to know when the meeting is done.

Agendas help, but only if they are real. A list of vague topics is not much better than no agenda. Instead of "marketing update," write "decide whether the homepage copy is ready for design review." Instead of "hiring," write "review three candidates and choose who moves to final interviews." A good agenda tells people what kind of attention they need to bring.

It is also worth being strict about who attends. Many meetings get crowded because organizers do not want anyone to feel excluded. But inviting people "just in case" can create a room where half the participants are silent and distracted. A smaller meeting with the right people is usually better. Others can receive notes afterward. Attendance should be based on contribution, decision rights, or direct need for context.

During the meeting, someone should actively guide the conversation. This does not have to be formal, but it does need to happen. Without facilitation, meetings often drift toward the loudest topic or the most confident speaker. A facilitator can bring the group back to the agenda, notice when a decision is being avoided, and ask quieter people for input when their perspective matters.

Time limits are useful too. A meeting does not become more valuable because it fills the calendar slot. If a decision takes fifteen minutes, end the meeting. If the group is stuck after twenty minutes, name what is missing and assign follow-up rather than circling the same points. Ending early is not rude. It is a sign that people's time is being treated honestly.

One of the biggest failures is leaving without a record. At the end of a meeting, there should be a quick summary of decisions, owners, and next steps. This can be very short. For example: "We chose option B, Priya will update the timeline by Thursday, and Marco will confirm the legal question." Without this step, people often leave with different understandings of what happened.

Recurring meetings deserve special scrutiny. They are easy to create and hard to stop. A weekly meeting that was useful in January may be unnecessary in April. Teams should occasionally ask whether each recurring meeting still has a purpose, whether the cadence is right, and whether the same outcome could be achieved another way. Canceling a meeting is not a loss if the work still moves forward.

There is also a cultural side to meeting productivity. If an organization rewards visibility more than outcomes, people will keep attending meetings to appear involved. If decisions are repeatedly reopened, meetings will multiply because no conversation feels final. Better meeting habits depend partly on better decision habits.

The goal is not to eliminate every imperfect conversation. Work involves people, and people sometimes need time to think out loud. But meetings should not become the default container for every uncertainty. Used well, they create alignment and momentum. Used poorly, they turn attention into a shared resource that nobody owns.

A simple standard helps: before scheduling a meeting, ask what decision, understanding, or action should exist afterward that does not exist now. If there is no answer, do not schedule it yet.
