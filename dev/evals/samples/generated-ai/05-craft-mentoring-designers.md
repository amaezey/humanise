# What junior designers taught me about design

The first thing I learned from mentoring junior designers is that most design advice is too abstract.

We tell them to be curious, think strategically, advocate for users, communicate clearly and become comfortable with ambiguity. None of that is wrong, but it does not tell a designer what to do when a product manager questions a research sample or a developer asks whether an error message should be a toast, an inline alert or a modal.

Mentoring junior designers has forced me to translate judgement back into teachable moves. That has changed how I think about design, not just how I explain it.

Early in my career, I thought seniority meant seeing the answer faster. I now think it means showing your working without taking the pen out of someone else’s hand.

A common example is the first research synthesis session after fieldwork. A junior designer will often arrive with a board full of quotes, colour-coded notes and tentative clusters. They can usually identify what people said. The harder move is deciding what matters. I remember working with a junior designer on research for a public information service. Participants repeatedly said the content was “hard to find”. The first synthesis read this as a navigation problem. The proposed response was to simplify the menu and promote the most common pages.

That was plausible, but incomplete. When we went back through the evidence together, a more precise pattern emerged. People were finding pages. They did not trust that they had found the current, authoritative version of the guidance. Some were checking three pages, downloading old PDFs and then using Google to confirm what the site itself should have made clear. The issue was not only findability. It was confidence. That distinction changed the design response. It moved the work from “make the menu shorter” to “make currency, ownership and task relevance visible at the point of need”.

The lesson for the junior designer was not that their first interpretation was wrong. It was that synthesis is not tidying. It is judgement under constraint. The lesson for me was that I had started using the word “insight” too casually. An insight is not a theme with a better name. It is a shift in understanding that changes what the team should do next.

Mentoring has also taught me that juniors often struggle less with design craft than with the social conditions around it. The harder problem is knowing what to do when the room rewards premature certainty.

I once watched a junior designer present three concept options to senior stakeholders. The options were thoughtful, but the discussion quickly collapsed into preference. One executive liked the version with fewer words. Another wanted more reassurance. A third asked whether “users even read”. The junior designer responded by defending each screen in turn. The more they defended, the more the room treated the work as opinion.

Afterwards, we worked through a different framing. Instead of presenting concepts as alternatives to be liked or disliked, they could have presented them as hypotheses: “This option tests whether people need reassurance before they start”; “this option tests whether eligibility needs to be resolved before account creation”; “this option tests whether plain-language sequencing reduces calls for help”. The screens did not change much. The conversation did. The work became a vehicle for decision-making rather than a set of artefacts awaiting approval.

That experience reminded me that design maturity is not just better artefacts. It is better control of the conversation around artefacts. Junior designers need to learn how to make work discussable: name the decision required, separate evidence from interpretation, and make trade-offs visible before the loudest person in the room defines them.

Confidence is often misdiagnosed. Some junior designers look confident because they talk readily and produce polished work. Others look uncertain because they pause, qualify their language and ask many questions. The second group is often closer to good design judgement than they appear.

One designer I mentored rarely spoke first in critiques. When I reviewed their notes, they had identified risks others had missed: assumptions about literacy, edge cases in eligibility rules, inconsistent terminology, and moments where the service asked users to provide information the organisation already held. Their problem was not a lack of insight. It was a lack of structure for entering the conversation.

Before critique, they began writing three sentences: the decision they wanted feedback on, the constraint they were working within, and the risk they were worried about. That small scaffold changed how they participated. Instead of asking, “What do you think?”, the team had something concrete to engage with.

This has made me wary of advice that tells juniors to “speak up more”. Sometimes they do need to speak up. Often the environment needs better mechanisms for listening. Mentoring is also about noticing when the room is producing the behaviour we then attribute to the individual.

Juniors also need exposure to the boring parts of design, because that is where design becomes real. Design publishing still over-indexes on the visible moments: the workshop, the prototype, the launch, the elegant case study. Much of the actual work sits elsewhere: the content spreadsheet with 1,700 rows, the naming convention no one wants to resolve, the accessibility review that finds the same pattern failing in six different templates.

One junior designer once told me they were disappointed to be spending so much time on content governance rather than “proper UX”. We were working on a service with hundreds of pages, multiple publishers and inconsistent ownership. The interface problems were real, but they were symptoms of a deeper operating problem. Pages decayed because no one knew who owned them, when they should be reviewed, or what standard they needed to meet. We mapped the publishing workflow, identified where accountability disappeared, and designed a review model that matched the organisation’s capacity. It did more for the user experience than another round of page templates would have done.

Craft and context cannot be separated. A button label, a service blueprint, a consent form, a search result and a policy summary all carry organisational assumptions. Junior designers can be taught patterns, heuristics and methods, but they also need to learn how to read the system around the artefact. Who benefits from ambiguity? Who is carrying risk? Which constraint is real?

Junior designers do not need mythology. They do not need to be told that design will transform everything if only people would listen. They need a more durable and honest account of the work. Design is powerful, but it is not magic. It is a disciplined way of making decisions under uncertainty, constraint and consequence. It asks for imagination, but also for evidence. It requires empathy, but also precision.

The best junior designers I have worked with are not the ones who arrive with perfect instincts. They are the ones who can stay with complexity without becoming vague. They can hear difficult feedback without collapsing into obedience. They can care about users without turning every user quote into a command. They can make something concrete, test it, learn from it and change their mind.

Mentoring them has made me less interested in the performance of expertise. It has made me more interested in how expertise is built, shared and tested. It has reminded me that design judgement is not a private talent. It is a practice formed through examples, critique, repetition, language and context.

Every time a junior designer asks, “Why would you do it that way?”, there is a choice. You can give them a rule, or you can reopen the reasoning. The second option takes longer. It is also where the real mentoring happens.

What I have learned, in the end, is that mentoring junior designers is one of the ways senior practice stays honest. It prevents fluency from hardening into dogma. It turns instinct back into explanation. It forces the work to become legible, not only to clients or stakeholders, but to the people who will carry the discipline forward.
