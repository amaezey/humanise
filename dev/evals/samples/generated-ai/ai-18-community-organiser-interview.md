# "People Already Know What Their Neighbourhood Needs": A Conversation with Lina Patel

Lina Patel does not describe herself as an activist first. She prefers "community organiser," and even that, she says, sounds more official than the work often feels. "A lot of it is chairs," she tells me, laughing. "Finding chairs, stacking chairs, realising you do not have enough chairs." Patel coordinates Eastbank Together, a fictional but familiar kind of neighbourhood group that runs food-sharing tables, tenant meetings, youth workshops, and weekend clean-ups.

We met in the back room of a local library, where Patel had just finished a meeting about pedestrian safety near the primary school. She is in her thirties, with a direct way of speaking and the tired warmth of someone who spends much of her week listening to other people's problems.

**Q: How did Eastbank Together begin?**

**Lina Patel:** It started very informally. During a rent increase wave about eight years ago, a few families in one apartment block realised they were all trying to deal with the same landlord separately. Someone put a handwritten note in the lift asking people to meet in the courtyard on Saturday. Twenty people came. They shared letters, compared rent increases, and worked out that the landlord was counting on everyone feeling alone. There was no agenda. But people left with phone numbers, and that changed things.

**Q: What made you get involved personally?**

**Patel:** My mother ran a corner shop here when I was growing up, so I knew the area through customers before I knew it through politics. People told her everything: who was sick, whose son needed a job, which building had mould. After she died, I realised the shop had been a kind of informal community centre. I think I wanted to continue that function in a different form. Also, I was angry. Anger is not enough to sustain the work, but it can get you to the first meeting.

**Q: What does the organisation actually do week to week?**

**Patel:** It depends on the week. We run a Thursday food table where people can take what they need and leave what they can. We help tenants document repairs and understand their rights. We organise translation help for council forms. There is a homework club run by volunteers, though I always say it is really a snack and listening club with some maths attached. We also campaign for safer crossings, public toilets, and keeping the health clinic open.

**Q: What do outsiders misunderstand about community organising?**

**Patel:** They think organisers arrive with solutions. Good organising is almost the opposite. People already know what their neighbourhood needs. They know which crossing is dangerous because they cross it with a pram. They know the bus timetable does not work because they lose wages waiting for it. They know the building is unsafe because their child coughs all winter. The organiser's job is to help turn private frustration into public pressure. That means connecting people, gathering evidence, and keeping everyone from burning out too quickly.

**Q: Is it hard to keep people engaged?**

**Patel:** Of course. People are busy and tired. They are working shifts, caring for parents, raising children, dealing with their own health. We make a mistake if we treat participation as a moral test. Not everyone can come to a two-hour meeting on a Tuesday night. So we try to create different levels of involvement. Someone can sign a letter, cook rice for an event, translate a flyer, speak at council, or just tell us what is happening in their building. All of that counts.

**Q: The neighbourhood is changing quickly. How do you talk about that without making it only a story of loss?**

**Patel:** Carefully. Change is not automatically bad. New people move in, and they also need connection. New cafes are not the enemy. The problem is when change means existing residents are priced out, ignored, or treated like a colourful background for someone else's lifestyle. We try to invite newer residents into the work too. If you like the neighbourhood, help protect the people who made it liveable before you arrived.

**Q: What has been the hardest campaign?**

**Patel:** Keeping the clinic open. That one was emotional because everyone had a story. Pregnant women, older men with diabetes, parents whose kids had asthma. We collected hundreds of testimonies and still had to fight for months. The hardest part was convincing officials that inconvenience can be a serious barrier. They would say, "There is another clinic two suburbs away." But for whom? With what transport? In what language? During what work hours? Policy can sound reasonable until you put an actual person inside it.

**Q: What keeps you going?**

**Patel:** Small wins, mostly. A light installed in a dark walkway. A landlord finally fixing a leak. A teenager from homework club coming back as a volunteer. Also, the meetings themselves. People think meetings are boring, and many are, but sometimes you see someone realise they are not the only one struggling. Their shoulders change. That moment is powerful.

Before leaving, Patel checks her phone and sees three missed calls. One is about a broken lift. Another is from a volunteer asking where the spare tablecloths are kept. The third, she says, can wait five minutes. Then she begins stacking the chairs.
