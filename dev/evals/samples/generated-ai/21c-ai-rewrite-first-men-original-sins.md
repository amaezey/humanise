First Men and Original Sins

Faith and the American Space Program

Kendrick Oliver. To Touch the Face of God: The Sacred, the Profane, and the American Space Program, 1957–1975. Johns Hopkins, 2013.

Catherine L. Newell. Destined for the Stars: Faith, Future, and America’s Final Frontier. Pittsburgh, 2019.

First Man. Directed by Damien Chazelle. Universal/Amblin/DreamWorks, 2018.

⸻

“Profane” is a revealing word. Its roots point to what lies outside—or more precisely, in front of (pro)—the temple (fanum). How, then, should spaceflight be understood in these terms? Is Earth the sacred centre, with outer space as its exterior? Or are the heavens themselves the temple, leaving humanity confined to a merely preliminary, secular threshold? Does the sense of awe associated with space exploration arise from a fundamentally religious instinct? Or should Apollo be read as the victory of resolutely secular science, engineering, and material capability?

Kendrick Oliver addresses these questions in To Touch the Face of God, a detailed social history of the American space program. He resists simplistic attempts to project the personal religious beliefs of astronauts and engineers directly onto Apollo. Even so, he demonstrates how thoroughly the program was infused with a providential, broadly Protestant sensibility. His analysis weighs the merits and limitations of treating spaceflight as a quasi-religious undertaking. In particular, he shows how the program intersected with national debates about church–state separation, sharpened after the 1963 U.S. Supreme Court ruling against compulsory school prayer.

A striking example came in December 1968, when the crew of Apollo 8, orbiting the moon, broadcast a message to “all the people back on earth” before reading aloud from Genesis 1. Oliver documents the scale of the response. The Christian Century described itself as “struck dumb,” while flight director Gene Kranz later recalled feeling “the presence of creation and the Creator” as he listened.

The moment was not universally welcomed. Madalyn Murray O’Hair, a central figure in the earlier school prayer case, protested the broadcast and gathered tens of thousands of supporters. Her formal objections had limited institutional impact—NASA acknowledged them but moved on—but they reflected a broader strand of opposition to Apollo.

Other critics focused on cost. The program was attacked as a misuse of public funds that could instead address urban decline or the Vietnam War. Enthusiasm waned over time. After the symbolic impact of Armstrong’s first step, public interest declined sharply through the 1970s. NASA struggled to justify continued missions once the initial objective had been achieved. The question became unavoidable: having reached the moon, what purpose remained?

Oliver suggests an alternative path. He notes that between 1968 and 1975 NASA received more than eight million letters and signatures defending astronauts’ right to religious expression in space—far exceeding correspondence on any other topic. In his view, this indicated an untapped constituency. Framing space exploration in explicitly religious terms—“going for God”—might have renewed public engagement. He implies that such a reframing could still hold relevance.

This raises a gap in Oliver’s account: the role of science fiction. Public disengagement was not with space itself but with the routine character of later Apollo missions. What mission control valued—predictability and reliability—translated into diminished spectacle. Yet in 1975, the same year as the final Apollo-Soyuz mission, George Lucas was preparing Star Wars, released in 1977. The film restored a sense of wonder and excitement to space, albeit in fictional form. Audiences accepted the substitute. In effect, cinematic space displaced real space: a more immediate, less demanding version of exploration.

The scale of this shift is measurable. The Apollo program cost roughly $25.4 billion in 1960s dollars (around $100 billion today). By contrast, the acquisition and expansion of the Star Wars franchise alone has generated tens of billions in revenue. Since the late twentieth century, expenditure on fictional representations of space has plausibly exceeded that on actual lunar exploration. This is not an argument against science fiction, but it indicates a cultural preference for mediated experience over physical achievement.

Science fiction also carries a spiritual dimension. The Force in Star Wars functions as a quasi-religious system, shaping a dedicated global fan base. The term “fan” itself shares linguistic roots with temple imagery. Large-scale, organised fandom exhibits behaviours not unlike religious communities. The persistence of such engagement suggests that the appeal of space exploration may partly reside in its capacity to host spiritual narratives.

One difficulty for real-world spaceflight is theological mismatch. The Christian God is not a specialised sky deity in the manner of Zeus or Uranus. Rather, the divine is conceived as omnipresent, not confined to a particular domain. By contrast, space exploration—particularly lunar ascent—resembles older mythic encounters with sky gods. Science fiction, more flexible in its cosmology, supplies new forms of cosmic divinity, from Lovecraftian entities to the Force itself. It may be filling a conceptual gap left by traditional theology.

Catherine Newell approaches related themes in Destined for the Stars, focusing on artist Chesley Bonestell. She traces how mid-twentieth-century American culture popularised space exploration through imagery that combined scientific plausibility with aesthetic clarity. This work, she argues, drew on earlier traditions of frontier painting, particularly the Hudson River School, linking outer space to longstanding narratives of expansion and destiny.

Newell details Bonestell’s career, including his collaborations with Willy Ley and his contributions to the film Destination Moon. However, the later sections shift toward Wernher von Braun, whose influence on the space program becomes central.

Her portrayal of von Braun is descriptive rather than evaluative. While acknowledging controversy, she emphasises his technical achievements and personal religiosity. This treatment risks minimising significant ethical issues. Von Braun’s involvement with the SS and the V-2 rocket program—linked to forced labour and mass casualties—is documented, though not foregrounded in her account.

A more balanced interpretation would treat von Braun as embodying both innovation and moral compromise. This duality is not incidental but integral to the history of Apollo. If the program is framed in religious terms, then its origins in wartime technology complicate any narrative of pure progress. The analogy to original sin is not incidental: a foundational achievement entangled with prior wrongdoing.

Newell returns, in conclusion, to Bonestell’s imagery as a source of renewed purpose. She argues that the aesthetic and spiritual qualities of his work could support a future revival of space exploration. The optimism is clear, though it sits uneasily alongside the historical realities that enabled the technology itself.

First Man (2018), directed by Damien Chazelle and starring Ryan Gosling and Clare Foy, offers a different perspective. The film underperformed commercially despite critical regard, likely due to its restrained tone. It avoids spectacle in favour of psychological focus.

Based on James Hansen’s biography, the film reconstructs the technical environment of Apollo while concentrating on Neil Armstrong’s internal life. The narrative centres on grief—specifically, the death of his daughter Karen—and its long-term effects. The lunar landing becomes less a national triumph than a personal endpoint.

Chazelle’s direction emphasises confinement and tension. Flight sequences are rendered as physically disorienting and hazardous, contrasting with the more expansive imagery typical of space films. Domestic scenes are subdued, depicting emotional restraint rather than overt drama. The result is a study of isolation rather than heroism.

The film’s closing gesture—Armstrong leaving a memento in a lunar crater—is speculative but thematically consistent. It frames the moon not as an object of conquest but as a site of release. In this sense, First Man engages the same question as Oliver and Newell: what constitutes the spiritual dimension of space exploration?

Oliver links this dimension to charisma and collective belief. First Man instead suggests a quieter interpretation. Grace, in this context, is not grandeur but relief—something encountered in stillness rather than spectacle. The film implies that the significance of the space race lies not only in technological achievement but in its capacity to hold human vulnerability.

If Apollo represents both aspiration and compromise, then its meaning may lie in that tension. The journey outward is inseparable from what is carried within: ambition, grief, and unresolved moral inheritance. Space exploration, in this reading, is neither purely sacred nor purely profane, but a convergence of both.
