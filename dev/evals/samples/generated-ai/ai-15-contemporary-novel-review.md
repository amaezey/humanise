# Review: *The Bee Sting* by Paul Murray

Paul Murray's *The Bee Sting* is the kind of contemporary novel that seems, at first, to be doing several familiar things at once. It is a family saga, a social comedy, a portrait of economic decline, and a slow-motion disaster story. Its central family, the Barneses, are outwardly ordinary enough: Dickie runs a car dealership inherited from his father, Imelda is beautiful and restless, their daughter Cass is preparing to escape to university, and their younger son PJ is drifting into the strange half-world of online friendships and private fears. The family lives in an Irish town where money has thinned out, reputations still matter, and the past has a habit of returning with a practical purpose.

What makes the novel more than an accomplished domestic drama is Murray's patience with concealment. Everyone in the book is hiding something, but the secrets are not treated simply as plot devices. They are forms of weather. They shape the pressure in rooms, the decisions people make, the jokes they tell, the purchases they cannot afford, and the messages they do not send. Murray understands that family life is often made from shared misunderstandings, and that intimacy can mean knowing exactly where not to look.

The novel is long, and it feels long in both good and occasionally frustrating ways. Murray gives each member of the Barnes family a distinct section and style, allowing the reader to move through the same household from different emotional angles. Cass's chapters have the bruised alertness of a young person who is both clever and more vulnerable than she realises. PJ's sections are especially strong in their evocation of childhood anxiety, where adults become unreadable giants and the internet offers both escape and danger. Imelda's voice, presented in a breathless rush with minimal punctuation, can initially feel like a technical challenge, but it gathers force as the reader settles into its rhythm. Dickie's story, with its mixture of shame, nostalgia, and thwarted longing, gives the novel much of its ache.

Murray is very good on money, not as an abstract theme but as a daily humiliation. The failing dealership, unpaid bills, social appearances, and desperate schemes all contribute to a sense that the family is living inside a structure that has already begun to collapse. Yet the book is not merely about recession or class anxiety. It is about the stories people tell to keep moving when the facts become unbearable. Dickie retreats into the woods to build a bunker. Imelda clings to versions of glamour and self-command. Cass imagines reinvention through departure. PJ, perhaps most heartbreakingly, looks for an adult who will tell him the truth and finds only fragments.

One of the pleasures of *The Bee Sting* is its comic intelligence. Murray has an eye for absurdity, especially the absurdity of people trying to maintain dignity under ridiculous conditions. Conversations go wrong in recognisable ways. Teenagers are cruel and funny. Adults say things that are both stupid and revealing. The comedy does not lighten the novel so much as sharpen it. It allows Murray to approach pain without turning every scene into a declaration of seriousness.

There are moments, however, when the machinery of the book becomes visible. The careful withholding of information is mostly effective, but at times one feels the author arranging revelations with slightly too much ceremony. The novel's architecture is impressive, but it can also make certain coincidences or delayed disclosures feel engineered. A reader less tolerant of maximalist family narratives may find the book overextended, particularly in the middle stretches where dread accumulates without always deepening.

Still, Murray's emotional range is difficult to dismiss. The novel keeps widening its field of sympathy. Characters who first seem vain, weak, foolish, or evasive are gradually granted histories that do not excuse them exactly, but do complicate any easy judgment. This is one of the book's major moral strengths. It suggests that people are not reducible to their worst evasions, even when those evasions harm others. The result is a novel that can be unsparing without becoming cold.

The title itself carries this doubleness. A bee sting is minor and painful, a small wound that can become dangerous depending on the body receiving it. The novel is full of such injuries: embarrassments, insults, betrayals, omissions, moments that should pass but instead lodge somewhere tender. Murray traces how these small wounds accumulate until they become fate.

As a contemporary novel, *The Bee Sting* feels ambitious in an old-fashioned way. It believes in plot, character, social texture, and the slow revelation of motive. It is not minimalist or evasive about feeling. It wants to contain a whole family and, through that family, something about the precariousness of modern life. If it sometimes strains under that ambition, it also earns much of its weight. By the end, the reader has not simply learned what happened to the Barneses. The reader has inhabited the terrible suspense of people who love one another, fail one another, and keep trying to survive the consequences.
