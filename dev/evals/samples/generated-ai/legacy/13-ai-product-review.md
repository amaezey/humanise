The Framework Laptop 16 is not just a laptop. It's a statement about what consumer electronics should be.

In a market dominated by sealed, disposable devices, Framework has built something genuinely different: a high-performance laptop where every component, from the GPU to the keyboard, can be replaced, upgraded, or repaired by the user. And after six months of daily use, I can confirm that the promise holds up in practice.

Build quality and design

The first thing you notice is the build quality. The aluminium chassis feels solid without being heavy, and the modular expansion card system on both sides adds a level of customisation that no other laptop manufacturer offers. You can swap USB-C ports for HDMI, SD card readers, or additional storage on the fly, without tools, without rebooting, and without compromise.

The 16-inch display runs at 2560x1600 with a 165Hz refresh rate. Colours are accurate, brightness is adequate for outdoor use, and the matte finish reduces glare effectively. It's not the best display in its class — the MacBook Pro's mini-LED panel is noticeably better in HDR content — but it's more than good enough for professional work.

Performance

This is where the Framework 16 makes its strongest case. The AMD Ryzen 9 7940HS delivers consistently strong performance across productivity, development, and creative workloads. Compilation times, render speeds, and multitasking responsiveness all meet or exceed what you'd expect at this price point.

The modular GPU bay is the headline feature. You can run the laptop without a dedicated GPU for maximum battery life, or slot in the AMD Radeon RX 7700S for gaming and GPU-accelerated workloads. Switching between configurations takes about two minutes and requires no technical expertise.

What works well:

The repairability is genuine, not marketing theatre. Every component is accessible, documented, and available for purchase on Framework's marketplace. I replaced the RAM (16GB to 64GB) and added a second NVMe drive in under ten minutes. The experience felt more like building a desktop than servicing a laptop.

Battery life is reasonable at 6-8 hours for productivity work, though the GPU module cuts that roughly in half. The keyboard is excellent — good travel, satisfying tactile feedback, and a sensible layout.

Where it falls short:

The trackpad, while functional, doesn't match Apple's Force Touch standard. Gestures work reliably but the surface lacks the precision and consistency you get from the best trackpads on the market.

Fan noise under sustained load is noticeable. The cooling system handles thermals well, but the acoustic profile is less refined than competitors like the ThinkPad X1 Carbon or MacBook Pro. In a quiet office, you'll hear it during video calls.

The bottom line

You can't evaluate the Framework 16 on specs alone. You can't assess it purely on build quality. And you can't judge it without considering what it represents for the industry: proof that repairable, upgradeable laptops can also be desirable ones.

At $1,399 for the base configuration, it's competitively priced against comparable machines from Lenovo and Dell that offer none of the modularity. For developers, creators, and anyone who keeps laptops for more than three years, the Framework 16 is a compelling choice that rewards long-term ownership.
