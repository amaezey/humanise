How MDMA therapy changed my life

I underwent MDMA-assisted therapy, and it profoundly transformed my life.

I had two sessions, one at the start of September and another at the beginning of October.

In terms of personal growth, it’s easily among the three most significant experiences I’ve ever had.

I’m sharing this because I genuinely believe it has the potential to help many people, yet awareness remains limited.

In this article, I’ll discuss my reasons for choosing MDMA therapy, what the experience entailed, its impact on me, and where to find further information.

This isn’t intended to persuade you to try MDMA therapy. However, I’m not neutral—I genuinely think MDMA therapy has profound therapeutic potential and will likely become central to mental health treatment in the future.

Disclaimer

It’s crucial to acknowledge:

MDMA is currently illegal in the United States.

By engaging in therapeutic MDMA use under professional supervision, I knowingly broke the law.

To protect those involved, all names related to my MDMA therapy are pseudonyms—except my own.

Yes, there’s a risk in openly writing about this under my real identity, but I’ve accepted this risk for two primary reasons:

1. MDMA therapy is nearing completion of Stage Three clinical trials and is projected to become legally available for prescribed therapeutic use within approximately four years. Authorities typically aren’t pursuing therapeutic users.
2. More importantly, I’ve always openly shared my experiences, especially when I believe the message could profoundly impact or even save lives. If there’s a legal risk involved, I’m willing to accept it.

1. Why I chose MDMA therapy

Simply put, I chose MDMA therapy because traditional methods had failed to adequately address lingering trauma.

To provide context, my therapeutic history includes:

* Four years of intensive psychoanalysis (four sessions weekly), which initially greatly benefited me, helping me understand and manage my emotions. But after four years, its effectiveness waned.
* Meditation followed, which initially aided me significantly. However, after two years, progress stalled—likely due to my lack of sustained discipline.
* Numerous other methods (yoga, CBT, EMDR) proved ineffective despite empirical support.

I then encountered a shaman—not advocating for ayahuasca—but offering to help directly. Skeptical at first, I experienced noticeable benefits from her unconventional approach. After a year, however, this method also ceased to progress my healing.

Despite extensive therapeutic exploration, a deep emotional blockage remained untouched. Recognising my unresolved trauma and emotional barriers, I sought advice from a network of trusted individuals knowledgeable about therapeutic psychedelics.

MDMA emerged consistently as the recommended choice due to its targeted effectiveness for trauma treatment. Unlike hallucinogens such as LSD or ayahuasca, MDMA’s primary therapeutic function is precisely to facilitate trauma processing. Early research indicates MDMA achieves remission in 70–85% of individuals with treatment-resistant PTSD.

Given this compelling evidence, personal testimonies, and my stalled therapeutic progress, I decided MDMA was the next logical step.

2. Finding my MDMA therapist

I was fortunate.

My publishing company, Scribe, produced a book titled Trust, Surrender, Receive by “Anne Other,” detailing MDMA therapy cases. A trusted employee profoundly benefited from Anne’s MDMA sessions, inspiring me to book my own.

3. Preparing for my first MDMA session

Anne conducted an initial call, confirming medication safety (no SSRIs allowed), understanding my motives, and providing simple session instructions:

* Minimal breakfast, arriving by 10 am.
* Initial 125mg dose, potentially followed by a 75mg dose after an hour.
* Wearing an eyeshade, listening to calming music, and embracing whatever emotional responses surfaced.
* No active therapeutic dialogue unless requested, favouring introspective processing.

4. Experiencing the first session

After ingesting the initial dose, I initially slept briefly. After waking and taking the second dose, the experience began profoundly:

Describing MDMA to someone inexperienced with it is challenging, but fundamentally, it felt like experiencing pure, unconditional love—not directed toward an external object or person, but an overwhelming internal state.

I repeatedly distracted myself with intellectual discussions rather than fully surrendering initially. Recognising this, I later allowed the medicine to trigger powerful emotional and physical (somatic) releases, especially in my legs, manifesting as involuntary shaking and deep breaths.

While MDMA intensely stimulates emotional processing, it’s uniquely manageable, allowing controlled modulation of intensity.

5. The aftermath of my first session

Post-session, I continued releasing stored trauma physically. Observations included significantly reduced aggression, less emotional reactivity, and notable improvements in relationships, as colleagues and loved ones noticed my positive emotional shifts.

This release created unexpected feelings of emptiness—like mourning the loss of familiar emotional burdens. It revealed deep unconscious reliance on anger as a coping mechanism, providing power, motivation, and control, even with its negative aspects.

6. The second session

I chose a home setting for my second session, supervised by my wife, roughly one month later. Anxiety emerged beforehand, manifesting as irritability.

This session involved deeper somatic releases—full-body movements—and emotional introspection about relationships, purpose, and past confrontations, highlighting past emotional isolation.

7. Aftermath of the second session

Post-session insights included recognising how suppressed pain had diminished my capacity for joy, playfulness, and openness. MDMA helped me understand the necessity of transitioning from a defensive warrior mentality toward becoming a constructive leader, building positively rather than defensively reacting to past hurt.

These realisations led to enhanced emotional detachment from reactive identities, fostering greater inner peace and resilience.

8. What’s next

Given my experiences, I anticipate regular future sessions, possibly annually, and considering exploration with other psychedelics. The increasing interest and private discussion within influential circles underscores a widespread therapeutic demand.

MDMA and similar treatments represent an emerging wave in mental health care, likely expanding significantly in coming years.
