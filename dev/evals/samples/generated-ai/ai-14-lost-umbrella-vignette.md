# The Red Umbrella

By the time Mara noticed the umbrella was gone, the rain had already stopped. This annoyed her more than the loss itself. If the sky had still been pouring, she could have felt properly wronged. Instead, the clouds had thinned into a weak silver light, and the pavement was only shining, not flooding. The umbrella had chosen the exact moment when it was least necessary to disappear.

It was red, the sort of red that looked cheerful even when folded. Her sister had given it to her three winters ago after watching Mara arrive at lunch with wet hair for the third time in a month. "You live like weather is a rumour," her sister had said, and pushed the umbrella across the table still wrapped in plastic. Mara had laughed, but she had kept it. Since then it had ridden in tote bags, leaned against office desks, dripped into train stations, and once sheltered both Mara and a stranger who had been crying outside a pharmacy.

Now it was missing.

She stood under the awning of the bakery and searched her bag again, though she had already searched it twice. Wallet, keys, notebook, receipt, a bruised pear, no umbrella. She looked through the bakery window at the table where she had sat with her coffee. A young father was there now, breaking a croissant into pieces for a toddler who seemed suspicious of pastry. No red umbrella leaned beside the chair.

Mara retraced her steps because that is what people do when something vanishes. They walk backward through their own recent life, hoping the past has left a clue. She passed the chemist, the newsagent, the bus stop with the torn poster for guitar lessons. At each place she paused and examined corners, chair legs, bins, doorways. The world contained many things that were not her umbrella: a black glove flattened by rain, a blue bottle cap, a receipt plastered to the curb, three cigarette ends, a feather.

At the bus stop, an old man in a brown coat watched her scan the ground.

"Lose something?" he asked.

"An umbrella," Mara said. "Red."

"Ah," he replied, as if red umbrellas were especially prone to misfortune. "Someone might have needed it."

Mara wanted to say that needing something did not make it yours, but the old man had spoken without accusation. He was looking up the street, where the gutter water moved in thin streams toward the drain. His coat collar was damp. She imagined someone taking the umbrella by mistake, or in a small panic when the rain came down hard. A student late for an exam. A nurse after a night shift. People were always inventing emergencies in which they became the main character.

"Maybe," she said.

The old man nodded, satisfied with this moral arrangement, and climbed onto the next bus.

Mara kept walking. The city after rain had a rinsed look that never lasted. Shop signs reflected in the footpath. Buses sighed at curbs. A dog shook itself violently near a cafe table, decorating the shoes of its owner, who pretended not to mind. Everyone seemed to be continuing, which felt unfair. The umbrella had been with her through several jobs, one breakup, two apartments, and a haircut she still regretted. It was not valuable, exactly, but it had been present.

Outside the library she found a red umbrella in the rack near the entrance. Her heart gave a little leap before she saw that the handle was wrong. This one was curved wood, elegant and older. Hers had a black plastic handle with a small crack near the end. Still, she stood there for a moment, looking at it. The library doors opened and a woman with a stack of returned books stepped out.

"Is that yours?" the woman asked.

"No," Mara said quickly.

The woman smiled, took the umbrella, and walked away under a sky that no longer required it.

By then Mara felt foolish. She had spent almost an hour hunting for a thing that could be replaced for fifteen dollars. She told herself this as she bought a new umbrella from the discount shop, navy blue, automatic open, with a tag promising wind resistance. It was probably better than the old one. Stronger ribs. Cleaner fabric. No crack in the handle.

At the corner, the rain began again, lightly at first and then with more conviction. People lifted newspapers over their heads and hurried under awnings. Mara pressed the button and the navy umbrella sprang open with a sharp, efficient sound. It kept the rain off perfectly. There was nothing wrong with it.

Still, as she waited for the crossing signal, she imagined the red umbrella opening somewhere else in the city. Perhaps above a person who had taken it by accident and would later feel guilty. Perhaps above someone who would never think of Mara at all. Its red circle would move through the grey afternoon, bright and separate, carrying on without her.

The signal changed. Mara stepped into the street beneath her new umbrella. Halfway across, she surprised herself by laughing. Not because the loss was funny, exactly, but because it was small and complete. Some things leave with no explanation. Some things shelter you for a while and then become part of someone else's weather.
