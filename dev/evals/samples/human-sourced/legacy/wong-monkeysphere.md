# The Monkeysphere: Why Dunbar's Number Explains Everything Wrong With the World

Have you ever wondered why the world seems to be such a mess? Why people can be incredibly kind to their friends and family, yet seemingly indifferent to the suffering of millions? The answer, it turns out, might lie in a fascinating concept from evolutionary psychology known as Dunbar's Number — or, as it's more entertainingly called, the "Monkeysphere."

## What Is the Monkeysphere?

Here's the deal. British anthropologist Robin Dunbar discovered that there's a correlation between the size of a primate's brain and the size of their social group. When he extrapolated this to humans, he came up with a number: approximately 150. That's it. That's the maximum number of meaningful relationships your brain is equipped to handle.

Think of it as your brain's social hard drive. You've got about 150 slots for people you can genuinely care about as full, three-dimensional human beings. Your family takes up some slots. Your friends take up more. Your coworkers, your neighbors, that barista who knows your order — they all take up space. And once those 150 slots are filled? Everyone else becomes, well, basically an abstraction.

## Why This Matters (A Lot)

This is where things get interesting — and a little dark. See, outside your Monkeysphere, other people aren't really "people" to your brain. They're more like background characters in a video game. You know they exist intellectually, but you don't feel it in your gut.

This is why you can hear about thousands of people dying in a natural disaster on the other side of the world and feel mildly sad for about thirty seconds before going back to worrying about whether your coworker is mad at you. It's not because you're a bad person. It's because your brain literally was not designed to process empathy at that scale.

## The Monkeysphere in Action

Let's look at some real-world implications. Ever notice how people are perfectly lovely in small towns where everyone knows each other, but turn into absolute monsters on the internet? That's the Monkeysphere at work. Online, you're interacting with people who aren't in your 150, so your brain basically treats them like NPCs. Hence, the comments section of any YouTube video.

This also explains why large corporations can make decisions that harm thousands of people without anyone in the boardroom losing sleep. To the executives, those affected aren't real people — they're numbers on a spreadsheet, safely outside the Monkeysphere.

## Can We Fix This?

The honest answer is: sort of, but not really. You can't rewire millions of years of evolution overnight. However, being aware of the Monkeysphere is the first step. When you catch yourself dehumanizing someone — whether it's a stranger online or a group of people you've never met — remember that your brain is running on ancient hardware that wasn't built for a world of eight billion people.

## Conclusion

In conclusion, the Monkeysphere is a powerful concept that explains many of the social problems we face today. From online toxicity to corporate indifference, the limitations of our social cognition play a crucial role. By understanding Dunbar's Number and its implications, we can begin to consciously work against our biological limitations and treat all people with the empathy and respect they deserve — even the ones outside our 150.
