Most meetings are a waste of time. I know that sounds like a lazy take, but after fifteen years of sitting through them in government, in agencies, in consultancies, I am fully convinced. The majority of meetings exist because someone felt obligated to schedule one, not because there was something that actually needed a room and an hour.

Think about your last week. How many meetings did you attend where you walked out knowing something you did not know before? Where a decision was made that could not have been made in a two-paragraph email? I would bet the number is close to zero. The usual rhythm is someone shares a screen, reads bullet points aloud, and then asks if anyone has questions. Nobody does, because nobody was paying full attention in the first place.

The problem is not that collaboration is bad. Collaboration is fine. The problem is that we have confused presence with participation. Sitting in a room or on a call while someone talks at you is not collaboration. It is an audience. And yet organisations treat attendance as proof that communication happened, as if hearing words is the same as understanding them and acting on them.

You don't learn how your team operates by scheduling a weekly sync. You don't build trust by forcing people into a video call every Monday morning. Those things might feel productive to the person running them, but for everyone else it is just dead time with a calendar invite.

What actually works is writing things down. Written updates, written proposals, written decisions. Writing forces clarity in a way that talking rarely does. When you have to commit your thoughts to text, you discover what you actually think versus what you were going to improvise in the moment. I have watched senior leaders completely reverse their position mid-sentence in a meeting and nobody blinked. That does not happen when you have to write it out and put your name on it.

There are exceptions, obviously. Workshops where people are sketching together, retrospectives where the whole point is surfacing different perspectives, difficult conversations that need tone and nuance. Those need a room. But those are maybe ten percent of what ends up on a calendar.

The rest is theatre. Comfortable, familiar, defensible theatre. Nobody gets in trouble for scheduling a meeting. Try cancelling six recurring meetings and replacing them with a shared document and see how people react. You would think you had suggested they come to work in pyjamas.

I am not saying abolish all meetings. I am saying most of them should never have been booked in the first place, and the ones that remain should be shorter, sharper, and far less frequent. But nobody wants to hear that, because meetings are the one place where looking busy and being busy feel like the same thing.
