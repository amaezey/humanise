I was seven when I decided I wanted to be a vet. My parents had a border collie called Meg who got hit by a car on the road outside our house. The vet, Dr Singh, came to our kitchen because Meg could not be moved. He set her leg on our kitchen table while my mother held her head and talked to her. Meg bit my mother twice. Dr Singh did not flinch.

I told everyone at school the next day that I was going to be a vet. Mrs Patterson, my teacher, said that was a fine ambition. My father said vets had to be good at science, which worried me because I was not good at science. I was good at reading and drawing and making up stories about dogs.

By the time I was twelve I had changed my mind. I wanted to be a writer instead, which my father said was worse than being a vet because at least vets got paid. He was not wrong about that.

I did not become a writer or a vet. I became an accountant, which is what happens when you are good at nothing in particular but need to pay rent. I am forty-three now and I still think about Meg on the kitchen table, and Dr Singh's hands, and the way my mother talked to the dog as if the dog could understand her. Maybe the dog could. I would not know. I am an accountant.
