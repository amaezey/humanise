How to give feedback that people actually use

I have been on both sides of bad feedback more times than I can count. I have sat through design critiques where the only comment was "I like it" or "Something feels off." I have also been the person giving unhelpful notes and wondering later why nothing changed. It took me a long time to realise that giving useful feedback is a skill, and most of us are terrible at it because nobody teaches it.

Here is what I have learned over about a decade of design work, mostly through getting it wrong first.

Step 1: Decide what you are actually reacting to

Before you say anything, figure out what is driving your response. Are you reacting to the visual design or the content? Is it the interaction pattern, or something about the underlying logic? Most bad feedback happens because the person giving it has not separated these layers. They say "this feels cluttered" when they mean "I do not understand the hierarchy" or "there is too much text." Those are different problems with different fixes.

I once spent an entire afternoon redesigning a layout because a stakeholder said it looked "busy." Turned out they just wanted the font size bigger so they could read it on their laptop without leaning forward. That would have taken thirty seconds.

Step 2: Name the problem, not the solution

This is the one that trips up almost everyone, including designers. Saying "make the button bigger" is not feedback. It is a design decision dressed up as a note. The actual feedback might be "I did not notice the primary action" or "it is not clear what I should do next."

When you name the problem, you give the person receiving the feedback room to solve it their way. That usually produces a better result because they have context you do not. They know the constraints and the technical limitations. They know about the fifteen other screens that need to stay consistent.

Step 3: Be specific about where and when

"The onboarding flow is confusing" is almost useless. Which screen? Which step? What were you expecting to see versus what you saw? Good feedback has coordinates. It points to a moment in time and a place in the interface where something went wrong.

I keep a running habit of taking screenshots when I hit friction. Not to build a case against anyone, but because by the time a feedback session rolls around I have forgotten half the details. The screenshot reminds me where I was and what threw me off.

Step 4: Say what is working too

This is not about being nice, though that helps. It is about giving the other person information about what to protect. If you only flag problems, there is a real risk they will accidentally break the things that were already good while fixing the things that were not.

A quick "the hierarchy on this page is really clear" or "the tone of this copy is spot on" gives them a foundation. They know what to keep and what to change.

Step 5: Check your assumptions at the door

You are not the user. I am not the user. The feedback we give is filtered through our own preferences and habits, our screen sizes and contexts. I have caught myself saying "nobody would do that" about something that turned out to be the most common user behaviour in testing.

So before you deliver feedback with full confidence, ask yourself whether you are describing a real usability issue or just a personal preference. Both are valid inputs. But they carry different weight, and the person receiving the feedback needs to know which one you are offering.

A few things I have learned the hard way

Giving feedback in front of a big group usually makes it worse. People perform instead of communicating. The feedback gets cautious or political depending on who is in the room.

Timing matters. Giving detailed visual feedback on a rough wireframe is like copy-editing a first draft. You are solving the wrong problems at the wrong fidelity.

And written feedback almost always lands better than verbal, because the person can sit with it and re-read it instead of having to react on the spot.

None of this is complicated. But it does require you to slow down and think before you talk, which, honestly, is harder than it sounds.
