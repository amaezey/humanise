The morning light moved across the kitchen floor in slow blocks. I noticed it because I was waiting for the kettle and had nothing else to look at.

Outside, a magpie worked at a crust someone had dropped near the bins. It tore the edges off and hopped sideways. Then another magpie landed on the fence above it.

My grandmother used to feed magpies from her hand and claimed they remembered faces, though I never tested whether that was true.

The kettle clicked off. I poured the water and waited again.

When I lived in the house with the lemon tree, the kitchen had a window that faced south. The bench under it was always warm by ten in the morning. My housemate at the time used to sit there with the cat in his lap and complain about his manager. The cat shed white hairs into his coffee and he didn't seem to notice.

I left that house in the autumn. The lemon tree had fruit on it but the fruit was hard, and the man who took over the lease said he would let it ripen. I drove past months later and the tree was gone.

In the new flat there was no garden, only a balcony with a single concrete tub. I planted basil, which died within a fortnight, and replaced it with parsley. The parsley survived two years through summers when I forgot to water it for a week at a time. By the end it was woody and the leaves had curled in on themselves, but the plant kept going.

You learn things from plants you didn't expect to learn. Mostly that the ones you fuss over die and the ones you ignore become difficult to kill.

The kettle is one I bought when I moved in here, a steel one with a flat bottom that fits the induction plate properly. Before this I had an electric one with a glass body and you could see the limescale building up at the base. It looked like a chemistry experiment after a few months and I never descaled it because I was always about to and never did.

Things accumulate over time. A plate gets a chip in it and you keep using the plate. The chip gets bigger. One morning a piece comes off in your hand and you stand at the sink looking at it and decide the plate has finally crossed the line. You put it in the bin. The bin is full so you take it down to the wheelie bin in the courtyard and on the way back up the stairs you think about everything else in the flat that has a chip in it and how you are not going to deal with any of it today.

The magpie had been joined by another magpie. They were having some kind of disagreement over the crust. The first one had it in its beak and the second one was hopping behind it making a noise I couldn't hear through the glass. After a while the first one flew up onto the fence and ate the crust there. The second one stood on the ground looking up.

I poured the tea into a mug. It was a gift from someone whose name I have forgotten. It has a picture of a fox on it, badly drawn, and the words "good morning" underneath in lower case. I have had it for at least eight years and would never throw it out, even if it cracked.

My grandfather had a workshop at the back of his house with hand tools hung on a board on the wall. Each tool had its outline drawn around it in black marker, so you could tell at a glance if anything was missing. He did the outlines himself in 1972, the year after he retired. I asked him once why he had bothered and he said it was because he liked the look of it.

The workshop is gone now, the house was sold after he died, but I think about that board sometimes when I am looking for something in my own kitchen drawers and cannot find it.

There was a fruit shop on the corner of the street I lived on before this one. The owner was Vietnamese and had been there twenty-one years, since before the suburb went expensive. He stocked things you couldn't get at the supermarket nearby. Tamarind paste came in plastic tubs from a wholesaler in Cabramatta. Fresh kaffir lime leaves, custard apples in season, dried squid wrapped in cellophane bundles. I bought my coriander there because it lasted longer than the supermarket version, and a packet of dried chillies once or twice a year. He always asked after my dog. I have never owned a dog. After the third or fourth time I corrected him I gave up and just told him the dog was well.

The light had moved off the floor and was on the side of the cupboard now. I drank the tea and watched the magpies until they both flew off, and then I went to put a load of washing on.

The washing machine was the loud kind, the kind that builds from a low rumble to a vibration that travels through the floorboards. Visitors always remarked on it. The meter reader once had to pause mid-sentence until the spin cycle changed gear.

I had been meaning to get someone in to look at it. The sticker on the back of the machine had a phone number on it, but ringing strangers about household repairs is the sort of task I find ways to avoid for months at a stretch.

I had developed a habit of running the wash only when leaving the flat. By the time I returned the cycle would be finished and the clothes ready to hang.

The flat had a small balcony with a metal railing. The clothes line was strung between two hooks I had screwed into the wall myself, after the previous occupant's line had snapped under a load of wet towels. The hooks were the wrong colour for the wall, gold instead of white, but I had not got around to painting them.

I have a habit of not getting around to things. Not in a paralysed way. More that the things I do not get around to are ones I have decided, without admitting it to myself, that I do not actually mind, even though I had originally meant to address them. The hooks. The chipped plate. The dripping tap in the bathroom that I have been about to call a plumber about for nearly a year.

The dripping tap is an interesting case. It drips roughly once every fifteen seconds. I counted, sitting on the edge of the bath one Sunday morning, while waiting for the bath to fill. After about a minute of counting I decided that the drip was actually quite restful, like a metronome, and that I would leave it alone.
