## Why this matters for the skill

Wikipedia's "Signs of AI writing" is the most heavily cited single source in `humanise/references/patterns.md` and the per-pattern evidence map. It looks like a primary source but is actually a *literature synthesis* — most of its claims are backed by academic and journalistic research it cites by footnote. This file extracts those upstream sources so the evidence map can attribute claims to the underlying research, not just to Wikipedia.

Where the article makes a claim and cites an upstream source, the upstream source is the better citation. Where the article makes a claim with no citation, that's editor consensus and should be marked as such.

## Per-pattern Wikipedia coverage and upstream attribution

Each row maps a Wikipedia-described pattern to (a) what Wikipedia says, (b) the upstream sources Wikipedia cites for that specific claim. Used by the per-pattern evidence map to attribute claims to underlying research rather than to Wikipedia.

### Significance / legacy / broader-trends inflation

**Wikipedia says:** LLMs inflate importance via generic statements about "pivotal moments" and "broader trends." Trigger phrases include "stands/serves as", "is a testament", "vital/significant/crucial/pivotal/key role", "underscores its importance", "evolving landscape", "indelible mark", "marking a pivotal moment", "represented a significant shift".

**Upstream citations:** `chronicle` (Belcher, Chronicle of Higher Education), `Juzek` (delve / lexical overrepresentation), `sun` (Sun et al., LLM idiosyncrasies).

**Maps to evidence map:** #1 Significance inflation.

---

### High-density "AI vocabulary"

**Wikipedia says:** LLMs overuse specific words at statistically unusual frequencies post-2022. Word list includes "additionally, align with, boasts, bolstered, crucial, delve, emphasizing, enduring, enhance, fostering, garner, highlight, interplay, intricate, landscape, meticulous, pivotal, robust, showcase, tapestry, testament, underscore, valuable, vibrant".

**Upstream citations (per-word):**
- `delve` → Juzek, Kobak, Russell, merrill (Washington Post tracked frequency decline 2023–2025)
- `pivotal`, `meticulous`, `intricate`, `tapestry` → Kobak, Juzek, Reinhart, Kriss
- `enhance`, `fostering`, `showcase`, `valuable` → Russell, Kobak, geng
- `vibrant`, `interplay`, `enduring` → Reinhart, Kobak
- General overuse claim → Juzek, Kobak, Reinhart, Kousha, geng, Kriss

**Maps to evidence map:** #7 AI vocabulary words and phrases. The corpus claim that "delve frequency rose ~2,700% in PubMed abstracts 2022-2024" originates from Kriss/NYT but echoes Kobak's biomedical analysis.

---

### Copula avoidance ("is/are" → "serves as", "boasts")

**Wikipedia says:** LLMs replace simple "is/are" with elaborate constructions ("serves as / stands as / marks / represents", "boasts / features / maintains / offers").

**Upstream citations:** `geng2` (Geng & Trotta — documented over 10% decrease in usage of "is"/"are" in academic writing in 2023).

**Maps to evidence map:** #8 Copula avoidance.

---

### Negative parallelisms ("not just X, but Y")

**Wikipedia says:** LLM pattern of contrast structures that seem to correct an incomplete conclusion. "Not only ... but", "It is not just ..., it's", "less X than Y", "more Y than X", "beyond X, it is Y".

**Upstream citations:** `Russell`, `merrill`, plus reference [15] (untraceable).

**Maps to evidence map:** #9 Contrived contrast / negative parallelism.

---

### Rule of three (triads)

**Wikipedia says:** Overuse of three-item lists (adjective, adjective, adjective).

**Upstream citations:** `Russell`, `Kriss`.

**Maps to evidence map:** #10 Rule of three.

---

### Synonym cycling / elegant variation

**Wikipedia says:** Overuse of synonyms or related terms instead of name repetition, attributed to repetition-penalty in LLM decoding.

**Upstream citations:** `chronicle` (Belcher names the repetition-penalty mechanism explicitly), plus a Guardian editorial style reference.

**Maps to evidence map:** #11 Synonym cycling.

---

### Title case in headings

**Wikipedia says:** AI chatbots capitalise all main words in headings.

**Upstream citations:** `Russell`.

**Maps to evidence map:** #15 Title case in headings.

---

### Em dashes

**Wikipedia says:** LLMs use em dashes more frequently than human nonprofessional text, often formulaically.

**Upstream citations:** `merrill` (Washington Post analysis), `Kriss` (NYT — drenching as quality marker), `edwards` (Ars Technica — GPT-5.1 attempts to suppress em dashes after user complaints).

**Maps to evidence map:** Em-dashes density grader check (`no-em-dashes`).

---

### Superficial -ing analyses

**Wikipedia says:** AI inserts vague "-ing phrase" analyses about significance. "Highlighting / underscoring / emphasizing / ensuring / contributing to / cultivating / fostering / encompassing / valuable insights / align with / resonate with".

**Upstream citations:** `Reinhart` (PNAS — grammatical and rhetorical style variation), `chronicle`.

**Maps to evidence map:** #3 Superficial -ing analyses.

---

### Detection ability claims

**Wikipedia says:** Heavy LLM users identify AI text ~90% of the time; non-users barely better than random.

**Upstream citations:** `Russell` (Russell, Karpinska, Iyyer — ACL 2025 paper directly establishes this finding).

**Maps to evidence map:** Background context for the skill's stance on detection vs review framing.

---

### Vague attributions / overgeneralisation

**Wikipedia says:** Attribution to vague authorities, claiming consensus where none exists. "Industry reports", "Observers have cited", "Experts argue", "Some critics argue".

**Upstream citations:** None — Wikipedia editor consensus only.

**Maps to evidence map:** #5 Vague attributions.

### Outline-like challenges/future-prospects sections

**Wikipedia says:** Rigid formula: "Despite X, faces challenges ... Future outlook remains positive."

**Upstream citations:** None — Wikipedia editor consensus only.

**Maps to evidence map:** #6 Formulaic challenges sections.

### Promotional / advertisement-like language

**Wikipedia says:** AI adopts press-release or travel-guide tone despite neutrality instructions. "Boasts a", "vibrant", "rich", "profound", "enhancing", "showcasing", "exemplifies", "commitment to", "natural beauty", "nestled", "groundbreaking", "renowned".

**Upstream citations:** None for the trigger list itself (Wikipedia editor consensus). Claim about "older LLMs (e.g. GPT-4) tend to output more blatantly positive text than newer LLMs" links to an arXiv abstract (likely `Reinhart` or `Russell` based on context).

**Maps to evidence map:** #4 Promotional language.

---

### Notability / media-coverage emphasis

**Wikipedia says:** LLMs obsessively list media coverage to "prove" notability rather than summarise content. "Independent coverage", "local/regional/national media outlets", "profiled in", "active social media presence".

**Upstream citations:** None — Wikipedia editor consensus only.

**Maps to evidence map:** #2 Notability claims.

### Patterns with NO upstream Wikipedia citation (editor consensus only)

These are described in the article but not backed by an external source:

- Knowledge-cutoff disclaimers ("As of my last training update")
- Collaborative communication / chatbot residue ("I hope this helps", "Let me know")
- Curly quotation marks
- Inline-header vertical lists
- Boldface overuse
- Skipping heading levels
- Thematic breaks before headings
- Use of Markdown formatting
- Submission statements in AFC drafts
- Pre-placed maintenance templates
- Permissions gaming
- Canned emphasis on quality / good faith
- Emoji as formatting
- Treating Wikipedia lists / broad titles as proper nouns
- Broken external links
- Invalid DOIs / ISBNs
- Reference markup bugs (`contentReference`, `oaicite`)
- Non-existent categories / templates
- Pronounced shift in writing style
- Age of text relative to ChatGPT launch

For evidence-map entries that map to these (e.g., #19 Collaborative artifacts, #17 Curly quotes, #13 Boldface overuse, #16 Emojis, #20 Knowledge-cutoff disclaimers), the citation should read "Wikipedia 'Signs of AI writing' (editor consensus, no upstream source)" — this is more honest than implying Wikipedia is itself the primary source.

## How this changes the evidence map

For each entry in the per-pattern evidence map that cites Wikipedia:

1. **If Wikipedia cites an upstream source for that specific claim**, add the upstream source to Source claims with attribution like: `Kobak (cited by Wikipedia "Signs of AI writing"): [the specific claim]`.
2. **If Wikipedia is editor consensus on that pattern**, mark explicitly: `Wikipedia "Signs of AI writing" (editor consensus, no upstream source for this claim)`.
3. **Wikipedia stays as a source** — it is genuinely a load-bearing catalogue, even when its claims are unsourced (the WikiProject AI Cleanup project is a real signal).
