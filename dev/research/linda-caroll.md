**Source:** https://lindac.substack.com/p/good-writing-ai-slop-and-the-dragon
**Published:** 3 February 2026 (The Art of Writing newsletter, Substack)
**Author:** Linda Caroll (runs four publications on Medium; writes about craft)

---

## 1. AI Writing Patterns, Tells, and Markers Mentioned

Linda Caroll's article is less a catalogue of surface-level AI tells (like em dashes or vocabulary words) and more a diagnosis of AI's **structural and experiential** limitations. The patterns she identifies:

### 1a. Genericness / Lack of Unique Human Perspective

The central AI tell Caroll identifies. Quoted directly:

> "A machine has no stories to tell, so it has to generalize."

> "AI is a machine with no human stories to tell. It can only ever be generic, and anything that's not generic has to be added in manually."

This is not about specific word choices but about the **absence of lived experience** producing text that reads as universally relatable but never personally specific. The AI output she shows on "gratitude" talks about light hitting a kitchen table, the smell of coffee, hearing your name -- all pleasant but **interchangeable observations anyone could have written**.

### 1b. "Warm Fuzzies" Tone

Caroll notes that ChatGPT's writing on gratitude succeeds commercially because it produces pleasant, emotionally safe content -- what she calls "warm fuzzies not lectures." This is a structural pattern: AI defaults to **positive, comforting, universally agreeable sentiments** rather than taking emotional risks or expressing vulnerability.

> "ChatGPT gets away with it because it's writing warm fuzzies not lectures."

### 1c. Simple Vocabulary (5th-6th Grade Reading Level)

Not framed as a flaw per se, but identified as a characteristic:

> "ChatGPT writes at a 5th to 6th grade comprehension unless you ask it to write at a higher level."

Caroll notes this is actually *why people like AI output* -- it's accessible. But it's also a tell: uniformly pitched at that level regardless of audience or topic.

### 1d. Simple Sentence Structure (One Action Per Sentence)

> "One 'action' per sentence is easier to follow. Because easy to read isn't just simple words. It's simple sentence structure, too."

AI defaults to short, single-clause sentences. This makes it readable but also contributes to a recognisable rhythm.

### 1e. Universal Relatability Without Specificity

The third "reason people respond favorably to AI":

> "They can relate. People will read along and think 'omg! me, too'"

But this is also a weakness because the relatability is **generic** -- it comes from surface-level observations rather than deeply personal stories. The relatability is broad but shallow.

### 1f. Broken Flow When Human Content Is Inserted

> "anything that's not generic has to be added in manually. Usually, manual additions break the flow."

This is a practical tell for edited AI text: when someone tries to inject personal stories or specific details into AI-generated prose, the seams show. The register shifts, the rhythm breaks.

## 2. Patterns NOT Already on the Existing Detection List

The existing list covers:
> em dashes, manufactured insight, staccato fragments, significance inflation, notability claims, superficial -ing analyses, promotional language, vague attributions, formulaic challenges sections, AI vocabulary words, copula avoidance, negative parallelisms, rule of three, synonym cycling, false ranges, boldface overuse, inline-header lists, title case, emojis, curly quotes, hyphenated compound modifier overuse, collaborative artifacts, knowledge-cutoff disclaimers, sycophantic tone, filler phrases, excessive hedging, generic positive conclusions, anaphora, contrived contrast

**New patterns from this article not clearly covered by the existing list:**

1. **Genericness / absence of lived experience** -- The existing list has "generic positive conclusions" but Caroll's point is broader: the *entire texture* of AI writing is generic, not just conclusions. It's the absence of personal anecdote, specific memory, named people, real places, felt emotions. This is more fundamental than any surface marker. Could be called **"experiential vacancy"** -- text that describes human experience without ever having had any.

2. **Warm-fuzzy defaulting / emotional safety** -- Related to "sycophantic tone" but distinct. This isn't about flattering the reader; it's about AI always choosing the comforting, positive, emotionally safe framing. It never takes an uncomfortable position, shares a painful memory, or risks making the reader feel something difficult. Could be called **"emotional risk aversion"**.

3. **Uniform reading level** -- The existing list doesn't cover AI's tendency to write at a consistent 5th-6th grade level regardless of topic or audience. This could be a statistical tell: human writers vary their reading level across pieces; AI writing clusters tightly around that band.

4. **Flow disruption in hybrid text** -- Not on the list. When human-specific content is inserted into AI-generated text, the register shift is detectable. This is a tell for *edited* AI text rather than pure AI text.

5. **Shallow relatability** -- Adjacent to "manufactured insight" but distinct. AI produces observations designed to trigger "me too!" recognition, but they're always at the surface level (coffee smells, morning light, the sound of your name). Never the deep, uncomfortable, specific relatability of a real story.

## 3. Before/After Examples

Caroll provides a direct **AI vs. human** comparison on the same prompt: "write up to 200 words on gratitude."

### AI version (ChatGPT):

> "Sometimes gratitude arrives on a regular day, when nothing is happening. Like noticing how the light hits your kitchen table in the morning. Or the way your coffee smells before you even take a sip and savoring it. Like hearing someone say your name and realizing how much you like the way it sounds in their voice."
>
> "Maybe that's all gratitude needs to be. Just paying attention. Letting yourself feel the small, ordinary pieces of life. And for a few seconds, letting yourself be glad for them. Sometimes, the best kind of gratitude arrives when you aren't even looking for a reason to be grateful."

**Characteristics Caroll identifies:** Simple vocabulary, simple sentence structure, universally relatable, pleasant. But generic -- no specific person, place, memory, or risk.

### Human version (Alice Wild):

> "Melody Beattie said, 'Gratitude unlocks the fullness of life,' but I don't believe that."
>
> "Maybe it's because I grew up in a home where gratitude meant something else. Maybe it's because my dad used to rattle off judgment and self-righteousness faster than the preacher that Sunday, but then always told me to 'have some gratitude.'"
>
> "Stay quiet. That's what he really meant. A thorough resection of my identity. Except for a small trembling flame left in that hollow place..."

**What makes the human version work, per Caroll:**
- Specific person (her dad), specific scene (Sunday, the preacher)
- Emotional risk -- contradicting a beloved quote, sharing painful family history
- Vulnerability -- "she said she was nervous sharing that"
- The reader can identify ("Who hasn't felt silenced?") but through a deeply *specific* story, not a generic observation

> "ChatGPT can never share a story like that. It doesn't have any. It doesn't have a dad. No one ever tried to silence it."

## 4. Insights About WHY These Patterns Exist

Caroll doesn't discuss model architecture, RLHF, or training data in technical terms, but she provides a clear **experiential** explanation for why AI writes the way it does:

### 4a. No Lived Experience

> "A machine has no stories to tell, so it has to generalize. How ironic, considering that AI has a belly full of stories stolen from other writers. And yet, even with a belly full of peoples' stories, it lacks what every writer has. One person's lived experience."

This is her core thesis on causation: the model has ingested millions of stories but cannot *have* a story. It can recombine and generalise from training data, but it cannot produce the kind of specific, risky, personal writing that comes from actually having lived through something.

### 4b. Training on Human Mediocrity

Caroll doesn't phrase it this way, but her argument implies it: AI succeeds commercially because it mimics the patterns that already work for mediocre human writing. It writes at 5th-6th grade level because that's what gets engagement. It produces "warm fuzzies" because that's what gets hearts on Notes. It generalises because general content reaches the widest audience.

The implication is that AI is a **mirror of average human writing** -- and the reason people can't always distinguish AI from human work is that much human writing is already generic, lecture-y, and cliched:

> "The problem with calling AI slop is that it creates a subconscious divide that says AI is bad, and therefore human is good. But the truth is, a lot of human writing isn't very good."

> "If I asked a hundred people to write me a short excerpt on gratitude, a good chunk, maybe close to half, would write it using the word 'you.' As in 'you should' -- like they're preaching to the world about how we should collectively be."

### 4c. The "You Should" Pattern Appears in Both

Caroll notes that the finger-wagging, lecturing "you should" pattern is common in both human and AI writing, though humans do it more aggressively:

> "It's so common, even ChatGPT did that. Except there's a difference in how humans do it. Humans use it more like pointing the finger. Lecturing."

This suggests a shared origin in training data: AI learned to use "you" constructions because they're massively overrepresented in the writing it was trained on.

## 5. Additional Notable Points

### On the Term "Slop"

Caroll pushes back on using "slop" as a dismissive label for AI content, arguing it creates a false sense of superiority:

> "That's how we use the word. To mock AI... But it's a little deeper. It's about mocking the people using it, too. Isn't it? Like, we're the 'real' writers. And they're just using AI. Churning out slop."

> "When I read that, I thought wow, don't we just love our moral high ground."

### Common Human Writing Problems (Relevant to Detection)

Caroll lists several patterns common in human writing that are *also* present in AI writing, making detection harder:

- **Excessive wordage** -- "Ninety of a hundred would use more words than they need."
- **Lecturing / "you should" constructions** -- moralising tone
- **Cliched thinking / tropes** -- "some kind of trope, like saying there are people who are worse off, or we should be grateful because it's how god wants us to be"
- **Writing to wordcount** -- stretching out unnecessarily

These overlap with AI patterns, which is precisely why AI passes for human in many contexts.

### The Zinsser Method (What Good Writing Looks Like)

Caroll cites William Zinsser's Yale exercise as the antidote to both AI and mediocre human writing:

> "When they turned in the essay, he'd tell them to take it home and cut it in half without losing any meaning... the finished piece would always be so much stronger."

And Dr. Seuss:

> "the writer who breeds more words than he needs, is making a chore for the reader who reads."

## 6. Summary Assessment for Humanise Skill

**Relevance to detection:** Moderate-high. This article doesn't catalogue surface tells (specific words, punctuation patterns) but identifies the **deeper structural pattern** that underlies many surface tells: **genericness born from having no lived experience**. This is arguably the root cause behind many items on the existing list (manufactured insight, generic positive conclusions, significance inflation, filler phrases).

**Key additions for the humanise skill:**

| Pattern | Description | Already covered? |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|
| Experiential vacancy | Text describes human experience without specificity -- no named people, places, real memories | Partially (generic positive conclusions) but this is broader |
| Emotional risk aversion | Always choosing the safe, comforting, positive framing; never uncomfortable or vulnerable | Not covered (sycophantic tone is adjacent but different) |
| Uniform reading level | Consistently 5th-6th grade regardless of topic/audience | Not covered |
| Hybrid flow disruption | Register/rhythm breaks where human content was inserted into AI text | Not covered |
| Shallow relatability | Designed-to-resonate observations that are broad but never specific | Partially (manufactured insight) |
| "Warm fuzzies" default | Defaulting to pleasant, emotionally safe content | Not covered distinctly |

**The article's most useful insight for the humanise skill:** The antidote to AI writing is not avoiding specific words or punctuation -- it's **writing from lived experience with emotional risk**. Detection should look not just for the presence of AI tells but for the *absence* of specificity, vulnerability, and personal stakes.
