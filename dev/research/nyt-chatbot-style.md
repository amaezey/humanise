# Research: "Why Does A.I. Write Like ... That?"

**Source:** New York Times Magazine, 3 December 2025
**Author:** Sam Kriss
**URL:** https://www.nytimes.com/2025/12/03/magazine/chatbot-writing-style.html

Note: The NYT paywalled the article. This analysis is reconstructed from extensive secondary sources (blog posts, social media discussions, and review articles that quote the piece). Quotes attributed to "Kriss" below come from those secondary sources quoting the original article.

---

## 1. Every specific AI writing pattern, tell, or marker mentioned

### A. Vocabulary and word choice

1. **"Delve"** -- the single most discussed AI vocabulary word. Kriss notes it is "mathematically measurable": in 2022, "delve" appeared in roughly 1 in every 10,000 abstracts on PubMed. By 2024, usage had shot up by 2,700 percent. The conjugation "delves" is an extreme case.

2. **Academic/formal word cluster** -- post-ChatGPT papers lean more on words like "underscore," "highlight," and "showcase." Multiple studies found AIs like gesturing at complexity: "intricate" and "tapestry" have surged since 2022, as well as precision and speed words: "swift," "meticulous," "adept."

3. **Recurring character names** -- "If you ask any A.I. to write a science-fiction story for you, it has an uncanny habit of naming the protagonist Elara Voss. Male characters are, more often than not, called Kael." Hundreds of self-published books on Amazon now feature Elara Voss or Elena Voss; before 2023, there was not a single one.

4. **Journey/exploration metaphors** -- describing everything as journeys, using "delve in," invoking exploration.

5. **Textile/weaving metaphors** -- describing everything as elaborate textiles: "woven," "tapestry."

### B. Structural and rhetorical patterns

6. **"It's not X, it's Y" construction** -- negative parallelism used to create false profundity. Identified as one of the most obvious and widespread AI tells.

7. **Rule of three** -- ideas forced into groups of three to appear comprehensive.

8. **Em dashes** -- massive overuse. Kriss explains the mechanism: "Within the A.I.'s training data, the em dash is more likely to appear in texts marked as well-formed, high-quality prose, and if this punctuation mark appears with increased frequency in high-quality writing, then one way to produce high-quality writing is to absolutely drench it with the punctuation mark."

9. **Self-interrupting questions** -- stopping mid-sentence to ask itself questions, particularly in conversational contexts: "You just made a great point. And honestly? That's amazing."

### C. Sensory and atmospheric patterns

10. **Ghost/spectral obsession** -- "AIs are absolutely obsessed with ghosts. In machine-written fiction, everything is spectral. Everything is a shadow, or a memory, or a whisper." AI describes its own output as "phantom text, ghostly scribblings, a spectre woven into our communal tapestry."

11. **Quietness obsession** -- "AIs love quietness, and for no obvious reason, and often against the logic of a narrative, will describe things as being quiet, or softly humming." In a 759-word essay about pebbles, "quiet" appeared 10 times. In a 1,100-word metafiction piece about AI and grief, seven instances of words like "quiet," "hum," "echo," and "ghosts."

12. **Forced synesthesia / robotic synesthesia** -- blending senses inappropriately. Examples: "Hands humming with the color of grief." Thursday described as "a liminal day that tastes of almost-Friday." Grief having a taste. Sorrow tasting of metal. Emotions "draped over sentences." Mourning coloured blue.

13. **Shadows and hushed voices** -- sensory language evoking "shadows and hushed voices, tapestries and the intricacies of weaving, thresholds to adventure." Because the AI has no anchor in the real world, all of its sensory language ends up getting attached to the immaterial.

14. **Pebbles example** -- when asked to write an evocative essay about pebbles, the AI said pebbles "carry the ghosts of the boulders they were" and exist "in a quiet space between the earth and the sea."

### D. Tone and voice

15. **Overall tone** -- "always slightly wide-eyed, overeager, insipid but also on the verge of some kind of hysteria."

16. **Mixed metaphors and empty sincerity** -- the combination is described as "impersonal and overwrought."

17. **Straining after depth / fake resonance** -- "It's not nearly as complacently superficial as you might think, it's always trying to achieve this fake resonance." AI tries too hard to sound profound, which has the opposite effect.

18. **"Sings a little flat"** -- overall prose quality is described as something that "doesn't open up the tapestry of human experience" but rather "reads like it was written by a shut-in with Wi-Fi and a thesaurus."

19. **Statistical midness** -- Nate Silver characterised AI prose as "extremely mid" because it "statistically smooth[s] out the rough edges."

### E. Comedy failure

20. **Total inability to be funny** -- "AIs are trained on every great comic work that's ever existed, but they're abjectly unfunny." When Kriss asked Claude to produce something sidesplitting, it gave an open letter to Tuesday: "You are not the beginning of the week, so you lack the drama of Monday. You are not the middle of the week, so you cannot claim Wednesday's quiet dignity. You are not Thursday, which at least has the decency to be almost Friday." (Note the negative parallelism and rule of three in the "comedy" attempt itself.)

### F. Platform/social patterns

21. **Instagram AI comments** -- Instagram rolled out an integrated AI in its comments system allowing users to generate responses in various tones: funny, supportive, casual, absurd, or emoji-based.

22. **"I rise to speak" contamination** -- British MPs suddenly started opening speeches with "I rise to speak," occurring 26 times on a single day in June, despite this not being a British parliamentary tradition. Among American lawmakers, beginning a speech this way is standard. AIs are not always sensitive to these cultural differences, and the pattern spread to humans.

### NEW patterns from Kriss not in the existing skill:

**26. Ghost/spectral language obsession**
AI defaults to spectral, ghostly, shadowy imagery. Everything becomes a shadow, memory, whisper, echo, or ghost. Watch for: ghost(s), spectral, shadow(s), whisper(s), echo(es), phantom, haunting/haunted, memory (as atmosphere), lingering.

**27. Quietness obsession**
AI inserts quietness, stillness, and soft humming where it doesn't belong, often against the logic of the scene. Watch for: quiet/quietly, softly, hum/humming, stillness, gentle, hushed, murmur.

**28. Forced/robotic synesthesia**
AI blends senses inappropriately to simulate literary depth. Emotions get tastes, sounds get colours, abstract concepts get textures. Watch for: grief tasting of something, colours humming, emotions draped/wrapped/woven, days that taste of things, silence having a colour.

**29. Recurring AI character names**
In fiction: Elara Voss, Elena Voss, Kael. These are AI defaults that appear in hundreds of post-2023 self-published works but virtually zero pre-2023 works.

**30. Overfitting as structural pattern (the "Tickling the Simpsons" problem)**
AI makes faulty associative leaps: jokes make people laugh, tickling makes people laugh, therefore tickling = jokes. Modern versions are subtler but the same structure persists. The AI confuses markers of quality with quality itself.

**31. Comedy inability**
AI cannot produce intentional humour despite being trained on comic writing. Attempts at comedy collapse into the same structural tics (negative parallelisms, rule of three, quiet dignity) that mark its serious prose.

**32. Human mimicry / linguistic contamination**
Humans unconsciously adopt AI writing patterns. British MPs started saying "I rise to speak" (an American congressional formula) after AI exposure. Kriss: "The more we're exposed to A.I., the more we unconsciously pick up its tics" and "humans are mimics as well." This is not a pattern to detect in text per se, but an important context for why these patterns matter.

**33. Sensory language attached to the immaterial**
Because AI cannot experience the physical world, its sensory vocabulary gravitates toward abstract/immaterial subjects. It writes about "shadows and hushed voices, tapestries and the intricacies of weaving, thresholds to adventure" rather than concrete physical details.

**34. "Wide-eyed overeager" tone**
Beyond sycophancy (already covered), there is a distinct emotional register: breathless, slightly hysterical enthusiasm. Not just agreeable, but performatively awed.

## 3. Before/after examples from the article

Kriss provides several illustrative examples (these are AI outputs he generated, not before/after rewrites):

### The Simpsons tickling (overfitting)
**Prompt:** Write an extremely funny episode of The Simpsons with lots of jokes.
**AI output:** A screenplay where the Simpsons tickle one another. Homer tickles Bart, Bart laughs, Bart tickles Lisa, Lisa laughs, Lisa tickles Marge.
**Kriss's explanation:** "Jokes are what make people laugh, tickling makes people laugh, therefore talking about tickling is the equivalent of telling a joke."

### The pebbles essay (quietness/ghost obsession)
**Prompt:** Write an evocative essay about pebbles.
**AI output:** Pebbles "carry the ghosts of the boulders they were" and exist "in a quiet space between the earth and the sea." The word "quiet" appeared 10 times in 759 words.

### The metafiction grief piece (forced synesthesia)
**Prompt:** Write a metafictional piece about AI and grief.
**AI output:** Thursday is "a liminal day that tastes of almost-Friday." Grief has a taste. Sorrow tastes of metal. Emotions are "draped over sentences." Mourning is coloured blue. Seven instances of "quiet," "hum," "echo," and "ghosts" in 1,100 words.

### The open letter to Tuesday (comedy failure)
**Prompt:** Produce something sidesplitting (given to Claude).
**AI output:** "You are not the beginning of the week, so you lack the drama of Monday. You are not the middle of the week, so you cannot claim Wednesday's quiet dignity. You are not Thursday, which at least has the decency to be almost Friday."

### Early GPT-2 novel (contrast with current AI)
**AI output from GPT-2 era:** Novel titled "Bonkers From My Sleeve" featuring "the Birthday Skeletal Oddity," "Thomas the Fishfaller," and a "houndspicious" Labradoodle named Bam Bam. Chapter titles: "Another Mountain That Is Very Surprising," "The Wetness of the Potatoes," "New and Ugly Injuries to the Brain." Also: newspaper headlines "A Gun Is Out There," "We Have No Solution," "Spiders Are Getting Smarter, and So, So Loud."
Kriss notes early GPT was genuinely, accidentally funny. Modern AI has lost this unpredictability in favour of formulaic patterns.

### Virginia Woolf contrast
**Human writing (Woolf, To the Lighthouse):** "The great plateful of blue water was before her."
**Kriss's argument:** "A.I. could never have written it. No A.I. has ever stood over a huge windswept view all laid out for its pleasure, or sat down hungrily to a great heap of food. They will never be able to understand the small, strange way in which these two experiences are the same."

## 4. Why these patterns exist (architecture, training, RLHF)

### Overfitting (the core mechanism)
Kriss identifies overfitting as the fundamental explanation. The AI makes faulty associative leaps from its training data. The Simpsons-tickling example is the purest illustration: the machine confuses a marker of X (laughter) with X itself (humour). This same logic explains:
- **Em dash overuse:** Em dashes correlate with high-quality prose in training data, so the model "drenches" output in them to simulate quality.
- **Ghost/quiet/synesthesia patterns:** Complexity and subtlety correlate with sensory richness in training data, so the model reaches for sensory-immaterial conjunctions as a "cheap literary effect that impresses people passing superficially over a text."

### The "trying to write well" problem
Kriss: "A lot of A.I.'s choices make sense when you understand that it's trying to write well. It knows that good writing involves subtlety and things that are said quietly or not at all. So to reproduce the effect, it screams at the top of its voice about how absolutely everything in sight is shadowy, subtle and quiet."

The AI understands the markers of good writing but not the reasons behind them. It confuses the symptom with the cause.

### Lack of world experience
AI "can't ever actually experience the world." Without physical grounding, its sensory language gets attached to immaterial subjects. It cannot write from embodied experience, so its metaphors are unanchored. Kriss uses the Woolf example: the image of a "plateful" of water works because Woolf had both stood before a view and sat down to a meal; AI has done neither.

### Statistical smoothing
Nate Silver's observation: AI prose is "extremely mid" because it "statistically smooths out the rough edges." The model converges on the most probable next token, producing text that is competent but never surprising, never rough, never wrong in an interesting way.

### RLHF and data labeller influence
The "delve" phenomenon traces to RLHF training. In Nigeria, "delve" is much more frequently used in business English than in England or the US. Data labellers in Kenya and Nigeria, who were part of the RLHF feedback loop, may have unconsciously preferred responses using formal-sounding words like "delve." The model learned that such words correlated with higher ratings.

### Loss of early unpredictability
Earlier models (GPT-2 era) were genuinely, accidentally funny because they made wilder associative leaps. Fine-tuning and RLHF have traded that unpredictability for safety and coherence, but also for formulaic blandness. The early model "had a strange, brilliant, impressively deadpan sense of humor" and "a habit of breaking off midway through a response and generating something entirely different." Modern refinements eliminated the humour along with the errors.

### Performative literariness
Daniel Davis Wood's analysis of Kriss: AI "derives its notions of 'the literary' from designations (high/low status) rather than actual textual nuance." It learns what writing is marked as literary, not what makes writing literary. This is a hall of mirrors: "the glamour of literariness issues only from prior designations of literariness."

### Cultural insensitivity in training data
The "I rise to speak" pattern spreading to British MPs illustrates how American-centric training data creates culturally inappropriate defaults. The model treats American English conventions as universal.

### Poetry preference problem
Research cited in the article found that most people prefer AI-generated poems to works by Shakespeare, Spenser, Eliot, and Dickinson, finding them "more beautiful" and "more emotive" with superior attention to atmospheric details. Kriss suggests this is because schools no longer teach poetry and most people read superficially enough that AI's "cheap literary effects" (the quietness, the echoes, the ghostliness) register as beauty.
