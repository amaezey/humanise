## 1. Every specific AI writing pattern, tell, or marker mentioned

The article organises tells into four categories: Red Herrings (yellow flags), Stylistic Tics, Structural Patterns, and Uncanny Content. Below is every pattern with direct quotes.

### Red Herrings (yellow flags, not conclusive on their own)

**A. Academic vocabulary**
> "Yes, AI uses a more sophisticated vocabulary than the average person - words like 'delve,' 'unpack,' 'ascertain,' and 'multifaceted.'"

Guo says this is not a reliable tell alone because professionals also use these words: "If someone writes, 'We need to ascertain the root cause,' in a work email, that doesn't mean ChatGPT wrote it. It might just mean they work in consulting."

**B. Lack of typos / perfect grammar**
> "a lack of typos may mean the text went through spell check, not that it was entirely generated by AI. Perfect grammar is no longer the smoking gun it once was."

**C. Lack of contractions**
> "writing that avoids contractions - 'it is' instead of 'it's,' 'do not' instead of 'don't' - might just be the result of superficial editing, or sometimes even an artifact of learning English as a second language."

### Stylistic Tics

**D. Em dashes**
> "One of the first telltale signs of AI writing was em dashes."
> "em dashes are something very few humans use in day-to-day writing, unless you're a professional columnist."

Cites "The Em Dash Conspiracy" (Reddit/GitHub), real data showing em dash usage in Reddit comments tripled in one year across tech/startup subreddits.

**E. Parallelism ("It's Not X, It's Y")**
> "Parallelism itself isn't the issue - it's a perfectly legitimate rhetorical device. But AI uses it constantly, reflexively, in situations where it adds nothing."

Four sub-patterns identified:

**E1. Snappy triads (rule of three)**
> "Giving three examples to drive a point home, or otherwise creating three-beat cadences. 'Fast, efficient, and reliable.' 'Think bigger. Act bolder. Move faster.'"

**E2. Unearned profundity**
> "Serious, narrative-shifting transitions that come out of nowhere. 'Something shifted.' 'Everything changed.' 'But here's the thing.'"

**E3. Mid-sentence questions**
> "'But now? You won't be able to unsee this one.' 'The solution? It's simpler than you think.'"

**E4. Vapid openers and transitions**
> "'As technology continues to evolve,' 'In today's fast-paced world,' 'At the end of the day.'"

**F. Random formatting / boldface**
> "random words or sentences are bolded without it being obvious why, it might have been AI-generated. The bolding doesn't necessarily emphasize key points - it's just sort of there."

**G. Unicode formatting characters**
> "when the author uses Unicode characters to format text as bold or italic... most authors (even professional ones) aren't going out of their way to grab Unicode arrows or multiplication signs to add to their text. This feels like almost exclusively an AI thing."

### Structural Patterns

**H. Lists (excessive bullet points)**
> "ChatGPT loves making lists."
> "if I'm looking at written text and see tons of bullet points, I'm immediately skeptical."

**I. Emojis in professional contexts**
> "No sane person I know is regularly formatting their work emails with a list of emoji-led bullet points"

Example given:
> "Complete quarterly report / Analyze market trends / Generate innovative solutions" (each led by emoji)

Notes GPT-4o does this more than predecessors.

**J. Monotony (sentence-level rhythm uniformity)**
> "AI-generated content often has a repetitive structure. Sentences are roughly the same length. Paragraphs follow the same rhythm. The cadence never varies."

**K. Fixed point-of-view / tense consistency**
> "Creative writing varies not only sentence structure, but also tense and point of view. It's uncommon to see LLMs switch from second or third person to first, and vice versa. They tend to pick a lane and stay in it with unnatural consistency."

### Uncanny Content

**L. Awkward / generic analogies and metaphors**
> "a lot of AI-generated metaphors feel like they're in the right ballpark, but the author clearly didn't think them through."
> "Human metaphors tend to be either highly specific (drawing from personal experience) or culturally resonant (drawing from shared references). AI metaphors are... plausible. Generic. They gesture toward meaning without quite achieving it."

Examples from article's ChatGPT-generated ukulele metaphors:
- "Learning the ukulele is like teaching your fingers to dance again after years of sitting still."
- "Every chord is a puzzle piece that finally clicks into a song."
- "Your first strums sound like a toddler learning to talk -- nonsense syllables slowly forming words."
- "Learning an instrument is a mirror for learning itself: messy, slow, and quietly addictive."

**M. Filler / saying nothing at length**
> "I'll find myself halfway through an article or very long comment and realize I have no clue what the author is actually saying. The words individually make sense, but there isn't a coherent throughline to what's being communicated."
> "a paragraph has said in four sentences something that could easily have been said in one."

**N. Surface polish with nothing underneath**
> "That specific combination - surface polish with nothing underneath - is AI's signature."

### Patterns from the embedded example (ukulele post)

The article's AI-generated example demonstrates multiple tells in one piece:
- Emoji-led bullet points
- Bold inline headers ("Start with songs, not scales.")
- Em dashes throughout
- "It's not just about the music -- it's about the joy of creativity" (negative parallelism / contrived contrast)
- "Whatever your 'ukulele' is -- start strumming." (manufactured profundity, metaphor overextension)
- "awkward, incremental, but unmistakably alive" (rule of three, atmospheric adjective)
- "No teacher, no plan" (staccato parallel fragments)

### NEW patterns not in the existing list

**1. Unearned profundity / dramatic narrative transitions**
Guo describes: "Serious, narrative-shifting transitions that come out of nowhere. 'Something shifted.' 'Everything changed.' 'But here's the thing.'"

The humanise list covers *manufactured insight* (performed knowingness like "Here's the thing:") and *staccato fragments* ("That matters."), but the specific sub-pattern of **standalone dramatic narrative beats** used as paragraph openers/transitions ("Something shifted." "Everything changed.") is not explicitly called out. These are more specific than staccato fragments because they claim a narrative turning point rather than just being short for emphasis.

**Verdict: Partially new.** "But here's the thing" is already in manufactured insight. "Something shifted" and "Everything changed" are a blend of staccato fragments and manufactured insight but are not explicitly listed as words to watch in either pattern.

**2. Mid-sentence rhetorical questions**
> "'But now? You won't be able to unsee this one.' 'The solution? It's simpler than you think.'"

The humanise list does not mention this pattern at all. These are short questions dropped mid-paragraph as a pacing device, followed by a declarative answer. They are distinct from filler phrases and manufactured insight.

**Verdict: NEW.** Not covered.

**3. Unicode symbol substitution (non-standard formatting characters)**
> "most authors (even professional ones) aren't going out of their way to grab Unicode arrows or multiplication signs to add to their text"

The humanise list covers emojis (#16) and curly quotes (#17) but does not mention the broader pattern of Unicode symbol substitution: using Unicode bold/italic characters instead of markdown/HTML formatting, or inserting Unicode arrows, multiplication signs, and similar symbols.

**Verdict: NEW.** Not covered. Curly quotes are one narrow case; this is broader.

**4. Awkward / generic metaphors**
> "AI metaphors are... plausible. Generic. They gesture toward meaning without quite achieving it."
> "Human metaphors tend to be either highly specific (drawing from personal experience) or culturally resonant (drawing from shared references)."

The humanise list does not have a pattern for AI metaphor quality. The closest is promotional language (#4) and significance inflation (#1), but neither addresses the specific problem of metaphors that are technically coherent but lack specificity or personal grounding.

**Verdict: NEW.** Not covered.

**5. Lack of point-of-view variation**
> "It's uncommon to see LLMs switch from second or third person to first, and vice versa. They tend to pick a lane and stay in it with unnatural consistency."

The humanise SKILL.md mentions "Creative writing varies not only sentence structure, but also tense and point of view" under Personality and soul, but the patterns.md catalogue does not include this as a numbered pattern with words-to-watch or before/after examples.

**Verdict: Partially new.** Acknowledged in the skill's guidance but not a catalogued detection pattern.

**6. Excessive list-making (bullet point density)**
> "ChatGPT loves making lists."

The humanise list covers emoji-led bullets (#16) and inline-header lists (#14), but not the broader meta-pattern of excessive or unnecessary list-making itself. A piece that converts flowing prose into bullet points when the content does not warrant it is a separate tell from the formatting of those bullets.

**Verdict: NEW.** The existing patterns address how lists are formatted, not whether the content should be a list at all.

### Summary of genuinely new patterns

1. **Mid-sentence rhetorical questions** ("But now? You won't be able to unsee this one.")
2. **Unicode symbol substitution** (Unicode bold/italic, arrows, multiplication signs)
3. **Generic / ungrounded metaphors** (plausible but lacking specificity or personal experience)
4. **Excessive list-making** (converting prose to bullet points unnecessarily)
5. **Unearned dramatic transitions** ("Something shifted." "Everything changed.") -- partially new, extends existing patterns

## 4. Insights about WHY these patterns exist

### RLHF / training incentives

**Lists and bullet points:**
> "It's something about its RLHF training, I think - people are more inclined to be impressed by answers that contain bullet points and will rate those responses higher."

This is the only direct attribution to a specific training mechanism. Guo connects list-making directly to reinforcement learning from human feedback, where human raters reward structured-looking responses.

### Training data (long-form journalism)

**Em dashes, parallelism, rhetorical flourishes:**
> "Long-form articles, particularly those published at prominent outlets (e.g., The New York Times, The Atlantic, Wired), were part of ChatGPT's training data. It literally learned to write from that style, among others. The em dashes, the parallelism, even some of the rhetorical flourishes - these aren't inherently bad."

This explains why AI writing patterns are borrowed from real human writing but deployed indiscriminately: the model learned the surface features without the editorial judgment about when to use them.

### Statistical prediction (stochastic parrot framing)

**Filler / surface polish with no substance:**
> "I empathize most with AI critics who claim it's nothing more than a 'stochastic parrot' - a prediction engine that generates plausible tokens without understanding or caring whether those tokens convey meaning."
> "That specific combination - surface polish with nothing underneath - is AI's signature."

This frames the filler problem as a consequence of next-token prediction: the model produces text that is statistically plausible at the token level but lacks coherent argumentation at the paragraph level.

### Frequency vs intentionality (the core insight)

> "a good writer uses these devices sparingly, and with intention. They deploy an em dash when they need that specific dramatic pause. AI litters them throughout its writing indiscriminately."

This is perhaps the article's most useful insight for detection: the problem is not the existence of any single pattern but its **density and lack of purpose**. Human writers use em dashes, triads, and parallelism, but as deliberate choices. AI uses them as default modes.

### Model-specific differences

> "I've noticed that GPT-4o does this more than its predecessors (and anecdotally, more than GPT-5). It's rare for me to see Claude do it, though that may be biased by my system prompts."

This is about emoji usage specifically. Guo notes variation across model families and versions, suggesting these patterns are not universal to all LLMs but are artefacts of specific training runs and RLHF configurations.

### The feedback loop

> "We're in a strange feedback loop where AI learns from us, we adjust to distinguish ourselves from AI, and AI learns from those adjustments. Each iteration narrows the space of 'authentic human writing' a little more."

Guo invokes Churchill's "We shape our buildings; thereafter they shape us" to describe how AI writing patterns are now changing human writing behaviour. Writers become self-conscious about using em dashes or parallelism because those features have become associated with AI, even though they were legitimate human devices first.

### AI detectors don't work

> "so-called 'AI writing detectors' don't work. They produce false positives at alarming rates, flagging human-written content as AI-generated based on crude heuristics."

Cites MIT source. This frames the article's pattern-matching approach as a human skill rather than an automated tool, and acknowledges its fundamental limitation: "just because something has all the tells doesn't mean a human didn't write it."

### Future erosion of tells

> "as AI gets better - as more advanced versions of ChatGPT, Claude and Gemini arrive - these tells will become less reliable. Even the act of me documenting these tells will get vacuumed up into a future LLM to help it reason through generating output that's less immediately recognizable as synthetic."

The patterns are transient. The article itself will become training data that helps future models avoid the very tells it documents.

## 5. Key takeaways for the humanise skill

### Patterns worth adding to the catalogue:
1. **Mid-sentence rhetorical questions** -- distinct pattern, easy to detect, not currently covered
2. **Unicode symbol substitution** -- broader than curly quotes, practical to detect
3. **Generic metaphors** -- harder to detect programmatically but important for the self-audit loop
4. **Excessive list-making** -- the meta-pattern above existing list-formatting patterns
5. **Dramatic narrative transitions** -- could be added as words-to-watch under manufactured insight

### Reinforces existing patterns:
- Em dashes as the single most cited tell (confirms the hard constraint)
- RLHF as root cause for list-making and structure-over-substance
- Density/frequency as the real signal, not individual occurrence
- The "surface polish with nothing underneath" framing aligns with the manufactured insight hard constraint

### Guo's personal defence against AI slop:
> "It's to cultivate specificity: to write things rooted in particular knowledge and tangible experience. To develop a voice and a point of view, and stay as true to them as I can. These are things that AI still struggles to replicate convincingly."

This aligns with the humanise skill's "Personality and soul" section and its emphasis on being specific rather than atmospheric.
