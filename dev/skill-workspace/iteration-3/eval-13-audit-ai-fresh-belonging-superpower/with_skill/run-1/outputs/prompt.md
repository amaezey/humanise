I walked into the session expecting a tools demo. What I got instead was a set of organising instincts—patterns the Loomio founders have learned the hard way while building software for groups that don’t rely on hierarchy to get things done. The throughline was simple: decentralisation doesn’t happen by default. It’s designed, practised, and maintained.

Below are the eight patterns as they were taught, with what landed for me in the room and how we’ve started to apply them.

⸻

1. Intentionally produce counter-culture (“belonging is a superpower”)

The facilitators were explicit: if you want a decentralised group, you have to create a culture that makes it possible. That means designing for belonging—rituals, language, onboarding, and signals that say “you’re allowed to act here”.

In the workshop, this showed up in small things: rounds where everyone spoke early, norms that prioritised listening over performance, and an assumption that newcomers could contribute meaningfully on day one. It felt engineered, not accidental.

What we took away is that “culture” isn’t a backdrop. It’s an active system. If you don’t build it, the default culture (often hierarchical, often exclusionary) fills the gap.

⸻

2. Systematically distribute care labour (stewards and stewardees)

Care work—checking in, following up, smoothing conflict—keeps groups alive, but it’s usually invisible and unevenly carried. The Loomio model pairs stewards with stewardees, making care a role with structure, not an afterthought.

In the session, we mapped who currently holds care in our own group. The pattern was predictable: a small number of people doing most of it. Naming that out loud shifted the conversation from “people should step up” to “the system needs to change”.

The steward/stewardee framing gives you a lightweight mechanism to distribute that load. It also legitimises care as work, which matters if you want it to be sustained.

⸻

3. Make explicit norms and boundaries

“Unwritten rules are just rules some people don’t know yet.” That line stuck.

The facilitators pushed us to externalise norms—how decisions are made, how conflict is handled, what’s in scope and what isn’t. Not as a static document, but as a living set of agreements that can be revised.

We did a quick exercise where each group wrote down three norms we assume everyone knows. Then we swapped lists. The gaps were obvious. What felt “obvious” internally wasn’t obvious at all.

The practical shift is to treat norms like infrastructure: visible, versioned, and easy to reference.

⸻

4. Keep talking about power

Decentralisation doesn’t eliminate power; it redistributes it. If you stop naming power, it recentralises quietly.

This pattern was less comfortable, which is probably the point. We were asked to identify where power actually sits in our groups—who sets agendas, who has context, who controls access to tools or information. The answers weren’t always aligned with formal roles.

The facilitators modelled a simple discipline: revisit power regularly, not just when something breaks. That normalises the conversation and reduces the friction of raising it.

⸻

5. Make decisions asynchronously

If decisions only happen in meetings, you privilege the people who can attend and perform in that format. Asynchronous decision-making widens participation and gives people time to think.

The Loomio approach structures this: proposals, time-bound input, clear decision rules. In the workshop, we ran a short async-style decision in the room—draft a proposal, gather reactions, iterate, then decide. Even compressed, it produced a more considered outcome than a typical “any objections?” moment.

The key is discipline. Async only works if people know when to engage, how to respond, and when a decision closes.

⸻

6. Agree how you use tech (the “holy trinity”: realtime / async / static)

Most groups accumulate tools without agreement. The result is fragmentation: decisions in chat, context in documents, history lost in threads.

The “holy trinity” frames three modes:

	•	Realtime (e.g. chat, meetings) for rapid exchange
	•	Async (e.g. forums, decision tools) for considered input and decisions
	•	Static (e.g. docs, wikis) for durable knowledge

The pattern is to agree what each mode is for—and stick to it. In the workshop, we mapped our current tools against these modes. The overlaps and gaps were obvious.

Our immediate change was simple: stop making decisions in chat. Use async spaces for decisions, and static spaces for the final record.

⸻

7. Use rhythm to cut information overload

Information overload isn’t just volume; it’s unpredictability. Rhythm—regular cycles of activity—helps people know when to pay attention.

The facilitators shared examples: weekly check-ins, monthly retros, quarterly planning. Not as meetings for their own sake, but as anchors that structure the flow of work.

We tested this by sketching a basic cadence for our group. Even a rough rhythm reduced the sense of constant interruption. It created “edges” around work, which is what people need to manage attention.

The insight here is that rhythm is a coordination tool. It reduces cognitive load without centralising control.

⸻

8. Generate new patterns together

The final pattern is meta: don’t just adopt patterns—create them. Decentralised groups need the capability to evolve their own practices.

The workshop itself modelled this. After introducing the patterns, the facilitators had us adapt them to our contexts, then share back what we’d changed. Some groups merged patterns; others added constraints or rituals.

What mattered wasn’t fidelity to the original model, but the act of collective design. That’s what builds ownership and keeps the system adaptable.

⸻

What changed for us

The workshop didn’t give us a finished operating model. It gave us a set of levers.

We left with a few concrete shifts:

	•	Care is now explicit. We’ve started pairing stewards and stewardees, with a simple expectation of regular check-ins.
	•	Decisions have a home. We’re moving proposals into an async space with clear timelines, and documenting outcomes in a static location.
	•	Norms are visible. We’ve drafted a short set of working agreements and put them somewhere everyone can find (and edit).
	•	We’re naming power earlier. Not perfectly, but more often, and with less hesitation.
	•	We’re testing a rhythm. A lightweight weekly and monthly cadence to stabilise attention.

None of this is dramatic. That’s the point. Decentralisation is built from small, repeatable practices.

Friction and limits

A few constraints are worth naming.

	•	Discipline is required. Async decision-making and tool agreements only work if people follow them. Without that, things drift back to the easiest path (usually chat).
	•	Time to adopt. Making norms explicit and redistributing care takes effort upfront. It can feel slower before it feels better.
	•	Power doesn’t disappear. Even with good patterns, informal power persists. The work of noticing and rebalancing is ongoing.

The facilitators didn’t present these as problems to solve once. They’re conditions to manage continuously.

Closing reflection

What stayed with me most is the framing of decentralisation as a design problem, not a personality trait. You don’t get it by hiring the right people or declaring flatness. You get it by building systems—cultural, social, and technical—that make distributed action viable.

“Belonging is a superpower” sounded soft at first. By the end, it felt operational. If people feel they belong, they act. If they act, work distributes. If work distributes, hierarchy loosens.

That’s the loop these patterns are trying to sustain.
