**Rewrite**

Walk through a forest after rain and the eye goes up first: wet bark, leaves catching light, birds working the branches. Most of the feeding, though, is happening under the path, in soil threaded with wet litter, where root tips are wrapped or entered by fungal threads thinner than a hair.

Mycorrhiza means "fungus-root". In this partnership, a fungus colonises a plant root and sends hyphae outward into the soil. The plant pays with carbon compounds made in photosynthesis. The fungus brings back nutrients that roots have trouble reaching, especially phosphorus along with nitrogen. Water and trace elements can move through the same route. More than 80% of plant species form some kind of mycorrhizal association, so this is ordinary plant life, not an odd forest trick.

The bargain works because fungi can search soil differently from roots. A root is costly plant tissue. A hypha can pass through small pores and bridge tiny gaps; it can also grow into leaf litter while nutrients are being released. Ectomycorrhizal fungi, common with pines, oaks and eucalypts, form a sheath around young root tips and grow between root cells. Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi go inside root cells and make branched exchange sites. The plumbing differs. The trade is the same: plant carbon one way, soil resources the other.

That trade shifts with circumstance. A tree in poor soil may spend heavily on fungal partners. A tree growing where nitrogen is plentiful may spend less, or choose different fungi. The fungi vary too. Some are good at scavenging inorganic phosphorus. Some can help release nitrogen from organic matter. Recent reviews of ectomycorrhizal forests make the nitrogen story look especially uneven, because transfer depends on the fungal species and on the chemical form of nitrogen in the soil. It is better to picture a market than a pipe.

The phrase "wood-wide web" grew out of this real biology, then ran ahead of the evidence. A common mycorrhizal network means a physical fungal link between the roots of two or more plants. Such links can move materials, at least in some settings. The hard part is proving what happened in a living forest. Carbon can leak from roots into soil. Microbes can consume it and release it again. Water can carry dissolved compounds. Plants also respond to airborne cues. Separating those routes from transfer through fungal hyphae is difficult work.

A 2023 review in Nature Ecology & Evolution pushed back on several public claims about these networks in forests. The authors argued that field evidence is still thin for claims that the networks are widespread, that they reliably help seedlings, or that mature trees preferentially supply their offspring. The review did not make mycorrhizal networks disappear. It made the story less tidy.

The classic experiment remains worth taking seriously. Suzanne Simard and her team ran a 1997 study in British Columbia. They used carbon isotopes to trace movement between paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings associated with ectomycorrhizal fungi. They found carbon transfer in both directions, with a net gain by Douglas-fir under the conditions they studied. That result mattered because it showed that neighbouring plants could be linked below ground by more than proximity.

It did not prove the nursery version of the story, where an old tree knowingly feeds a seedling. Carbon transfer in an experiment is one claim. A survival benefit in mature forest is another. Later reviews have found mixed results: network access can help seedlings in some cases and harm them in others; sometimes there is no measurable effect. A seedling may gain access to water or nutrients. It may also lose carbon to fungi or face stronger underground competition. Forest ecology is often more transactional than the popular version allows.

The communication story needs the same restraint. Plants detect chemical cues and alter their physiology. Leaves attacked by insects can change their defensive chemistry, and nearby plants may respond when neighbours are damaged. Some experiments suggest mycorrhizal networks can carry stress-related signals. One study reported carbon and defence-related signalling from damaged Douglas-fir to nearby ponderosa pine through ectomycorrhizal networks. Calling that a conversation gives the process a mind it does not have. A biochemical alarm is closer: a change in one organism alters the chemical setting for another.

The fungus is also an actor, not a cable. It needs carbon and has its own reproductive interests. It may send nutrients toward hosts that pay more carbon, grow into nutrient-rich patches, or reduce exchange when conditions change. A relationship that helps one host can drain another. The same network that appears to connect plants may also let the fungus move resources around its own body.

These fungi change the soil itself. Hyphae bind particles into aggregates and move nutrients over small distances. Dead fungal tissue can become part of longer-lived soil organic matter, especially when microbial remains bind to minerals. Still, mycorrhizal fungi are not simple carbon vaults. They receive plant carbon and build tissue from it. They also respire carbon dioxide. Some stimulate decomposition while mining organic matter for nutrients. Forest carbon studies now link fungal community composition with soil carbon patterns, which is useful precisely because the direction of the effect varies.

In a conifer forest on nutrient-poor soil, a large share of photosynthetic carbon may go below ground because nutrients are the limiting problem. Ectomycorrhizal fungi wrap root tips and push hyphae into soil. Enzymes or organic acids help them reach nitrogen and phosphorus that the tree cannot easily get alone. The mushrooms after rain are only fruiting bodies. Most of the organism is the mycelium in roots and soil. Soil compaction and clear-felling can change the living conditions for that mycelium fast.

Regeneration after disturbance also depends on what is already underground. Seedlings germinate into soil occupied by roots and fungi, with bacteria working through decaying plant matter nearby. Existing fungi may colonise young roots quickly and help some trees establish. The same fungal community may favour one tree species over another. Planting stems is only the visible part of recovery.

This matters for climate policy and conservation, but it should be handled carefully. Mycorrhizal fungi affect plant productivity and soil carbon cycling at large scales. A 2025 Nature study mapping global mycorrhizal fungal richness reported broad underground networks and found that many fungal biodiversity hotspots fall outside protected areas. That is a reason to include below-ground biodiversity in forest policy. It is not a shortcut climate fix.

The most accurate version sits between hype and dismissal. Mycorrhizal fungi help plants feed. They alter competition and seedling establishment. In some cases they link plants in ways that let materials or signals move. The evidence becomes weaker when those facts are turned into a sentimental story about wise parent trees and grateful young ones.

The forest below the path is a working system, full of appetite and competition, with decay feeding both. Its chemistry is hard to measure and its networks keep changing. That makes the reality less neat than the metaphor, and much more interesting.
