First men, original sins

Faith, frontier, and the moral imagination of American spaceflight

There is a familiar way to tell the story of the American space program: a sequence of technical milestones underwritten by Cold War urgency, culminating in the controlled triumph of Apollo 11 Moon Landing. The story moves from fear to mastery, from Sputnik to the Sea of Tranquillity, and it ends—implicitly—with a kind of secular apotheosis. Humanity leaves the earth, plants a flag, and returns with samples and images that seem to close the gap between myth and machine.

Yet this account is incomplete. It omits the dense field of meaning that surrounded these events as they unfolded: the language of calling and destiny, the moral vocabulary of sacrifice and risk, the persistent intuition that to leave the earth was not merely to extend human capability but to trespass into a domain long reserved for the divine. Three works—To Touch the Face of God by Kendrick Oliver, Destined for the Stars by Catherine L. Newell, and First Man directed by Damien Chazelle—offer a more complex interpretive frame. Together, they show that American spaceflight was never merely technological. It was, from the outset, entangled with religious imagination, haunted by ideas of sin and transgression, and sustained by a frontier mythology that recast exploration as both redemption and repetition.

What emerges is not a coherent theology of spaceflight but a set of overlapping, sometimes contradictory narratives: space as sacred frontier, as profane extension of earthly ambition, and as a site where the moral limits of human aspiration are tested.

⸻

The sacred and the profane

Oliver’s central claim is deceptively simple: that religion was not peripheral to the American space program but constitutive of its meaning. His title, To Touch the Face of God, taken from a line often (mis)attributed to pilots and later associated with astronauts, captures the ambiguity at the heart of the enterprise. Is spaceflight an act of reverence or of hubris? Does it bring humanity closer to the divine, or does it risk collapsing the distinction altogether?

Oliver tracks how religious language permeated NASA’s culture and public representation. Astronauts were framed not only as test pilots but as exemplars of moral character—disciplined, self-sacrificing, and oriented towards a purpose that exceeded individual ambition. This framing mattered. In a period defined by existential threat, the figure of the astronaut provided a counter-image: not the anonymous bureaucrat of nuclear deterrence, but the visible, almost liturgical figure of the explorer who risked death in the pursuit of knowledge.

Public rituals reinforced this framing. The reading from Genesis during Apollo 8 is a canonical example. As the spacecraft circled the Moon, the astronauts broadcast the opening lines of scripture to a global audience. Oliver notes the careful calibration of this moment: it was not a declaration of doctrine but an invocation of shared cultural language. The effect was nonetheless profound. The Moon—long a symbol in religious and poetic traditions—was recast as both object of scientific inquiry and stage for a quasi-liturgical performance.

Yet Oliver resists any simple reading of this as evidence of a unified “space religion”. Instead, he emphasises tension. The same programme that staged moments of reverence also operated within a technocratic framework that prioritised control, calculation, and efficiency. The sacred and the profane were not reconciled; they coexisted uneasily.

This tension becomes more acute when framed in terms of sin. If spaceflight is imagined as a reaching towards the divine, it risks repeating the archetypal narrative of overreach—the desire to transcend human limits. Oliver gestures towards this without fully resolving it. The language of transcendence, he suggests, always carries the shadow of transgression.

⸻

Destiny and its discontents

If Oliver’s account is attentive to the ambiguity of religious language, Newell’s is more explicitly concerned with its forward projection. Destined for the Stars examines how religious imagination shaped not only the interpretation of early spaceflight but the expectations attached to its future.

Newell argues that American space discourse drew heavily on eschatological motifs—ideas about ultimate destiny, fulfilment, and transformation. Space was not merely a new domain to explore; it was imagined as the next stage in a broader narrative of human development. This narrative was often framed in explicitly religious terms. Space colonisation became, in some accounts, a form of salvation: a way to transcend the limitations of earth, to escape ecological or geopolitical catastrophe, and to realise a latent potential embedded in human nature.

This language intersects with frontier mythology. The American frontier has long been imagined as a space of renewal—a place where old constraints fall away and new identities can be forged. Newell shows how this mythology was transposed into a cosmic register. The “final frontier” is not simply the last in a series of geographic expansions; it is a reactivation of a familiar narrative in a new context.

There is, however, a structural problem with this transposition. The frontier myth depends on a particular moral framing: expansion is justified because it is assumed to bring progress, order, and ultimately moral improvement. When applied to space, this assumption becomes harder to sustain. There are no indigenous populations on the Moon to displace, but the logic of expansion remains. The question is not who is harmed, but whether the underlying impulse—to extend control indefinitely—is itself subject to critique.

Newell is attentive to this tension. She notes that alongside narratives of destiny, there were persistent counter-narratives that framed spaceflight as ethically ambiguous or even dangerous. Religious critiques often focused on the risk of idolatry: the elevation of technological achievement to a status that displaces or distorts traditional understandings of the divine.

In this sense, spaceflight becomes a site where competing theologies of progress collide. One presents expansion as fulfilment; the other warns that the desire to transcend limits may itself be a form of moral failure.

⸻

Silence, grief, and the limits of transcendence

Where Oliver and Newell work primarily through historical and textual analysis, First Man offers a different entry point. Chazelle’s film, centred on Neil Armstrong, is notable for what it withholds. It avoids overt theological framing and largely sidesteps the triumphant rhetoric associated with the space race. Instead, it presents spaceflight as an extension of a more intimate narrative: the attempt to live with loss.

Armstrong’s journey in the film is structured around the death of his daughter. This loss is not resolved through the mission; it is carried into it. The Moon landing, in this context, is not a moment of transcendence in the usual sense. It is quieter, more ambiguous. The famous images are reframed through a lens of isolation. The lunar surface is not presented as a site of conquest but as an environment of radical distance—silent, indifferent, and resistant to projection.

This reframing has implications for how the film engages with religious imagination. Rather than staging explicit moments of reverence, it allows the viewer to confront the limits of meaning. The question is not whether space brings humanity closer to the divine, but whether such a question can be meaningfully posed in a context defined by absence.

There is a subtle but important shift here. In Oliver’s account, religious language provides a framework for interpreting spaceflight. In Newell’s, it shapes expectations about its future. In First Man, that framework begins to dissolve. The film suggests that the experience of space may resist the categories used to describe it. The sacred/profane distinction becomes less stable, not because it is resolved, but because it is rendered insufficient.

The film’s final sequence, often read as symbolic, underscores this point. Armstrong leaves a personal token on the lunar surface—a gesture that has been variously interpreted as mourning, closure, or offering. The act is private, almost hidden within the broader narrative. It does not resolve the tension between transcendence and limitation; it simply marks it.

⸻

Frontier myth and original sin

Across these works, the concept of the frontier operates as a unifying thread. It provides a narrative structure through which spaceflight is made intelligible: exploration leads to discovery, which leads to expansion, which leads to transformation. This structure is deeply embedded in American cultural history. It is also, as historians have long noted, morally ambivalent.

The frontier myth carries within it a logic of repetition. Each new frontier is framed as an opportunity to correct the failures of the previous one—to build a more just, more equitable, more enlightened society. At the same time, the mechanisms of expansion often reproduce the very dynamics they are meant to overcome.

When applied to space, this dynamic becomes abstract but no less significant. The absence of immediate ethical conflict does not eliminate the underlying question: what is the moral status of expansion itself? Is the drive to extend human presence indefinitely a form of fulfilment, or does it risk replicating a pattern of overreach that has historically been associated with harm?

This is where the language of sin becomes analytically useful. Not in a narrowly doctrinal sense, but as a way of naming the tension between aspiration and limitation. The desire to explore, to know, to extend—these are not inherently problematic. They are, in many accounts, constitutive of what it means to be human. The problem arises when these desires are framed as limitless, when the possibility of constraint is excluded from the narrative.

Oliver’s attention to the coexistence of sacred and profane language highlights this tension. Newell’s analysis of destiny narratives shows how it is projected into the future. First Man renders it at the level of individual experience. Together, they suggest that the space program can be read as a site where the moral imagination of mid-century America is both expressed and tested.

⸻

After Apollo

The period covered by these works—roughly from the late 1950s to the mid-1970s—has a particular coherence. It begins with uncertainty and ends with a sense of closure. The Moon landings mark both a culmination and a turning point. The narrative of ascent reaches its peak, and then, abruptly, it stalls.

What follows is less often framed in religious terms. The language of destiny persists, particularly in discussions of Mars and beyond, but it is attenuated. The sense of collective purpose that characterised the early space race is harder to sustain. The frontier remains, but its moral clarity is diminished.

This shift raises a question that these works collectively invite but do not fully answer: what happens to the religious imagination of spaceflight when the initial moment of transcendence has passed? If the Moon landing represents a kind of fulfilment, what follows is a period of recalibration. The categories that once structured the narrative—sacred and profane, destiny and restraint—are still available, but they no longer map as neatly onto the experience of space exploration.

In this sense, the early space program can be understood as a bounded episode: a moment in which technological capability, political urgency, and religious imagination aligned in a particular way. The challenge, for subsequent generations, is to interpret that moment without collapsing its complexity into a single narrative.

⸻

Conclusion

Taken together, these works resist the reduction of the American space program to either triumphalism or critique. They show that spaceflight was, and remains, a site of interpretive contestation. It invites readings that emphasise transcendence and those that foreground limitation. It can be framed as fulfilment of a destiny or as repetition of a pattern of overreach.

The language of religion—of sacred and profane, of sin and redemption—provides one way of navigating this terrain. It does not resolve the tensions it names, but it makes them visible. The frontier myth, similarly, offers a narrative structure that both illuminates and obscures. It explains why spaceflight was compelling; it also risks importing assumptions that are not easily justified.

First Man complicates both frameworks by attending to the experiential dimension of spaceflight—the silence, the isolation, the persistence of grief. It suggests that whatever meanings are projected onto space, they are mediated by the limits of human experience.

The result is a more restrained, but also more precise, account of what the space program represents. Not a simple story of progress or hubris, but a moment in which a particular culture confronted the boundaries of its own imagination—and, in doing so, revealed both its aspirations and its constraints.
