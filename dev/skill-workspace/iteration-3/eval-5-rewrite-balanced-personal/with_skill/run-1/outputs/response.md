**Rewrite**

I was twenty-four when I realised my parents were not perfect. It was not dramatic. No door slammed, no family secret came out of a drawer, no confession arrived across the kitchen table under a bad light. It happened on an ordinary Tuesday evening, with my father in the driveway holding a garden hose that had split near the nozzle, and my mother inside at the dining table, crying over an electricity bill.

The house looked as it always had: the brick path, the agapanthus along the fence, the bowl by the front door where keys and coins gathered with old receipts. Nothing in the room had announced a change. The furniture was where it belonged, dinner had not yet started, and the lemon tree outside was still getting watered badly. That was part of what unsettled me. The setting had not altered. My sight had.

I had come home for dinner because I was tired, underfed, and pretending not to be. My twenties had begun with the false confidence of someone who mistakes movement for direction. I had a job with an email signature, a rent payment that frightened me every fortnight, and firm opinions about how life should be managed. I was old enough to see my parents' habits as limitations, but not old enough to understand the work that had made my certainty possible.

Until then, I had carried an image of them that was almost architectural. They were the frame around everything. My father knew where the torch was, how to reverse a trailer, and what to do when the hot water went cold. My mother remembered birthdays, medical histories, and the names of teachers I had not thought about in years. Between them, they seemed to possess the operating manual for being alive.

That evening, the manual failed in two small places at once.

The hose burst while my father was watering the lemon tree. I heard the sound before I saw it, a frantic metallic hiss, followed by his short exhale of irritation. He turned the tap off too hard and the handle kept spinning loosely in his hand. For a second he just stared at it, as if this cheap piece of hardware had chosen him personally.

Inside, my mother had opened the electricity bill and gone quiet in a way that made the room change temperature. She smoothed the paper with both hands, folded it, opened it again, and pressed her fingers to her mouth. When she looked up, she seemed exposed rather than older. The composure I associated with her had gone. She looked like someone who had reached the end of a calculation and found there was still more to pay.

My first reaction was annoyance. It shames me now, but it is true. I wanted them to handle it. I wanted the hose fixed and the bill absorbed so dinner could begin and the world could return to its proper scale. I had come home to be looked after; instead, I found two people standing inside their own limits.

"Is it that bad?" I asked.

My mother said, "It's fine."

It was the first lie I recognised as reflex rather than deception. She said it quickly, with the competence of long practice. Fine meant she had no answer yet. It meant she was trying to protect me from an anxiety I was already old enough to see. It meant she did not know how to say: yes, this is difficult.

My father came in from the driveway with wet cuffs and a look of forced calm. He read the bill and made a small sound through his nose. Then he said something about calling them tomorrow. My mother said she had already called. He asked what they said. She told him they had said what they always said.

There was no argument, which almost made it worse. I had grown up thinking the great dangers were noise, rupture, visible unhappiness. I did not yet understand the quieter forms of strain: the spreadsheet, the postponed appointment, the envelope left unopened for two days, the adult conversation conducted in low voices after children have gone to bed.

I watched them speak past each other while each tried not to add to the other's fear. My father wanted a practical problem because practical problems gave him a role. My mother wanted acknowledgement because, for her, being seen was a kind of relief. Neither could give the other what they needed in that moment. They loved each other. They also lacked range.

That was the crack.

For years I had mistaken my parents' strengths for completeness. My father's competence had covered his terror of helplessness. My mother's care had covered her resentment at having to notice everything. I had thought their flaws were irritations they could fix if they tried harder: his stubbornness, her worrying, his silence, her sharpness when tired. But at that dining table, with the bill between them and water still dripping from my father's trouser hem, I saw something harder to dismiss. Their flaws were not decorative faults on otherwise finished people. They were part of the machinery: old defences, adaptations, bargains made so long ago they had become character.

I did not become kinder immediately. Realisation rarely improves us on contact. For a while I think it made me worse. There is a particular cruelty available to people in their twenties, especially those who have just learned enough language to diagnose everyone else. I could name family dynamics, emotional avoidance, gendered labour. Because I could see the patterns, I felt superior to them. I confused analysis with wisdom.

I wanted my parents to have read the books I had read and to have had the conversations I was having. I wanted them fluent in a language they had never been taught. I wanted my father to say, "I feel frightened when I cannot fix things." I wanted my mother to say, "I am exhausted by being the person who anticipates consequences." Instead, he went back outside with a wrench, and she started peeling potatoes with more force than necessary.

That was how they knew how to continue.

Dinner was late. The potatoes were undercooked in the centre, and nobody mentioned the bill again. My father made a joke about the hose. My mother gave him a look that was not quite amusement and not quite forgiveness. I remember watching them across the table and feeling a grief I could not explain. Nothing terrible had happened, yet something had been lost.

What I lost was not love. It was exemption.

Children exempt their parents from personhood because they need them to be weatherproof. Even when we rebel against them, we often rebel against monuments. We accuse them of being controlling, embarrassing, old-fashioned, unreasonable, without quite grasping that these are not fixed properties. They are behaviours shaped by fear, fatigue, class, disappointment, marriage, money, work, biology, and the small humiliations of adult life.

To see a parent as imperfect is one thing. To see them as contingent is another. They could have been otherwise. They were made, not issued.

After that night, memories rearranged themselves. My father sitting in the car after work before coming inside was no longer simply odd; it was a man taking three minutes of silence because the house would need him the second he opened the door. My mother checking the oven twice before we left for holidays was not just anxiety. It was the burden of being blamed, or blaming herself, if something went wrong. Their caution, their rules, their occasional emotional illiteracy all had roots.

This did not absolve them of everything. That distinction mattered then, and matters more now. Understanding is not the same as excusing. Some wounds remain wounds even after you learn who held the knife and why their hand was shaking. But the moral geometry of my family changed. There were fewer clean lines, less prosecution, and more evidence.

In the years that followed, I noticed other things. My father sometimes did not know the answer. My mother sometimes made herself indispensable and then resented being needed. Both of them misremembered events in ways that protected them. Both gave advice shaped more by fear than by truth. They loved me imperfectly, which is to say they loved me with the instruments they had.

That night in my twenties did not make me stop needing them. It made needing them more complicated. I could no longer collapse into the old fantasy that somewhere, behind the ordinary surface of life, my parents were still in charge. The adults were not elsewhere. There was no committee in reserve. No one was coming with the complete manual.

There was only us, improvising.

For a while, that frightened me. Later, it became a form of mercy. If my parents were not perfect, perhaps I did not have to become perfect before I could be decent. Maybe adulthood was not a threshold crossed by competent people, but a room everyone enters unfinished. A person learns where the torch is, forgets something they promised to remember, pays the bill late, says the wrong thing, mends what can be mended and carries what cannot.

I think often of my father in the driveway, holding the broken hose as water ran across the concrete. I think of my mother at the table, pressing the bill flat with both hands. At twenty-four, I thought I was witnessing their failure. Now I think I was seeing something closer to the truth: two people, tired and limited, trying to keep the house running anyway.

That was the moment they became smaller. It was also the moment they became real.
