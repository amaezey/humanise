After rain, a forest pulls attention upward: leaves flash against dark trunks while birds move through the canopy. A lot of the work, though, is under the path. Below the litter layer, fungal filaments wrap roots and pass through soil spaces too small for roots to enter. Those fungi help many forests feed themselves; they also shape recovery after stress, competition for resources, and the movement of carbon through soil.

The association is mycorrhiza, literally fungus-root. In it, a fungus colonises a plant root and sends microscopic filaments, called hyphae, out into the surrounding soil. The plant pays with carbon compounds made by photosynthesis. The fungus returns nutrients from places the roots would reach poorly on their own. The most important returns are phosphorus and nitrogen. It can also help with water or trace elements. More than 80 percent of plant species form these relationships. In some soils, mycorrhizal fungi account for a large share of the living microbial biomass.

For a tree, the arrangement changes the economics of feeding. Roots are thick, costly organs. Hyphae are cheaper and finer. A root hair samples the soil close to it, while a fungal filament can slip into small pores and grow through decomposing litter or mineral grains. The tree gains a larger absorbing surface and a different chemistry at its root edge. Ectomycorrhizal fungi are common on many forest trees; they sheath root tips and grow between root cells. Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi occur in a wide range of plants. They enter root cells and form small branched exchange structures. The forms differ, but the trade is recognisable: plant carbon goes to the fungus, and soil resources come back.

That trade shifts with conditions. Trees vary their investment, and fungi vary in what they offer. One partner may be good at scavenging inorganic phosphorus; another may help release nitrogen from organic matter. Reviews of ectomycorrhizal forests have found that nitrogen transfer depends strongly on the fungal species and the soil. The chemical form of the nitrogen also matters. A tree in poor soil may spend heavily on fungal partners. A tree in fertilised ground may spend less, because the return has changed.

The phrase wood-wide web grew out of this area of research, and it has done both useful and messy work. A common mycorrhizal network means a physical fungal connection between the roots of two or more plants. In principle, such a connection can move carbon or nutrients. It may also move water or chemical signals. Demonstrating that movement in an intact forest is hard. Researchers have to rule out other routes. Compounds can leak from roots or be recycled by microbes. They can move in water or travel as airborne signals. A 2023 review in Nature Ecology & Evolution argued that several public claims about these networks in forests outrun the field evidence. The review challenged claims that such networks are widespread. It also questioned whether they reliably improve seedling performance. The same review found weak evidence for claims that mature trees preferentially feed their own offspring.

The networks are real. The problem is the story often placed on top of them. A famous 1997 experiment by Suzanne Simard and colleagues traced carbon isotopes between paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings in British Columbia. The seedlings were associated with ectomycorrhizal fungi. The researchers found carbon moving in both directions between the species, with Douglas-fir gaining carbon overall under those conditions. The result mattered because it showed that plants could be linked below ground in more complicated ways than simple one-tree competition.

The careful conclusion is narrower. Carbon can move between plants in some experimental settings, and fungal pathways may be involved. That finding does not prove that an old tree is feeding a seedling by intention, or that transfer improves survival across forests generally. Later reviews have found mixed seedling results. Access to a network may help in one setting, hurt in another, or make little measurable difference. In ecological terms, connection is a condition, not a virtue.

Claims about communication need the same restraint. Plants detect chemical cues and alter their physiology after attack. Neighbouring plants can respond when damage changes the chemical environment around them. Some experiments suggest that mycorrhizal networks can carry stress-related signals. One study reported that damaged Douglas-fir transferred carbon and defence-associated signals to neighbouring ponderosa pine through ectomycorrhizal networks. That is biochemical signalling, not language. There is no evidence for intention or conscious warning.

The fungus also deserves better than the role of cable. It is a living partner with its own metabolism and evolutionary interests. It can allocate nutrients toward hosts that supply more carbon. It can proliferate in nutrient-rich patches or change exchange rates as conditions change. Some relationships are mutualistic. Others drift toward parasitism when one partner gives more than it receives. A fungus that helps one tree acquire nitrogen may also compete with another organism for the same pool of nitrogen.

These fungal systems affect soil structure as well as plant nutrition. Hyphae bind particles into aggregates and move resources over small distances. When they die, fungal remains can become part of longer-lived soil organic matter, especially when microbial residues bind to minerals. Still, mycorrhizal fungi are not simple carbon vaults. They receive plant carbon and build tissue. They respire carbon dioxide. They interact with decomposers and sometimes stimulate decomposition while mining organic matter for nutrients. Recent work links fungal community composition to forest soil carbon patterns, but different fungi can push carbon cycling in different directions.

A nutrient-poor conifer forest makes the point concrete. If nitrogen or phosphorus limits growth, trees may send a large share of photosynthetic carbon below ground. Ectomycorrhizal fungi sheath root tips and extend hyphae into soil. Enzymes or organic acids let them reach nutrient forms the tree cannot easily use alone. The mushrooms that appear after rain are fruiting bodies. Most of the organism is mycelium in roots and soil. Soil compaction can change the temperature and moisture around that mycelium quickly. So can topsoil loss or clear-felling.

Regeneration after disturbance also depends on this underground community. Seedlings germinate into soil already occupied by fungi and roots. Bacteria and decaying organic matter are there too. Existing mycorrhizal fungi can help young trees establish by colonising their roots quickly. In other places, the resident fungal community favours some tree species over others and changes which seedlings survive. Planting stems is only part of forest recovery; the soil community helps decide whether those stems become a forest.

The climate and conservation stakes are straightforward. Mycorrhizal fungi shape plant productivity and nutrient cycling. They also affect soil carbon dynamics at large scales. A 2025 Nature study mapping global mycorrhizal fungal richness reported extensive underground networks and found that many fungal biodiversity hotspots fall outside protected areas. That does not turn fungi into an easy climate fix. It does mean forest policy that ignores below-ground biodiversity is leaving out part of the system it aims to manage.

The most accurate account sits away from the popular myth. Mycorrhizal fungi are ancient, consequential, and difficult to measure in living forests. These fungi help trees acquire nutrients and influence competition. Their effects also reach seedling establishment and soil carbon. In some cases, they connect plants in ways that let materials or signals move between them.

The forest under the forest is infrastructure and exchange. It is also digestion and conflict. Its chemistry is more important than its sentiment. Its networks are patchy and changing. That makes the wood-wide web metaphor too tidy, but the underlying biology is still impressive: a forest is a layered community in which fungi do much of the work beyond the reach of light.
