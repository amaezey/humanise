# The forest beneath the forest

Walk through a forest after rain and it is tempting to think the important action is above you: leaves catching light, trunks widening year by year, birds moving between branches. But much of the forest’s metabolism is below the path. Under the litter layer, roots are threaded through with fungal filaments so fine that a teaspoon of soil can hold an intricate living mesh. These fungi are not decoration on tree roots. They are part of how forests feed, recover, compete and store carbon.

The relationship is called mycorrhiza, from the Greek for “fungus-root”. In a mycorrhizal association, a fungus colonises a plant’s root system and extends outward into the soil as microscopic filaments called hyphae. The plant supplies carbon-rich compounds made through photosynthesis. The fungus, in return, helps the plant acquire nutrients, especially phosphorus and nitrogen, and often water and trace elements from beyond the reach of the roots themselves. Mycorrhizal fungi form nutritional symbioses with more than 80% of plant species, and their underground networks can represent a substantial share of living microbial biomass in soils.

For a tree, this is not a minor upgrade. Roots are thick, expensive structures compared with fungal hyphae. A root hair may explore the soil immediately around it; a fungal filament can slip into smaller pores, cross tiny gaps, and spread through patches of decomposing leaf litter or mineral soil. The fungus effectively gives the tree a larger, finer and more chemically versatile absorbing surface. Some ectomycorrhizal fungi, common on many pines, birches, oaks, beeches and eucalypts, form a sheath around root tips and grow between root cells. Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi, found across a very large range of plants, enter root cells and form tiny branched structures where exchange occurs. The architecture differs, but the underlying bargain is similar: carbon flows from plant to fungus; soil-derived resources flow from fungus to plant.

The bargain is also dynamic. Trees do not simply hand over sugar to any fungus in the neighbourhood. Fungi vary in what they can provide, where they forage, and how much carbon they demand. Some are better at scavenging inorganic phosphorus; others can help mobilise nitrogen from organic matter. Recent reviews of ectomycorrhizal forests emphasise that nitrogen transfer is highly variable, depending on fungal identity, soil conditions and the form in which nitrogen is held.   The result is less like a pipe and more like a shifting set of trade relationships. A tree growing in poor soil may invest heavily in fungal partners; a tree in fertilised or nitrogen-rich conditions may invest differently because the return on that carbon has changed.

This is where the popular phrase “wood-wide web” enters the story, and where accuracy matters. A common mycorrhizal network is not just “fungi in the soil”. It means a physical fungal connection linking the roots of two or more plants. Such connections can, in principle, move carbon, nutrients, water or chemical signals between plants. But proving that this happens in intact forests is difficult. Researchers have to separate transfer through fungal hyphae from other routes: roots leaking compounds into soil, microbes consuming and re-releasing them, water moving dissolved chemicals, or plants responding to airborne signals. A 2023 review in Nature Ecology & Evolution argued that several strong public claims about common mycorrhizal networks in forests, including that they are widespread, that they reliably improve seedling performance, and that mature trees preferentially send resources to offspring, are not yet supported by enough field evidence.

That does not make the networks imaginary. It means they are harder to demonstrate than the metaphor suggests. One of the classic experiments was published in 1997 by Suzanne Simard and colleagues. Working with paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings in British Columbia, the researchers used carbon isotopes to trace movement between trees associated with ectomycorrhizal fungi. They found bidirectional carbon transfer between the two species, with a net gain by Douglas-fir under the conditions studied.   The experiment became famous because it suggested that forest plants were not merely competing as isolated individuals. Their roots and fungi could be part of a shared below-ground system.

The more careful conclusion is narrower and more interesting. Carbon can move between plants in some experimental settings. Fungal pathways may contribute. But a detected transfer is not the same as proof that a mature tree is deliberately feeding a seedling, or that the transfer meaningfully changes survival in all forests. Later reviews have found mixed evidence on whether access to common mycorrhizal networks improves, harms or has no effect on seedlings. In some cases, being connected may give a seedling access to nutrients or water. In others, the seedling may lose carbon to fungi, face stronger below-ground competition, or gain no measurable advantage. Forest life is not a morality play about generosity. It is an ecological economy with uneven terms.

The “communication” claim needs the same discipline. Plants certainly detect and respond to chemical cues. When leaves are attacked, they can alter defensive chemistry; when neighbours are damaged, nearby plants may respond. Some experiments suggest that mycorrhizal networks can mediate stress-related signalling. In one study, damaged Douglas-fir was reported to transfer carbon and defence-related signals to neighbouring ponderosa pine through ectomycorrhizal networks.   But this is not conversation in the human sense. There is no evidence of intention, language or conscious warning. A more accurate analogy is a biochemical alarm system: changes in one organism can alter the chemical environment of connected organisms, which may then shift their own physiology.

Even the fungal partner should not be treated as a passive cable. The fungus is alive, metabolically active and under selection to acquire carbon and reproduce. It may allocate nutrients towards hosts that supply more carbon, proliferate in nutrient-rich patches, or reduce exchange when conditions change. Some mycorrhizal relationships look mutualistic; others drift towards parasitism when one partner receives less than it gives. The same fungus that helps one tree may compete with another for nitrogen. The same network that appears to connect plants may also allow fungi to manage their own resources across multiple hosts.

The physical structure of these networks changes how soil works. Hyphae bind particles into aggregates, move nutrients across small distances, and leave behind fungal residues when they die. Those residues can become part of longer-lived soil organic matter, especially when microbial remains bind to minerals. But mycorrhizal fungi do not simply “store carbon” in a straightforward way. They receive plant carbon, build tissue, respire carbon dioxide, interact with decomposer microbes and, in some cases, stimulate decomposition as they mine organic matter for nutrients. Recent forest carbon research has found strong links between fungal community composition and soil carbon patterns, but it also shows why simple claims are risky: different fungi can push carbon cycling in different directions.

A concrete example is a conifer forest growing on nutrient-poor soil. The trees may allocate a large share of photosynthetic carbon below ground because nutrients, not light, are the limiting constraint. Ectomycorrhizal fungi form sheaths around root tips, extend hyphae into the soil, and use enzymes and organic acids to access forms of nitrogen or phosphorus the tree cannot easily reach alone. The visible mushrooms that appear after rain are only the fruiting bodies. Most of the organism is the mycelium threaded through roots and soil. When forestry machinery compacts soil, when topsoil is removed, or when a clear-fell changes temperature and moisture, the living conditions for this mycelium can change abruptly.

Another example is forest regeneration after disturbance. Seedlings do not enter a neutral underground world. They germinate into soil already occupied by fungi, roots, bacteria and decaying organic matter. In some settings, existing mycorrhizal fungi can help young trees establish by rapidly colonising their roots. In others, the existing fungal community may favour some tree species over others, shaping which seedlings survive. This is one reason forest recovery is not just a matter of planting stems in the ground. The below-ground community can influence whether those stems become a functioning forest.

The story also matters for climate and conservation. Mycorrhizal fungi are involved in plant productivity, nutrient cycling and soil carbon dynamics at large scales. A 2025 Nature study mapping global mycorrhizal fungal richness reported that mycorrhizal fungi form extensive underground networks and that many biodiversity hotspots for these fungi fall outside protected areas.   That does not mean protecting fungi is a simple climate solution. It means forest policy that ignores below-ground biodiversity is missing part of the system it is trying to manage.

The best way to understand mycorrhizal networks is to abandon both extremes. They are not magical forest internet cables through which wise old trees send care packages to their children. Nor are they a debunked fantasy. Mycorrhizal fungi are real, ancient and ecologically consequential. They help trees acquire nutrients, alter competition, influence seedling establishment, build and break down soil carbon, and sometimes connect plants in ways that allow materials or signals to move between them.

The forest beneath the forest is therefore not a hidden utopia. It is infrastructure, marketplace, digestive system and battleground at once. Its exchanges are chemical rather than sentimental; its networks are patchy, changing and difficult to measure. That makes them less tidy than the “wood-wide web” metaphor, but more impressive. A forest is not just a collection of trees. It is a layered community of organisms negotiating matter and energy above and below ground, with fungi doing much of the work where light cannot reach.
