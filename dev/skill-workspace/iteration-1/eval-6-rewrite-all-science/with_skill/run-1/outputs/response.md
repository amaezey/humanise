**Rewrite**

After rain, a forest can trick the eye. Leaves shine, trunks darken, birds start up again. The busier part of the place may be under your boots. In the damp soil, tree roots run through mats of fungal threads, many of them far thinner than a root hair. Those threads help decide what trees can eat and which seedlings get a start. They also shape the path carbon takes through the ground.

The name for the partnership is mycorrhiza. It joins old words for fungus and root. In this association, a fungus colonises a plant root and sends hyphae out into soil. The plant pays with sugars made by photosynthesis. The fungus returns phosphorus and nitrogen, plus water or trace elements that the root system would struggle to reach alone. More than 80 percent of plant species form some kind of mycorrhizal partnership, so this is ordinary forest machinery rather than a rare trick.

For a tree, fungal filaments are cheap reach. Roots are bulky compared with hyphae. A hypha can enter small pores, pass through wet leaf litter, and work across bits of mineral soil where a root hair has little room.

Ectomycorrhizal fungi, common with pines and many broad-leaved trees, wrap root tips in a sheath and grow between root cells. Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi go inside root cells and make branched exchange structures. The plumbing differs. The deal stays recognisable: plant carbon goes one way; soil resources come back.

That deal changes with the soil. A tree does not gain the same return from every fungus. One partner may be good at scavenging inorganic phosphorus. Another may help release nitrogen from organic matter. In ectomycorrhizal forests, reviews find wide variation in nitrogen transfer. The answer depends on the fungal species and on the chemical form of the nitrogen. Poor soil can make fungal partners worth a large carbon payment. Fertilised soil can change the price.

This is the real territory behind the phrase "wood-wide web". A common mycorrhizal network means a physical fungal connection between the roots of two or more plants. Materials can travel along such links in some settings. The hard part is proving the route in a living forest. Carbon or chemical cues can move through leaking roots and soil water. Microbes can consume compounds and release them again. Plants can also respond to airborne signals. A 2023 review in Nature Ecology & Evolution warned that several popular forest-network claims have outrun the field evidence, especially claims about seedling benefits and mature trees feeding offspring.

The warning matters because the underlying biology is already interesting. Suzanne Simard and colleagues published a famous experiment in 1997 using paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings in British Columbia. Carbon isotope labels moved between seedlings associated with ectomycorrhizal fungi, with a net gain by Douglas-fir under the conditions tested. That experiment helped loosen the picture of trees as isolated competitors. It showed that roots and fungi could make a shared below-ground system.

A careful reading leaves room for wonder without turning the result into folklore. Carbon transfer has been detected. Fungal pathways can contribute. Those facts do not show that an old tree chooses to feed a seedling, or that the transfer improves survival across forests generally. Later reviews give a mixed picture. Network access may help a seedling in one setting, cost it carbon in another, or make little measurable difference.

Claims about communication need the same restraint. Plants respond to chemical cues. Damaged leaves can change their defensive chemistry, and neighbouring plants sometimes shift their own chemistry after nearby damage. Some experiments suggest that mycorrhizal networks may carry stress-related signals. One study reported carbon and defence-related signalling from damaged Douglas-fir to nearby ponderosa pine through ectomycorrhizal links. That is biochemical response, not speech. One organism's changed chemistry can alter another's physiology without intention or language.

The fungus has its own interests too. It uses carbon and faces selection to reproduce. It may send more nutrients toward hosts that supply more sugar. It may grow harder into nutrient-rich patches. It may reduce exchange when conditions shift.

A relationship that benefits both partners in one season can become lopsided in another. The same network that joins plants also gives the fungus a way to manage resources across hosts.

Hyphae also change the soil itself. They bind particles into aggregates and move nutrients over small distances. After death, they leave residues. Some residues become longer-lived soil organic matter when microbial remains attach to minerals.

Carbon storage, though, is messy. Mycorrhizal fungi receive plant carbon and build tissue. They respire carbon dioxide. They interact with decomposers. Sometimes they stimulate decay while mining organic matter for nutrients. Forest carbon studies now link fungal community composition with soil carbon patterns, but the direction varies by fungal group and site.

Picture a conifer forest on poor soil. Light may be plentiful while usable nitrogen or phosphorus is scarce. The trees send a large share of photosynthetic carbon below ground. Ectomycorrhizal fungi sheath root tips and push hyphae into the soil. They use enzymes or organic acids to reach compounds the tree cannot easily use by itself. The mushrooms after rain are fruiting bodies. Most of the organism is the mycelium in the soil. Machinery can compact that habitat. Topsoil removal can damage it. A clear-fell can change heat and moisture fast.

Seedlings enter this already occupied ground. Existing fungi can help young trees establish quickly by colonising fresh roots. They can also favour one tree species over another.

That is one reason forest recovery involves more than putting stems into dirt. The underground community helps determine whether planted stems become a functioning forest.

The conservation argument follows from that. Mycorrhizal fungi are tied to plant growth and to the cycling of nutrients and soil carbon. A 2025 Nature study mapping global mycorrhizal fungal richness reported many hotspots outside protected areas. Protecting fungi is not a simple climate lever, but forest policy that ignores below-ground biodiversity leaves out part of the system it claims to manage.

So the better story is plainer and stranger than the slogan. Mycorrhizal fungi help trees obtain nutrients. They alter competition and influence regeneration. They affect soil carbon. In some cases, they connect plants so that materials or cues move between them. The underground forest is infrastructure and appetite at once. Chemical exchanges happen in patches and are hard to measure. That makes the forest less tidy than the internet metaphor and more alive.
