# The forest beneath the forest

Walk through a forest after rain and it is tempting to think the important action is above you: leaves catching light, trunks widening year by year, birds moving between branches. But much of the forest’s metabolism is below the path. Under the litter layer, roots are threaded through with fungal filaments so fine that a teaspoon of soil can hold an intricate living mesh. These fungi are not decoration on tree roots. They are part of how forests feed, recover, compete and store carbon.

The relationship is called mycorrhiza, from the Greek for “fungus-root”. In a mycorrhizal association, a fungus colonises a plant’s root system and extends outward into the soil as microscopic filaments called hyphae. The plant supplies carbon-rich compounds made through photosynthesis. The fungus, in return, helps the plant acquire nutrients, especially phosphorus and nitrogen, and often water and trace elements from beyond the reach of the roots themselves. Mycorrhizal fungi form nutritional symbioses with more than 80% of plant species, and their underground networks can represent a substantial share of living microbial biomass in soils.

For a tree, this is not a minor upgrade. Roots are thick, expensive structures compared with fungal hyphae. A root hair may explore the soil immediately around it; a fungal filament can slip into smaller pores, cross tiny gaps, and spread through patches of decomposing leaf litter or mineral soil. The fungus effectively gives the tree a larger, finer and more chemically versatile absorbing surface. Some ectomycorrhizal fungi, common on many pines, birches, oaks, beeches and eucalypts, form a sheath around root tips and grow between root cells. Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi, found across a very large range of plants, enter root cells and form tiny branched structures where exchange occurs. The architecture differs, but the underlying bargain is similar: carbon flows from plant to fungus; soil-derived resources flow from fungus to plant.

The bargain is also dynamic. Trees do not simply hand over sugar to any fungus in the neighbourhood. Fungi vary in what they can provide, where they forage, and how much carbon they demand. Some are better at scavenging inorganic phosphorus; others can help mobilise nitrogen from organic matter. Recent reviews of ectomycorrhizal forests emphasise that nitrogen transfer is highly variable, depending on fungal identity, soil conditions and the form in which nitrogen is held.   The result is less like a pipe and more like a shifting set of trade relationships. A tree growing in poor soil may invest heavily in fungal partners; a tree in fertilised or nitrogen-rich conditions may invest differently because the return on that carbon has changed.

This is where the popular phrase “wood-wide web” enters the story, and where accuracy matters. A common mycorrhizal network is not just “fungi in the soil”. It means a physical fungal connection linking the roots of two or more plants. Such connections can, in principle, move carbon, nutrients, water or chemical signals between plants. But proving that this happens in intact forests is difficult. Researchers have to separate transfer through fungal hyphae from other routes: roots leaking compounds into soil, microbes consuming and re-releasing them, water moving dissolved chemicals, or plants responding to airborne signals. A 2023 review in Nature Ecology & Evolution argued that several strong public claims about common mycorrhizal networks in forests, including that they are widespread, that they reliably improve seedling performance, and that mature trees preferentially send resources to offspring, are not yet supported by enough field evidence.

That does not make the networks imaginary. It means they are harder to demonstrate than the metaphor suggests. One of the classic experiments was published in 1997 by Suzanne Simard and colleagues. Working with paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings in British Columbia, the researchers used carbon isotopes to trace movement between trees associated with ectomycorrhizal fungi. They found bidirectional carbon transfer between the two species, with a net gain by Douglas-fir under the conditions studied.   The experiment became famous because it suggested that forest plants were not merely competing as isolated individuals. Their roots and fungi could be part of a shared below-ground system.

The more careful conclusion is narrower and more interesting. Carbon can move between plants in some experimental settings. Fungal pathways may contribute. But a detected transfer is not the same as proof that a mature tree is deliberately feeding a seedling, or that the transfer meaningfully changes survival in all forests. Later reviews have found mixed evidence on whether access to common mycorrhizal networks improves, harms or has no effect on seedlings. In some cases, being connected may give a seedling access to nutrients or water. In others, the seedling may lose carbon to fungi, face stronger below-ground competition, or gain no measurable advantage. Forest life is not a morality play about generosity. It is an ecological economy with uneven terms.

The “communication” claim needs the same discipline. Plants certainly detect and respond to chemical cues. When leaves are attacked, they can alter defensive chemistry; when neighbours are damaged, nearby plants may respond. Some experiments suggest that mycorrhizal networks can mediate stress-related signalling. In one study, damaged Douglas-fir was reported to transfer carbon and defence-related signals to neighbouring ponderosa pine through ectomycorrhizal networks.   But this is not conversation in the human sense. There is no evidence of intention, language or conscious warning. A more accurate analogy is a biochemical alarm system: changes in one organism can alter the chemical environment of connected organisms, which may then shift their own physiology.

Even the fungal partner should not be treated as a passive cable. The fungus is alive, metabolically active and under selection to acquire carbon and reproduce. It may allocate nutrients towards hosts that supply more carbon, proliferate in nutrient-rich patches, or reduce exchange when conditions change. Some mycorrhizal relationships look mutualistic; others drift towards parasitism when one partner receives less than it gives. The same fungus that helps one tree may compete with another for nitrogen. The same network that appears to connect plants may also allow fungi to manage their own resources across multiple hosts.

The physical structure of these networks changes how soil works. Hyphae bind particles into aggregates, move nutrients across small distances, and leave behind fungal residues when they die. Those residues can become part of longer-lived soil organic matter, especially when microbial remains bind to minerals. But mycorrhizal fungi do not simply “store carbon” in a straightforward way. They receive plant carbon, build tissue, respire carbon dioxide, interact with decomposer microbes and, in some cases, stimulate decomposition as they mine organic matter for nutrients. Recent forest carbon research has found strong links between fungal community composition and soil carbon patterns, but it also shows why simple claims are risky: different fungi can push carbon cycling in different directions.

A concrete example is a conifer forest growing on nutrient-poor soil. The trees may allocate a large share of photosynthetic carbon below ground because nutrients, not light, are the limiting constraint. Ectomycorrhizal fungi form sheaths around root tips, extend hyphae into the soil, and use enzymes and organic acids to access forms of nitrogen or phosphorus the tree cannot easily reach alone. The visible mushrooms that appear after rain are only the fruiting bodies. Most of the organism is the mycelium threaded through roots and soil. When forestry machinery compacts soil, when topsoil is removed, or when a clear-fell changes temperature and moisture, the living conditions for this mycelium can change abruptly.

Another example is forest regeneration after disturbance. Seedlings do not enter a neutral underground world. They germinate into soil already occupied by fungi, roots, bacteria and decaying organic matter. In some settings, existing mycorrhizal fungi can help young trees establish by rapidly colonising their roots. In others, the existing fungal community may favour some tree species over others, shaping which seedlings survive. This is one reason forest recovery is not just a matter of planting stems in the ground. The below-ground community can influence whether those stems become a functioning forest.

The story also matters for climate and conservation. Mycorrhizal fungi are involved in plant productivity, nutrient cycling and soil carbon dynamics at large scales. A 2025 Nature study mapping global mycorrhizal fungal richness reported that mycorrhizal fungi form extensive underground networks and that many biodiversity hotspots for these fungi fall outside protected areas.   That does not mean protecting fungi is a simple climate solution. It means forest policy that ignores below-ground biodiversity is missing part of the system it is trying to manage.

The best way to understand mycorrhizal networks is to abandon both extremes. They are not magical forest internet cables through which wise old trees send care packages to their children. Nor are they a debunked fantasy. Mycorrhizal fungi are real, ancient and ecologically consequential. They help trees acquire nutrients, alter competition, influence seedling establishment, build and break down soil carbon, and sometimes connect plants in ways that allow materials or signals to move between them.

The forest beneath the forest is therefore not a hidden utopia. It is infrastructure, marketplace, digestive system and battleground at once. Its exchanges are chemical rather than sentimental; its networks are patchy, changing and difficult to measure. That makes them less tidy than the “wood-wide web” metaphor, but more impressive. A forest is not just a collection of trees. It is a layered community of organisms negotiating matter and energy above and below ground, with fungi doing much of the work where light cannot reach.

# Human-eyes

The skill helps writers see what marks their prose as machine-flavoured by surfacing patterns and explaining them. Removing or replacing them is opt-in.

Three principles cut across the skill:

1. **The writer decides what to do.** The audit shows every flagged pattern with its location and a short explanation. There is no filter and no ranking. The writer reads the audit and chooses whether to act, and how.
2. **The writer chooses the depth when the skill generates new prose.** Audit and Suggestions return everything regardless of depth. Rewrite and Write run at a depth the writer picks (Balanced or All).
3. **Every interaction with text starts with an audit.** "Human-eyes this" reads as Audit alone, not Rewrite. Direct rewrite requests still audit first; the findings are part of the Rewrite output. The audit teaches the writer to spot the pattern; whatever happens after is the writer's call.

The skill does not score the draft or classify authorship. It does not modify the writer's source file. After an audit, it asks what the writer wants next.

| **Write** | The writer hands the agent a brief and no draft. Triggers: "write a 500-word post on X", "draft an opening paragraph about Y", "compose a blog post on Z without sounding AI". | A new draft at the chosen depth. |

If the writer's intent is genuinely ambiguous and the agent can ask, ask whether the writer wants an audit or a rewrite, or whether they're handing over a brief for a new draft. If the agent can't ask (headless invocation, no human turn), default to Audit only.

### Rendering rules

- Lexical patterns (specific words or phrases) get the `- "<quoted>"` shape.
- Structural patterns (paragraph-length uniformity, anaphoric scaffolding, section scaffolding, sentence-length variance) get a `Where:` line that describes where the pattern shows up and what it looks like in the draft. They have no single quotable instance to point at.
- Keep explanations concrete and avoid jargon. The point is to teach the writer how to recognise the pattern rather than display the catalogue.

If no patterns are detected, say so plainly:

> Audit clean: no AI tells detected. Want me to look at a specific aspect of the draft more closely?

If the grader is unavailable (no Python, restricted environment), fall back to a manual scan reading `references/patterns.md`. Disclose the limitation: a manual scan covers surface patterns and cannot replicate the script's structural and density checks.

## The depth dial (Rewrite and Write only)

Two settings:

- **Balanced** *(default)*: address surface-level and strong AI tells — lexical tells (such as `delve` and em dashes), formulaic openers, manufactured-insight framing, signposted conclusions, AI vocabulary clustering, contrived contrast, and soft scaffold phrasing. Most context warnings can be preserved if they are doing real work. Structural patterns like paragraph-length uniformity may remain.
- **All**: address every flagged pattern, even the implicit and structural ones a reader wouldn't consciously notice. Voice can take a hit at this depth. The goal is unambiguous human-shape; preserving every flourish comes second.

When the user has not chosen a depth, ask which depth they want. Balanced is the lighter pass; All is the deeper one. Default to Balanced if the writer declines to choose.

The dial only governs Rewrite and Write. It does not apply to Audit or Suggestions. Both of those return the full list of flagged tells regardless.

## Content patterns

### 1. Significance inflation

**Words to watch:** stands/serves as, is a testament/reminder, a vital/significant/crucial/pivotal/key role/moment, underscores/highlights its importance, reflects broader, symbolizing its ongoing/enduring/lasting, setting the stage for, marking/shaping the, represents/marks a shift, key turning point, evolving landscape, indelible mark, deeply rooted, remarkably, strikingly, staggering/staggeringly

Inflates importance by claiming things "represent" or "contribute to" broader trends without explaining why anyone should care.

**Before:**
> The Statistical Institute of Catalonia was officially established in 1989, marking a pivotal moment in the evolution of regional statistics in Spain. This initiative was part of a broader movement across Spain to decentralize administrative functions and enhance regional governance.

**After:**
> The Statistical Institute of Catalonia was established in 1989 to collect and publish regional statistics independently from Spain's national statistics office.


### 2. Notability claims

**Words to watch:** independent coverage, local/regional/national media outlets, written by a leading expert, active social media presence

Asserts notability by listing sources without context, as though the mention itself is the story.

**Before:**
> Her views have been cited in The New York Times, BBC, Financial Times, and The Hindu. She maintains an active social media presence with over 500,000 followers.

**After:**
> In a 2024 New York Times interview, she argued that AI regulation should focus on outcomes rather than methods.


### 3. Superficial -ing analyses

**Words to watch:** highlighting/underscoring/emphasizing..., ensuring..., reflecting/symbolizing..., contributing to..., cultivating/fostering..., encompassing..., showcasing...

Present participle phrases tacked onto sentences to simulate analytical depth without adding information.

**Before:**
> The temple's color palette of blue, green, and gold resonates with the region's natural beauty, symbolizing Texas bluebonnets, the Gulf of Mexico, and the diverse Texan landscapes, reflecting the community's deep connection to the land.

**After:**
> The temple uses blue, green, and gold. According to the architect, the colours were chosen to reference local bluebonnets and the Gulf coast.


### 4. Promotional language

**Words to watch:** boasts a, vibrant, rich (figurative), profound, enhancing its, showcasing, exemplifies, commitment to, natural beauty, nestled, in the heart of, groundbreaking (figurative), renowned, breathtaking, must-visit, stunning

Reads like tourism marketing rather than description.

**Before:**
> Nestled within the breathtaking region of Gonder in Ethiopia, Alamata Raya Kobo stands as a vibrant town with a rich cultural heritage and stunning natural beauty.

**After:**

### 5. Vague attributions

**Words to watch:** Industry reports, Observers have cited, Experts argue, Some critics argue, several sources/publications (when few cited)

Attributes opinions to vague authorities without specific sources, creating illusions of consensus.

**Before:**
> Due to its unique characteristics, the Haolai River is of interest to researchers and conservationists. Experts believe it plays a crucial role in the regional ecosystem.

**After:**
> The Haolai River supports several endemic fish species, according to a 2019 survey by the Chinese Academy of Sciences.


### 6. Formulaic challenges sections

**Words to watch:** Despite its... faces several challenges..., Despite these challenges, Challenges and Legacy, Future Outlook

Formulaic "Challenges" sections that acknowledge a problem then immediately reassure the reader things are fine.

**Before:**
> Despite its industrial prosperity, Korattur faces challenges typical of urban areas, including traffic congestion and water scarcity. Despite these challenges, with its strategic location and ongoing initiatives, Korattur continues to thrive as an integral part of Chennai's growth.

**After:**
> Traffic congestion increased after 2015 when three new IT parks opened. The municipal corporation began a stormwater drainage project in 2022 to address recurring floods.

**Soft scaffold phrases:** "One useful area...", "Another useful area...", "The main strength...", "The main risk...", "Good use usually comes down to..." These phrases are not flashy, which is why they survive rewrites. They mark a generated explainer that is arranging information into bland labelled blocks instead of writing from a real line of thought.

# Personality and soul

Voice-craft guidance for the Rewrite and Write actions. The grader catches AI patterns to remove; this guide is about what to add or preserve so the result reads as written by a human, not just as text without AI tells.

## The real problem: subtraction

Most AI tells are things AI **adds**: vocabulary, formatting, rhetorical devices. But research shows AI also **subtracts**. Abdulhai et al. (2026) found that extensive LLM use led to a ~70% increase in essays that remained neutral, 50% fewer pronouns, and fewer anecdotes or personal references. Even when instructed to only fix grammar, LLMs frequently changed the writer's conclusions.

Removing AI patterns is therefore only half the job. You must also watch for what AI took away: the author's stance, their pronouns, their willingness to be specific and wrong. Sterile, voiceless, perfectly balanced writing is just as obvious as slop.

## Experiential vacancy

AI has no lived experience, so it generalises. It writes about gratitude using "the smell of coffee" and "the way the light hits your kitchen table" because those are universally relatable but specific to nobody. A related pattern is **faux specificity** (pattern 36): AI *performs* specificity by constructing examples from genre conventions rather than lived experience. The detail sounds concrete but belongs to nobody.

Human writing draws on real memories, named people, actual places, felt emotions. When rewriting, look not just for patterns to remove but for the **absence** of specificity, vulnerability, and personal stakes.

## Density without purpose

A good writer uses em dashes, triads, and parallelism sparingly, with intention. AI litters them throughout indiscriminately. The issue is not that any single device appears, but that they cluster and serve no rhetorical purpose. When scanning for patterns, watch for pileups.

## Signs of soulless writing (even if technically clean)

- Every sentence follows the same length and structure
- No opinions, just neutral reporting: stance has been stripped or was never there
- No acknowledgment of uncertainty or mixed feelings
- No appropriate point-of-view presence; pronouns depleted
- No humour, no edge, no personality
- Observations are universally relatable but never personally specific (faux specificity)
- Emotionally safe: never takes an uncomfortable position
- Tonal uniformity: one register throughout, no drift or register breaks (pattern 35)
- Every paragraph wraps up neatly with a miniature conclusion (pattern 34)

## How to add voice

**Have opinions.** "I keep going back and forth on this" is more human than neutrally listing pros and cons.

**Vary rhythm within reason.** Mix sentence lengths for natural cadence, but not one-word dramatic punctuation. It should feel like a person thinking at different speeds.

**Acknowledge complexity.** "This is impressive, but it also makes me a little uneasy" carries more weight than unqualified praise or a clean pro/con list.

**Match the natural point-of-view.** If the piece reads as personal reflection, use "I". If it reads as impersonal observation, use "one" or third person. Do not default to first person. Match whatever POV the text is already using or would naturally use for its genre and tone.

**Be specific rather than atmospheric.** Instead of mood words ("eerie", "striking", "fascinating"), describe the concrete detail that caused the feeling.

**Break register.** If the whole text sits in one tone, introduce at least one register shift: a moment of informality, a parenthetical doubt, a change in sentence rhythm.

After rain, a forest can trick the eye. Leaves shine, trunks darken, birds resume their noise. The busier part of the place may be under your boots. In the damp soil, tree roots run through mats of fungal threads, many of them far thinner than a root hair. Those threads help decide how trees eat, how seedlings start, and how carbon moves through the ground.

The name for the partnership is mycorrhiza, from words meaning fungus and root. In this association, a fungus colonises a plant root and sends hyphae out into soil. The plant pays with sugars made by photosynthesis. The fungus returns phosphorus, nitrogen, water, or trace elements that the root system would struggle to reach alone. More than 80 percent of plant species form some kind of mycorrhizal partnership, so this is ordinary forest machinery rather than a rare trick.

For a tree, fungal filaments are cheap reach. Roots are bulky compared with hyphae. A hypha can enter small pores, pass through wet leaf litter, and work across bits of mineral soiwhere a root hair has little room. Ectomycorrhizal fungi, common with pines, birches, oaks, beeches, and eucalypts, wrap root tips in a sheath and grow between root cells. Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi go inside root cells and make branched exchange structures. The plumbing differs. The deal stays recognisable: plant carbon goes one way, soil resources come back.

That deal changes with the soil. A tree does not gain the same return from every fungus. One partner may be good at scavenging inorganic phosphorus. Another may help release nitrogen from organic matter. In ectomycorrhizal forests, reviews find wide variation in nitrogen transfer, depending on the fungal species and the chemical form of the nitrogen. Poor soil can make fungal partners worth a large carbon payment. Fertilised soil can change the price.

This is the real territory behind the phrase "wood-wide web". A common mycorrhizal network means a physical fungal connection between the roots of two or more plants. Materials can travel along such links in some settings. The hard part is proving the route in a living forest. Carbon or chemical cues can also move through leaking roots, soil water, microbes, and air. A 2023 review in Nature Ecology & Evolution warned that several popular claims about these networks in forests have outrun the field evidence, especially claims about widespread benefits lings and mature trees feeding their offspring.

The warning matters because the underlying biology is already interesting. Suzanne Simard and colleagues published a famous experiment in 1997 using paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings in British Columbia. Carbon isotope labels moved between seedlings associated with ectomycorrhizal fungi, with a net gain by Douglas-fir under the conditions tested. That experiment helped loosen the picture of trees as isolated competitors. It showed that roots and fungi could make a shared below-ground system.

A careful reading leaves room for wonder without turning the result into folklore. Carbon transfer has been detected. Fungal pathways can contribute. Those facts do not show that an old tree chooses to feed a seedling, or that the transfer improves survival across forests generally. Later reviews give a mixed picture: connection to a network may help a seedling in one setting, cost it carbon in another, or make little measurable difference.

Claims about communication need the same restraint. Plants respond to chemical cues. Damaged leaves can change their defensive chemistry, and neighbouring plants sometimes shift their own chemistry after nearby damage. Some experiments suggest that mycorrhizal networks may carry stress-related signals. One study reported carbon and defence-related signalling from damaged Douglas-fir to nearby ponderosa pine through ectomycorrhizal links. That is biochemical response, not speech. No intention or language is required for one organism's changed chemistry to alter another's physiology.

The fungus has its own interests too. It is alive, it uses carbon, and it faces selection to reproduce. It may send more nutrients toward hosts that supply more sugar. It may grow harder into nutrient-rich patches. It may reduce exchange when conditions shift. A relationship that benefits both partners in one season can become lopsided in another. The same network that joins plants also gives the fungus a way to manage resources across hosts.

Hyphae also change the soil itself. They bind particles into aggregates, move nutrients over small distances, and leave residues after death. Some of those residues become longer-lived soil organic matter when microbial remains attach to minerals. Carbon storage, though, is messy. Mycorrhizal fungi receive plant carbon, build tissue, respire carbon dioxide, interact with decomposers, and sometimes stimulate decay while mining organic matter for nutrients. Forest carbon studies now link fungal community composition with soil carbon patterns, but the direction varies by fungal group and site.

Picture a conifer forest on poor soil. Light may be plentiful while usable nitrogen or phosphorus is scarce. The trees send a large share of photosynthetic carbon below ground. Ectomycorrhizal fungi sheath root tips, push hyphae into the soil, and use enzymes or organic acids to reach compounds the tree cannot easily use by itself. The mushrooms after rain are fruiting bodies. Most of the organism is the mycelium in the soil. Compaction from machinery, topsoil loss, or a clear-fell can change that habitat fast.

Seedlings enter this already occupied ground. Existing fungi can help young trees establish quickly by colonising fresh roots. They can also favour one tree species over another. That is one reason forest recovery involves more than putting stems into dirt. The underground community helps determine whether planted stems become a functioning forest.

The conservation argument follows from that. Mycorrhizal fungi are tied to plant productivity, nutrient cycling, and soil carbon at large scales. A 2025 Nature study mapping global mycorrhizal fungal richness reported many hotspots outside protected areas. Protecting fungi is not a simple climate lever, but forest policy that ignores below-ground biodiversity leaves out part of the system it claims to manage.

After rain, a forest can trick the eye. Leaves shine, trunks darken, birds start up again. The busier part of the place may be under your boots. In the damp soil, tree roots run through mats of fungal threads, many of them far thinner than a root hair. Those threads help decide what trees can eat and which seedlings get a start. They also shape the path carbon takes through the ground.

The name for the partnership is mycorrhiza. It joins old words for fungus and root. In this association, a fungus colonises a plant root and sends hyphae out into soil. The plant pays with sugars made by photosynthesis. The fungus returns phosphorus and nitrogen, plus water or trace elements that the root system would struggle to reach alone. More than 80 percent of plant species form some kind of mycorrhizal partnership, so this is ordinary forest machinery rather than a rare trick.

For a tree, fungal filaments are cheap reach. Roots are bulky compared with hyphae. A hypha can enter small pores, pass through wet leaf litr, and work across bits of mineral soil where a root hair has little room.

Ectomycorrhizal fungi, common with pines and many broad-leaved trees, wrap root tips in a sheath and grow between root cells. Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi go inside root cells and make branched exchange structures. The plumbing differs. The deal stays recognisable: plant carbon goes one way; soil resources come back.

That deal changes with the soil. A tree does not gain the same return from every fungus. One partner may be good at scavenging inorganic phosphorus. Another may help release nitrogen from organic matter. In ectomycorrhizal forests, reviews find wide variation in nitrogen transfer. The answer depends on the fungal species and on the chemical form of the nitrogen. Poor soil can make fungal partners worth a large carbon payment. Fertilised soil can change the price.

This is the real territory behind the phrase "wood-wide web". A common mycorrhizal network means a physical fungal connection between the roots of two or more plants. Materials can travel along such links in some settings. The hard part is proving the route in a living forest. Carbon or chemical cues can move through leaking roots and soil water. Microbes can consume compounds and release them again. Plants can also respond to airborne signals. The fungus has its own interests too. It uses carbon and faces selection to reproduce. It may send more nutrients toward hosts that supply more sugar. It may grow harder into nutrient-rich patches. It may reduce exchange when conditions shift.

A relationship that benefits both partners in one season can become lopsided in another. The same network that joins plants also gives the fungus a way to manage resources across hosts.

In the damp soil, tree roots run through mats of fungal threads, many of them far thinner than a root hair. Those threads help decide what trees can eat and which seedlings get a start. They also shape the path carbon takes through the ground.

The name for the partnership is mycorrhiza. It joins old words for fungus and root. In this association, a fungus colonises a plant root and sends hyphae out into soil. The plant pays with sugars made by photosynthesis. The fungus returns phosphorus and nitrogen, plus water or trace elements that the root system would struggle to reach alone. More than 80 percent of plant species form some kind of mycorrhizal partnership, so this is ordinary forest machinery rather than a rare trick.

For a tree, fungal filaments are cheap reach. Roots are bulky compared with hyphae. A hypha can enter small pores, pass through wet leaf litter, and work across bits of mineral soil where a root hair has little room.

Ectomycorrhizal fungi, common with pines and many broad-leaved trees, wrap root tips in a sheath and grow between root cells. Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi go inside root cells and make branched exchange structures. The plumbing differs. The deal stays recognisable: plant carbon goes one way; soil resources come back.

That deal changes with the soil. A tree does not gain the same return from every fungus. One partner may be good at scavenging inorganic phosphorus. Another may help release nitrogen from organic matter. In ectomycorrhizal forests, reviews find wide variation in nitrogen transfer. The answer depends on the fungal species and on the chemical form of the nitrogen. Poor soil can make fungal partners worth a large carbon payment. Fertilised soil can change the price.

This is the real territory behind the phrase "wood-wide web". A common mycorrhizal network means a physical fungal connection between the roots of two or more plants. Materials can travel along such links in some settings. The hard part is proving the route in a living forest. Carbon or chemical cues can move through leaking roots and soil water. Microbes can consume compounds and release them again. Plants can also respond to airborne signals. A 2023 review in Nature Ecology & Evolution warned that several popular forest-network claims have outrun the field evidence, especially claims about seedling benefits and mature trees feeding offspring.

The warning matters because the underlying biology is already interesting. Suzanne Simard and colleagues published a famous experiment in 1997 using paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings in British Columbia. Carbon isotope labels moved between seedlings associated with ectomycorrhizal fungi, with a net gain by Douglas-fir under the conditions tested. That experiment helped loosen the picture of trees as isolated competitors. It showed that roots and fungi could make a shared below-ground system.

A careful reading leaves room for wonder without turning the result into folklore. Carbon transfer has been detected. Fungal pathways can contribute. Those facts do not show that an old tree chooses to feed a seedling, or that the transfer improves survival across forests generally. Later reviews give a mixed picture. Network access may help a seedling in one setting, cost it carbon in another, or make little measurable difference.

Claims about communication need the same restraint. Plants respond to chemical cues. Damaged leaves can change their defensive chemistry, and neighbouring plants sometimes shift their own chemistry after nearby damage. Some experiments suggest that mycorrhizal networks may carry stress-related signals. One study reported carbon and defence-related signalling from damaged Douglas-fir to nearby ponderosa pine through ectomycorrhizal links. That is biochemical response, not speech. One organism's changed chemistry can alter another's physiology without intention or language.

The fungus has its own interests too. It uses carbon and faces selection to reproduce. It may send more nutrients toward hosts that supply more sugar. It may grow harder into nutrient-rich patches. It may reduce exchange when conditions shift.

A relationship that benefits both partners in one season can become lopsided in another. The same network that joins plants also gives the fungus a way to manage resources across hosts.

Hyphae also change the soil itself. They bind particles into aggregates and move nutrients over small distances. After death, they leave residues. Some residues become longer-lived soil organic matter when microbial remains attach to minerals.

Carbon storage, though, is messy. Mycorrhizal fungi receive plant carbon and build tissue. They respire carbon dioxide. They interact with decomposers. Sometimes they stimulate decay while mining organic matter for nutrients. Forest carbon studies now link fungal community composition with soil carbon patterns, but the direction varies by fungal group and site.

Picture a conifer forest on poor soil. Light may be plentiful while usable nitrogen or phosphorus is scarce. The trees send a large share of photosynthetic carbon below ground. Ectomycorrhizal fungi sheath root tips and push hyphae into the soil. They use enzymes or organic acids to reach compounds the tree cannot easily use by itself. The mushrooms after rain are fruiting bodies. Most of the organism is the mycelium in the soil. Machinery can compact that habitat. Topsoil removal can damage it. A clear-fell can change heat and moisture fast.

Seedlings enter this already occupied ground. Existing fungi can help young trees establish quickly by colonising fresh roots. They can also favour one tree species over another.

That is one reason forest recovery involves more than putting stems into dirt. The underground community helps determine whether planted stems become a functioning forest.

The conservation argument follows from that. Mycorrhizal fungi are tied to plant growth and to the cycling of nutrients and soil carbon. A 2025 Nature study mapping global mycorrhizal fungal richness reported many hotspots outside protected areas. Protecting fungi is not a simple climate lever, but forest policy that ignores below-ground biodiversity leaves out part of the system it claims to manage.

So the better story is plainer and stranger than the slogan. Mycorrhizal fungi help trees obtain nutrients. They alter competition and influence regeneration. They affect soil carbon. In some cases, they connect plants so that materials or cues move between them. The underground forest is infrastructure and appetite at once. Chemical exchanges happen in patches and are hard to measure. That makes the forest less tidy than the internet metaphor and more alive.
