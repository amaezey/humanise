The first thing I learned about linocut printing is that the block remembers hesitation. I expected the carving to be the tidy part, almost like drawing with a different tool, but the lino pushed back. Every line had to be chosen twice: once in pencil, then again with the blade in my hand. The small gouge made me slow down in a way I am not used to. If I rushed, the mark widened or wandered. If I pressed too lightly, nothing happened.

Printing was messier, and better, than I imagined. The ink looked smooth on the roller until it met the block, where it picked up every shallow cut, crumb, and uneven patch. My first print came out pale in the centre and heavy at the edge. I was annoyed for about ten seconds, then I started liking it. The mistakes made the image feel less like a copy and more like evidence of the process.

What surprised me most was how backwards the thinking felt. I had to carve away the light and leave the dark behind, which made me notice shapes instead of outlines. It is a simple reversal, but it changes the whole rhythm of making. By the end, I was not chasing a perfect print. I was learning how pressure, ink, paper, and patience all leave their own marks. That feels like the point of a first attempt: not mastery, but a more honest eye. And, honestly, that was more satisfying than getting it right.
