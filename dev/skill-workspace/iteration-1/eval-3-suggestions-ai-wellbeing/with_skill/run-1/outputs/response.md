**Audit, 6 AI tells found**

Generic staccato emphasis  
Where: the draft uses runs of short dramatic sentences, especially around the dream passage and the ending: "They are in the kitchen. They are wearing the old jumper. They have not died after all." / "Not disappears. Not resolves neatly. But changes."  
Why: short beats can work in grief writing, but stacked too neatly they start to feel generated, like the prose is pressing the reader for emotion.

Mechanical repeated sentence starts  
Where: four consecutive sentences begin with "Sometimes" in the insomnia paragraph.  
Why: repetition can be intentional, but here it creates a template rhythm: example, example, example, example.

Curly quotes  
- "good sleep habits"
- "How do I force myself to sleep?"
- "How do I make the night less hostile?"
- "Now I am grieving and failing at sleep."
Why: curly quotes are not an authorship tell by themselves. They only matter if the intended output is plain cleaned Markdown, where smart typography can look like generated polish.

Headings in prose  
- "# The hour grief comes looking"  
Why: a Markdown H1 can make an essay feel packaged rather than written as continuous prose. This is context-sensitive: it is fine if this is meant to be a web article with a title.

Dense negation  
Where: the piece repeatedly frames claims through "not": "does not respect", "not just", "does not simply", "not always", "not poor time management", "not caused", "not to win", "not because", "not healing", "not devotion", "does not keep", "not perfect sleep", "Not disappears. Not resolves neatly."  
Why: too much negation makes the prose feel over-shaped around corrective turns. It can sound like the writer is constantly laundering a point through what it is not.

Triad density  
Where: several three-part structures recur: "the dead feel closer, the mind gets louder, and the body refuses...", "memory, emotional regulation, attention, immune function...", "less caffeine, a dark room and no phone in bed", "sadness, tearfulness and insomnia", "depression, anxiety, intrusive thoughts..."  
Why: three-part lists create a recognisable generated cadence when they pile up. A few are fine; seven makes the rhythm feel too engineered.

**Suggestions, one per flag**

Generic staccato emphasis  
Try: combine or roughen the short runs so they do not all land as dramatic beats. For example: "In the dream they are back in the kitchen, wearing the old jumper, and for a few seconds the mind accepts the impossible."

Mechanical repeated sentence starts  
Try: keep one or two "Sometimes" openings, then vary the rest: "It can be a clean, brutal wakefulness at 3 am. Or the mind may replay the last conversation, the hospital room, the phone call, the thing said badly, the thing never said. Sleep may come easily and break before dawn. The body may sleep for ten hours and wake exhausted."

Curly quotes  
Try: if this is plain Markdown, change smart quotes to straight quotes: `"good sleep habits"`, `"How do I force myself to sleep?"`, `"Now I am grieving and failing at sleep."`

Headings in prose  
Try: if this is an essay title, remove the Markdown marker and leave it as a title line: `The hour grief comes looking`. If it is meant to flow as prose, cut the title and begin with "Grief has its own clock."

Dense negation  
Try: recast some corrective sentences as direct claims. For example, change "Sleep does not cure grief" to "Sleep gives grief a body that can endure it." Change "The aim is not perfect sleep" to "The aim is enough safety for the body to stand down, even briefly."

Triad density  
Try: break the list rhythm by making some lists shorter, more specific, or syntactically different. For example, change "memory, emotional regulation, attention, immune function and the body's capacity to recover" to "memory and emotional regulation, as well as the body's capacity to recover."
