**Rewrite**

The interesting part of a forest is mostly underground. Above the path, the obvious things: light through leaves, the slow widening of trunks, birds in the branches. Below the litter, something stranger: a teaspoon of forest soil holds a dense mesh of fungal filaments, threaded through tree roots so completely that the two are barely separable. Much of how the forest feeds itself and stores carbon runs through them.

The arrangement has a name, mycorrhiza, from the Greek for fungus-root. A fungus colonises a plant's root system and sends out hyphae, microscopic filaments, into the soil around it. The plant supplies sugars made through photosynthesis; the fungus repays that carbon with phosphorus and nitrogen drawn from places too small or too distant for the roots to reach themselves, plus the water and trace elements the tree could not easily collect on its own. More than 80 per cent of plant species form one of these partnerships, and in many soils the fungal biomass involved is enormous.

For a tree the upgrade is real. Roots are thick and metabolically expensive, while a fungal filament can slide into pores that block any root, run through patches of decomposing leaf, and reach across small gaps the root cannot bridge. The fungus enlarges the tree's absorbing surface and widens its chemical menu. The two main fungal designs handle that exchange differently. Ectomycorrhizal fungi are common on pines and oaks (also on many beeches, birches and eucalypts); they sheath the root tip and grow between cells without entering them. Arbuscular fungi turn up across a much wider range of plants. They push inside root cells and form tiny branched exchange organs. The architecture differs; the trade is similar. Carbon goes out, nutrients come back.

The trade is also dynamic. Fungi vary in what they can scavenge and how much carbon they ask for. Some specialise in inorganic phosphorus; others pull nitrogen out of organic matter that no plant could touch directly. A 2023 review of ectomycorrhizal forests concluded that nitrogen transfer is highly variable, depending on the fungal species involved and on the chemistry of the surrounding soil. So a spruce on poor ground may pour carbon into its fungal partners, while one growing on fertilised soil pulls back, because the carbon it would spend buys less of what the tree actually needs.

This is where the wood-wide web enters the picture, and where the popular version slips its anchors. A common mycorrhizal network, in the technical sense, means one fungal individual that physically connects the roots of two or more plants. Such a connection could in principle carry materials and chemical signals between them. Showing that this happens in a living forest is much harder. Researchers have to rule out other routes: roots leaking compounds into the soil; microbes consuming and re-releasing those compounds; water moving dissolved chemicals about; plants responding to volatile cues in the air. A 2023 review in Nature Ecology and Evolution argued that several of the most repeated public claims about these networks (that they blanket forests, that they reliably help seedlings, that mature trees deliberately feed their offspring) are still ahead of the field evidence.

The networks are real. The story that grew up around them ran some way past the data.

The classic experiment is from 1997. Suzanne Simard and her colleagues, working in British Columbia, used carbon isotopes to track movement between paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings sharing ectomycorrhizal partners. They detected bidirectional transfer, with a net flow towards the Douglas-fir under the conditions of the experiment. The paper became famous because it suggested that forest plants might be doing something more interesting than competing as isolated individuals.

The narrower reading is also the more interesting one. Carbon can move between plants and fungi can carry some of it, but that is a much smaller claim than "mature trees deliberately feed seedlings", and smaller again than "those transfers matter for whether the seedling lives". Later reviews looking across more forests have found mixed results. In some settings, plugging into an existing network helps a seedling get established. In others, the seedling loses carbon to the fungus and shows no benefit; sometimes it faces stronger underground competition besides. Forest life is an ecological economy with uneven terms, and the terms shift by species and by site.

The communication claim wants the same restraint. Plants do detect chemical cues: a leaf under attack changes its defensive chemistry, and nearby plants pick up volatile signals from damaged neighbours and adjust their own. Some experiments suggest mycorrhizal networks can carry stress-related signals between plants. One study reported that injured Douglas-fir transferred carbon and defence-related compounds to ponderosa pine through ectomycorrhizal connections. Calling this conversation overstates the case. It is biochemistry: chemical changes in one organism alter the chemical environment of organisms it is linked to, and those organisms respond in their own metabolism.

The fungus is hungry too. It is alive and metabolically active, under selection to grab carbon and reproduce. It can move nutrients towards hosts that pay better, grow into nutrient-rich patches, and pull back from exchange when conditions stop favouring it. Some mycorrhizal partnerships are mutualistic; others tip towards parasitism when the balance breaks. The same fungus that benefits one tree can compete with another over the same patch of nitrogen. The same network that links plants also lets the fungus juggle resources across them.

These networks change how soil works as a physical thing. Hyphae bind soil particles into aggregates and shuttle nutrients across small distances. When they die, their residues can become part of the longer-lived soil carbon pool, especially where microbial remains stick to mineral surfaces. Mycorrhizal fungi do more than store carbon, though. They take it in from plants, build tissue, breathe carbon dioxide back out, and in some cases speed up decomposition as they mine organic matter for the nutrients they need. Recent forest-carbon studies show tight links between fungal community composition and soil carbon patterns; they also show why simple "fungi store carbon" claims should be handled with care. Different fungi push the cycle in different directions.

Take a conifer forest on poor soil. The trees may push a large share of their photosynthate underground because nutrients, and not light, are the limit. Ectomycorrhizal fungi sheath the root tips and send hyphae through the soil. They secrete enzymes and organic acids to break open forms of nitrogen and phosphorus the tree could otherwise never reach. The mushrooms that appear after rain are only the fruiting bodies. Most of the organism is the mycelium running through soil and root. When a logging machine compacts that soil, when topsoil is stripped, when a clear-fell drops the canopy and the local temperature and moisture swing suddenly, the conditions the mycelium depends on shift fast.

Forest regeneration runs into the same underground community. Seedlings germinate into soil already occupied by fungi and bacteria and decaying organic matter, and by the roots of whatever was there before. Sometimes the resident fungi colonise the new roots quickly and help the young tree get established. Sometimes the existing community favours one tree species over another and shapes which seedlings live. Replanting a site is therefore more than a matter of getting stems into the ground.

The story matters for climate and conservation, too. Mycorrhizal fungi sit inside plant productivity and nutrient cycling at large scales, which puts them inside the global soil carbon picture too. A 2025 Nature paper that mapped global mycorrhizal fungal richness reported that many biodiversity hotspots for these fungi fall outside the world's protected areas. That is awkward news: a fair amount of the soil worth protecting for its below-ground biodiversity is currently protected, where it is protected at all, on the basis of what is happening above ground. Calling fungi a climate solution would overstate the case in a different direction. Calling forest policy that ignores below-ground biodiversity adequate would also overstate it.

Two popular framings overshoot. One turns the underground forest into a warm internet of sharing trees. The other dismisses the science as wishful thinking. The honest picture sits between, and is more interesting than either. Mycorrhizal networks are ancient and consequential. The fungi help trees acquire nutrients. They alter how plants compete and shape which seedlings establish in disturbed ground. They build soil structure as well as breaking it down. In some settings they let materials or signals move between plants. All of it varies by species and by site, and a fair amount has yet to be settled by experiment.

So: the layer beneath the leaf litter is a working part of the forest. Infrastructure and marketplace, with some battleground. Its exchanges are chemical rather than sentimental. The connections are patchy and provisional, and the way they change from one square metre to the next defeats the wood-wide web metaphor. A forest is a layered community of organisms negotiating matter and energy above and below ground, and the fungi handle most of the work in the part you cannot see.
