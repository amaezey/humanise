Workshops as portals

And how to create clarity in consulting work

Halfway through the morning, someone in the room says the thing everyone has been avoiding.

“So are we actually trying to fix the onboarding process, or are we trying to decide who owns it?”

There is usually a small silence after a question like that. Not an awkward silence, exactly. More like the sound of a group recalibrating. People look at each other. Someone leans back. Someone else gives a tiny nod, because yes, that is the real question. The slide on the wall says “current-state journey map”, but the work has moved somewhere more useful. The room has crossed the threshold from performance into truth.

This is why I like workshops.

Not because they are fun. Not because they produce sticky notes. Not because everyone gets to feel included, though that can matter. I like workshops because they are portals. They move a client and a consultant from abstract possibility into a shared experience of working together. They show the client how you think. They show you how the client makes decisions. They reveal whether the problem is clear, whether the group has authority, whether the politics are manageable, and whether there is enough trust to do the larger piece of work.

A good workshop is not a miniature version of the solution. It is a charged, time-boxed way to create clarity. That clarity is valuable in its own right. It is not a free prelude to the real work. It is consulting work.

This is one of the most useful lessons I have learned as a consultant: you do not need to sell the whole transformation, research program, operating model, content strategy or service redesign upfront. Often, you should not. You can sell the first intelligent container. A one-day workshop is often the best container there is.

The inside/outside problem

Consulting has a persistent inside/outside problem.

The client knows the organisation from the inside. They understand the history, the personalities, the prior attempts, the acronyms, the informal rules, the sensitivities, the teams that do not talk to each other, and the executive who has already formed a view. They know more than you do.

The consultant comes from the outside. You bring pattern recognition, method, pace, independence, external examples, and the ability to ask questions that insiders may no longer be allowed to ask. You can see things that have become normalised inside the system. But you do not yet understand the local terrain.

Both sides are slightly exposed at the beginning.

The client is wondering: will this person get it? Will they understand our context without making us explain everything from first principles? Will they be useful, or will they sell us a generic process? Will they make us look foolish in front of our stakeholders?

The consultant is wondering: is this the stated problem or the real problem? Does the sponsor have authority? Are the constraints real or inherited? Is there a decision to be made, or just an appetite for activity? Is this work feasible?

A proposal can only answer so much. A credentials deck answers even less. A workshop answers something more important: how does it feel to work together?

That question is not soft. It is commercial. It tells both parties whether there is enough shared language, confidence and momentum to proceed. It also prevents one of the most common failures in consulting: selling a large engagement before anyone has understood the shape of the problem.

A workshop lets the relationship become real without pretending everything is already known.

Clarity is a deliverable

Consultants often undercharge for clarity because they confuse it with preparation.

They treat discovery as something that happens before the paid work. They have a few unpaid conversations, diagnose too much too early, write a proposal full of assumptions, and hope the client agrees. Then the project begins, and everyone discovers that the assumptions were incomplete.

A better model is to make clarity the first paid outcome.

The point of a one-day workshop is not to solve the problem. It is to create a shared understanding of the problem, the decision context, the constraints, the stakeholders, and the next sensible move. That is not a lesser outcome. In many organisations, that is the work nobody has been able to do.

Clarity matters for the client because it reduces internal fog. A team may have been carrying a problem for months, but carrying it in fragments. One person sees a governance issue. Another sees a technology issue. Another sees a resourcing issue. Another sees a leadership issue. Another sees a “comms problem”, which often means nobody has agreed what is true.

The workshop makes the implicit visible. It gives the group a way to see the problem as a system rather than as competing grievances. It also creates a record: what we know, what we do not know, what we disagree on, what matters most, and what we are going to do next.

Clarity matters for the consultant because it protects the work. It stops you from accepting a vague brief and turning it into an overconfident scope. It lets you test the client’s appetite for evidence, decisions and trade-offs. It shows you where the risks are before you are committed to delivery.

A workshop also gives you something no written brief can provide: behavioural data.

Who speaks first? Who keeps returning to risk? Who reframes everything as a technology issue? Who has authority but stays silent? Who is treated as difficult, but is actually naming the constraint? Who asks practical questions? Who performs certainty? Who is absent, and what does their absence tell you?

This information changes the way you scope the work. It often changes whether you want the work at all.

Do not sell the workshop as the solution

A mistake I see consultants make is trying to sell a one-day workshop as though it will fix something.

“We’ll solve your strategy.”
“We’ll align the team.”
“We’ll map the whole experience.”
“We’ll define the operating model.”
“We’ll create the roadmap.”

No, you probably will not. Not properly. Not in a day.

A one-day workshop can create the conditions for those things. It can clarify the decision to be made. It can surface assumptions. It can establish priorities. It can identify evidence gaps. It can generate options. It can produce a draft direction. It can build enough shared confidence to move into a larger engagement.

But if you promise resolution, you will either over-facilitate the room into false agreement or disappoint the client when reality proves more complex.

The more honest position is stronger: this workshop will create clarity about the problem, the stakeholders, the decision points and the next phase of work.

That is chargeable. That is useful. That is a legitimate consulting outcome.

The language matters. I try to avoid selling a workshop as “collaborative ideation” or “a fun session to get everyone aligned”. That makes it sound optional. Instead, I describe it as a structured working session to establish the current state, clarify the target outcome, identify constraints and agree the next decision pathway.

This frames the workshop as serious work. It also attracts better participants. People understand they are not attending a morale exercise. They are there to think, decide and expose what needs to be resolved.

Structure for outcomes, not happiness

A workshop can feel good and still be useless.

This is a harsh truth, but it saves a lot of trouble. Participants may enjoy the session because it is different from their usual meetings, because they get to speak, because the facilitator is competent, or because the catering is decent. Enjoyment is not the same as progress.

The question is not “did people like the workshop?”

The question is “what changed because the workshop happened?”

Sometimes what changes is a decision. Sometimes it is a reframed problem. Sometimes it is agreement about scope. Sometimes it is the discovery that the client is not ready to proceed. Sometimes it is the recognition that three teams are using the same word to mean different things.

Those are outcomes.

When designing a workshop, I start with the exit conditions. By the end of the day, what must be clearer than it is now? What must the sponsor be able to say? What must the group understand? What must be documented? What decision does this workshop enable, even if it does not make the decision itself?

That usually leads to a simple structure.

First, establish the frame. Why are we here? What decision or uncertainty does this session support? What is in scope? What is out of scope? What will not be resolved today?

Second, build the shared picture. What is happening now? What evidence do we have? What are people experiencing? Where are the pain points, risks or missed opportunities? What do we know versus assume?

Third, expose the tensions. Where do participants disagree? What constraints matter? What trade-offs are unavoidable? What has been tried before? What would make this fail?

Fourth, create direction. What options are available? What criteria should be used to assess them? What is the most sensible next step? What needs further investigation?

Finally, close with commitments. Who needs to be involved next? What decisions are required? What artefact will come out of the session? What will the consultant do, and what does the client need to provide?

This is not complicated, but it is disciplined. Without that discipline, workshops become a sequence of activities rather than a progression of thinking.

The workshop is a diagnostic instrument

The workshop itself tells you what kind of consulting work may be needed next.

For example, if the group cannot agree on the problem, the next phase may need to be discovery, research or diagnostic work. If the problem is clear but ownership is confused, the next phase may need to focus on governance. If there is strong agreement but no evidence, the next phase may need to test assumptions with users, staff or data. If everyone knows the answer but nobody has permission to act, the issue is probably sponsorship or decision rights.

This is why workshops are such effective entry points. They do not just produce outputs. They reveal the nature of the work.

In one project, I was asked to run a session that was initially described as a content planning workshop. The client wanted to make a large website easier to use, and the assumed activity was to map content types and page priorities. Once we got into the room, it became obvious that the content problem was real but downstream. The deeper issue was ownership. Different business areas were publishing content to meet local needs, but nobody had authority over the whole user experience. Search was poor, navigation was inconsistent, and users were encountering the organisation as a set of internal divisions rather than as a coherent service.

The workshop did not fix the website. It did something more useful at that stage: it clarified that a content uplift alone would fail without a governance model. That changed the next piece of work. The client did not need a better page template first. They needed decision rights, publishing standards, and a clearer model for who owned the digital front door.

In another piece of work, a client came in wanting help designing a program for a new audience. The first conversation was full of good intent but too many assumptions. A one-day workshop helped us separate the policy ambition from the delivery mechanism. We mapped the audience’s likely barriers, the client’s constraints, the evidence already available, and the parts that still needed co-design. By the end, the most valuable output was not a polished program concept. It was a set of questions the organisation had to answer before investing in delivery.

That sounds modest. It was not. It prevented premature design. It saved the client from building a program around internal enthusiasm rather than participant reality.

Useful prompts for jam-sessions

Some workshops need formal activities. Others need well-held conversation.

I use “jam-session” to describe a working conversation with enough structure to stay productive but enough openness to let the real material emerge. These sessions are useful early in consulting work, especially when the client knows a lot but has not yet organised that knowledge into a clear brief.

The facilitator’s job is to keep the conversation moving between concrete evidence and useful abstraction. Too concrete, and the room drowns in detail. Too abstract, and everyone agrees with words that do not mean anything.

A few prompts I return to:

“What is the problem this work is meant to change?”

Not “what is the project?” Not “what are we building?” Start with the change. If the group cannot answer this, the brief is not ready.

“What makes this hard here?”

This prevents generic diagnosis. Most problems have a local shape. The method may be familiar, but the constraints are specific.

“What has already been tried?”

This respects the client’s history and avoids accidentally recommending something that failed two years ago. It also surfaces organisational scar tissue.

“What do people disagree about?”

This is often more useful than asking what people agree on. Agreement can be polite. Disagreement is informative.

“What would make this work fail?”

This prompt changes the energy in the room. People are often more honest when asked about failure than when asked about success.

“Who needs this to be true?”

This is useful when a claim keeps appearing without evidence. It helps separate fact from institutional preference.

“What decision does this enable?”

This keeps the workshop from becoming an exercise in discussion. Consulting work should eventually support a decision, even if that decision is to investigate further.

“What would we need to know to proceed with confidence?”

This prompt is especially useful when the room wants certainty too early. It turns anxiety into an evidence plan.

“What is out of scope, and are we comfortable with that?”

Scope is not just what you include. It is what you exclude without later pretending you forgot.

“What is the smallest useful next step?”

This helps when the group is overwhelmed. Not every next step needs to be dramatic. It needs to be real.

These prompts work because they are not clever. They are plain. They create pressure in the right places. They also make the consultant’s value visible. You are not there to dominate the room with frameworks. You are there to help the room think better than it could without you.

The artefact matters

A workshop should produce something after the room clears.

Not a transcript. Not photographs of sticky notes dumped into a slide deck. Not a decorative summary that says everyone had a productive discussion.

The artefact should capture the clarity created.

Depending on the work, this might be a concise findings note, a problem statement, a decision brief, a current-state map, a prioritised opportunity list, a draft roadmap, a scope recommendation, or a proposed next-phase plan.

The artefact is where the consulting value becomes portable. It allows the sponsor to brief upward, socialise the work, secure funding, or decide not to proceed. It also protects against the workshop becoming a pleasant memory rather than a business input.

I usually think about the artefact before designing the session. If the final output is a decision brief, the workshop needs to generate decision criteria. If the output is a discovery plan, the workshop needs to expose assumptions and evidence gaps. If the output is a roadmap, the workshop needs to clarify dependencies and sequencing.

This also helps with pricing. The client is not paying for seven hours in a room. They are paying for preparation, facilitation, synthesis, judgement and a usable artefact.

Do not undersell the day

A one-day workshop should not be priced like a casual meeting.

If you are a consultant with meaningful expertise, $4,000 for a day is not outrageous. In many contexts, it is low. The day itself may be seven hours, but the work is not.

You need to understand the context, design the session, prepare materials, manage the room, adapt in real time, synthesise the outputs, and produce something the client can use. You are also bringing years of pattern recognition into that room. The client is not buying time. They are buying compression.

A well-run workshop can save weeks of circular internal discussion. It can prevent a poorly scoped project. It can reveal that the client needs a different intervention. It can create the confidence required to fund the next stage. It can also show that a larger engagement would be wasteful or premature.

That is valuable.

Underpricing creates other problems too. It signals that the workshop is lightweight. It attracts clients who treat it as a brainstorming session rather than a serious working session. It also pressures the consultant to over-sell the next phase because the first phase was not commercially viable.

A properly priced workshop lets you be more honest. You can sell the day as its own useful engagement, not as a loss leader. If more work follows, good. If it does not, the client still receives value and you have not subsidised their indecision.

The workshop as mutual audition

There is another reason workshops work well as an entry point: they are mutual auditions.

The client is testing you. That is fair. They want to know whether you can hold complexity, work with their people, understand their language, and create momentum without bulldozing the room.

You are also testing the client. That is equally fair. You want to know whether they can make decisions, tolerate ambiguity, provide access to the right people, and engage with evidence. You want to know whether the sponsor will protect the work or disappear when trade-offs appear.

This does not need to be cynical. It is responsible.

Some clients are not ready for the work they think they want. Some are ready, but for a different piece of work. Some have a strong sponsor and a messy problem, which is often workable. Some have a neat brief and no authority, which is often not.

A workshop reveals these things quickly.

I have left workshops more confident that the client needed a substantial program of work. I have also left workshops thinking the best recommendation was to pause, gather evidence, or narrow the scope. That recommendation can feel commercially counterintuitive, but it builds trust. It also prevents the consultant from inheriting a problem that nobody is willing to own.

What the client is really buying

When a client buys a workshop, they are not only buying facilitation.

They are buying a temporary thinking environment.

Inside that environment, the normal patterns are interrupted. The urgent gives way to the important. People who usually communicate through documents or meetings are asked to think together. Assumptions become visible. Decisions become explicit. Ambiguity gets named rather than carried.

The consultant’s job is to design and hold that environment.

That means paying attention to pace, evidence, authority, participation and synthesis. It means knowing when to let the room explore and when to intervene. It means not mistaking volume for insight. It means protecting quieter forms of expertise. It means noticing when the group is avoiding the real issue.

It also means resisting the temptation to make everyone happy.

Some of the best workshops involve discomfort. Not hostility, not confusion, not theatrical conflict. Just the discomfort of being more precise than usual. The discomfort of admitting that the problem is not the one written in the brief. The discomfort of realising that a favourite solution has no clear owner. The discomfort of seeing that “alignment” has been used to cover unresolved decisions.

That discomfort is often the portal.

A workshop is not a trick

I do not see workshops as a sales trick. They are not a way to smuggle in a larger engagement. They are a way to begin in proportion to what is known.

Sometimes the next step after a workshop is a major discovery phase. Sometimes it is a small advisory engagement. Sometimes it is a roadmap. Sometimes it is a recommendation that the client do internal work before bringing in external support. The point is not to force a predetermined path. The point is to create enough clarity that the next path is credible.

This is why the one-day workshop is such a useful consulting offer. It is contained, understandable and low-risk compared with a large engagement. But it is not trivial. It gives the client something they need and gives the consultant enough exposure to scope responsibly.

It bridges the inside/outside problem by creating a shared inside, even briefly. For one day, the consultant is no longer looking at the organisation through a brief, and the client is no longer assessing the consultant through a proposal. Both are in the work.

That changes the relationship.

The threshold

By the end of a good workshop, the room should feel different. Not euphoric. Clearer.

People should be able to say what the work is really about. They should understand where they agree, where they do not, what evidence is missing, and what decision comes next. The sponsor should have language they can use. The consultant should have enough information to recommend a sensible next step.

That is the threshold.

A workshop is a portal because it moves everyone through that threshold together. Before it, the work is still mostly imagined. After it, the work has a shape. It may be larger than expected, smaller than expected, or different from what was first described. But it is no longer vague.

That is why clarity deserves to be sold.

Not as a warm-up. Not as a free diagnostic. Not as a cheerful day of sticky notes.

As real consulting work.
