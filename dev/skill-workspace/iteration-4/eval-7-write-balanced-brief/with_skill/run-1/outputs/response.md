**Brief: A 250-word personal reflection on the experience of trying linocut printing for the first time.**

**Depth: Balanced**
Why: A reflective personal piece needs voice and rhythm latitude that Balanced preserves; All would risk flattening register breaks that carry the personality of the form.

**Draft**

I bought the cheapest starter kit because I wasn't ready to take it seriously. Five gouges, a sheet of soft grey lino, a tube of black ink, watercolour paper salvaged from another box. The internet had promised a satisfying afternoon.

It wasn't. My first cut went too deep and the gouge skidded off the lino into the meat of my thumb. I stopped, taped it, kept going. The design I'd sketched (a sprig of wattle, ambitious) turned out to require more patience than my wrist had on offer. I kept forgetting to carve in mirror image. By the time I inked the block, the leaves were sitting on the wrong side of the stem.

The first pull was still good. I peeled the paper back slowly, half expecting it to come up blank, and underneath was the print. Not technically good. The registration was off, the ink was patchy where I hadn't rolled enough, and one petal had a thumbprint inside it. But it was a real thing that came out of an afternoon. A small black sprig on cream paper. Wonky. Mine.

Other people call linocut meditative. Mine wasn't, or not yet. It was twitchy and physical, full of small mistakes I could feel happening in real time. It felt more like drawing while a small machine fights back. I have a stack of failed pulls drying on the kitchen bench and I want to do another one tonight, which probably tells me something I don't fully understand yet.
