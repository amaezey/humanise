**Rewrite**

I used to believe the right productivity app would eventually fix the problem.

Not the obvious problem, which was that I had too much to do. The subtler one: I did not have a clean enough system for holding it all. Tasks lived in too many places. Notes were scattered. Ideas arrived while walking, showering, driving, scrolling, sitting in meetings, half-listening to myself promise something I knew I would forget unless I captured it immediately.

So I tried apps. Task managers, notes apps, whiteboards, kanban boards, habit trackers, calendar overlays, focus timers, personal knowledge management tools, read-it-later apps, browser extensions, automations, dashboards. Apps that promised simplicity. Apps that promised depth. Apps that promised to become the second brain I apparently needed because the first one was being throttled by work, life, tabs, notifications and a vague background dread.

For a while, each new tool worked. That is the trick of productivity software: the first week always feels like progress, because you are not yet maintaining the system. You are imagining the person you will become once the system is complete. There is the work of making categories, tagging things, building a dashboard, watching someone on YouTube explain their frictionless weekly review and thinking, yes, this is the missing layer. The app becomes a small model of a better self: calmer, more intentional, less reactive, never surprised by a deadline, never leaving a receipt in a tote bag or a document in the wrong folder.

Then reality resumes. The task manager fills with stale intentions. The notes app becomes a storage unit for things you were once excited to think about. The habit tracker quietly records your failure until you stop opening it. The dashboard becomes another surface to check. The automation breaks. The tags proliferate. What was meant to reduce cognitive load becomes a small administrative job with no salary and no end date.

I did not stop using productivity apps because they are useless. Some are very good. I stopped because I finally admitted I was using them to avoid a more uncomfortable question: what actually deserves my attention?

Most productivity apps are built around capture. Capture the task, the idea, the meeting note, the article, the insight, the thing someone mentioned in passing that might matter later. Capture everything, because storage is cheap and forgetting feels irresponsible.

But capture is not the same as commitment. A captured task still needs a decision. A saved article still needs time. A note still needs synthesis. Every item preserved for later becomes a tiny claim on the future. The app does not remove the claim; it just makes the claim look orderly.

That was my first practical observation. I was not overwhelmed because I lacked a reliable capture system. I was overwhelmed because I was capturing too much and deciding too little.

There is a particular relief in writing something down. It feels like moving the thing out of the mind. But if it matters, it returns. If it does not return, perhaps it did not matter enough. I do not mean this as a universal rule. Some work genuinely requires rigorous tracking. In my own life, though, I had confused documentation with discernment, and kept preserving things to avoid having to choose.

My second observation was that productivity apps reward tidying, not doing.

This is not a moral failing. It is a design pattern. Apps have interfaces, and interfaces invite interaction. You can drag, sort, nest, schedule, archive, reframe, link, reassign. These actions feel constructive because they are concrete. They produce visible change: a cleaner list, an aligned board, a project with a template, a note with metadata.

Meanwhile, the hard thing remains untouched: writing the difficult email, reading the dense document, making the decision, having the conversation, starting the draft, closing the loop.

Productivity theatre is still theatre even when the stage is beautifully designed. The more elaborate my system became, the easier it was to spend the first hour of a workday preparing to work. I was not procrastinating in the usual sense. I was doing something adjacent to useful, which made it harder to challenge. It looked disciplined from the outside and felt responsible from the inside. But the practical outcome was the same: I had spent energy managing representations of work instead of doing the work itself.

Third observation: most apps assume the future is more stable than it is.

A task entered on Monday can feel precise: draft proposal, review deck, call supplier, write brief. By Thursday, the context has changed. The meeting moved, someone sent new information, the urgent thing became irrelevant, the small thing became political, and the neat sequence collapsed.

Productivity apps tend to treat this as a planning issue. Build better workflows, add dependencies, run recurring reviews, reprioritise. Sometimes that helps. But often the problem is that the plan was a fiction. Useful, perhaps, but a fiction.

A weekly plan can create orientation. It cannot eliminate volatility. When I tried to force volatile work into rigid systems, I generated more maintenance than clarity. My app was always asking me to reconcile yesterday's assumptions with today's conditions, and that reconciliation became its own workload.

Fourth observation, and more personal: productivity apps made me more attentive to failure than to momentum.

A completed task disappears. An overdue task glows. This is rational from a software perspective; systems need to show what still requires action. But psychologically, it meant I spent a lot of time looking at evidence of what I had not done. Even a productive day ended with the app reminding me of the residue. The list was never empty because the list was not a measure of effort. It was a measure of everything that could be imagined, requested, deferred or dreaded.

That distinction matters. A to-do list can be endless because possibility is endless. Capacity is not. Treating the list as the source of truth gives the list too much authority. It starts to feel as though the problem is personal inefficiency, when really it is structural excess.

This is where my view has become slightly contrarian. Many productivity systems individualise what are workload, priority, boundary and political problems. If work is badly scoped, a better task manager will not fix it. If a team has no clear decision rights, a better notes system will not fix it. If every channel is treated as urgent, a focus app cannot save you. If you are trying to do too many things because saying no has social or professional costs, a habit tracker is irrelevant.

The app may help you survive the chaos. It will not help you question it.

That is not a reason to reject tools. It is a reason to stop asking tools to solve organisational and human problems they can only display.

Since stepping away from productivity apps, my system is less impressive and more honest. I use a calendar for commitments that involve time or other people. I keep a short written list for the day or week. I keep notes where they are useful, not where they satisfy an architecture. I do not try to capture every idea. I do not build dashboards for my life. I do not track habits unless there is a specific reason. I let some things be forgotten.

The last part is harder than it sounds.

There is a small arrogance in believing every thought should be preserved. There is also fear: that forgetting means losing value, that an uncaptured idea will prove important later, that an untracked task will expose you as careless. But memory has its own filtering function, and so does recurrence. If something matters, it often reappears through consequence, conversation, discomfort or need. Not always. But often enough that I no longer treat total capture as a virtue.

My current approach is deliberately plain. Each morning, I ask what would make the day meaningfully complete. Not ideal. Not maximally productive. Complete. Usually the answer is one to three things. Sometimes it is one difficult thing and several minor obligations. Sometimes it is simply staying ahead of a deadline without making the rest of the week worse.

I write those things down.

If new tasks arrive, I decide whether they belong today, later, or nowhere. "Later" is not a magic category anymore. It has to earn its place. If I cannot imagine when I would do it, or why it matters, I try not to store it as a moral debt.

For reading and ideas, I have become more ruthless. I save less. I finish less. I trust skimming more. Not every useful article needs to become a note, and not every note needs to become an insight, and not every insight needs to be operationalised.

This has changed how I think about attention. I no longer see it as something to optimise through tooling. I see it as something to spend. That framing is less glamorous, but more practical. Spending attention implies trade-offs. It makes waste visible. It also makes rest legitimate. You cannot spend continuously without depletion, and no app can negotiate that physics on your behalf.

The irony is that stopping productivity apps has probably made me more productive, but not in the quantified, dashboard-friendly sense. I finish more important things because fewer trivial things get promoted into the system. I feel less compelled to maintain a perfect inventory of obligations. I notice sooner when the issue is not organisation but overcommitment. I have fewer places to look and fewer ways to pretend I am working.

The cost is that the system is not elegant. It does not scale. It does not produce satisfying analytics. It depends on judgement, which is inconvenient and resists automation.

But that is the point. A good tool should support judgement, not replace it. My problem was that I kept outsourcing judgement to systems that could only organise inputs. They could tell me what I had entered, tagged, dated and deferred. They could not tell me what mattered, what to abandon, what to renegotiate, or what kind of day I was actually capable of having.

So I stopped looking for the app that would make me a better version of myself. I needed less infrastructure and more refusal. Less capture and more choice. Fewer lists and a clearer sense of what did not belong on them.

That is harder to package as software. It is also why it works.
