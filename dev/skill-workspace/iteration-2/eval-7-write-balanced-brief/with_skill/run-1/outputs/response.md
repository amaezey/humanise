**Draft**

I expected linocut printing to feel like drawing with a knife, but the first surprise was how much of it happened before the ink came out. I spent nearly an hour deciding which parts of a small pear should stay black and which should be carved away. Every line felt slightly irreversible, because pencil lets you fuss while lino asks for a decision.

The cutting itself was calmer than I expected, at least once I stopped gripping the tool like it was about to escape. The blade made a soft, gritty sound as it lifted curls from the block. I liked the physical evidence of progress gathering beside my hand. I also learned quickly that impatience has a shape: one rushed gouge, too close to the stem, left a pale nick I had to pretend was reflected light.

Printing was the messy part. The ink looked smooth on the slab but behaved differently on the roller, then differently again on the lino. My first pull was patchy, with one over-inked corner and a bald spot through the middle. Still, peeling the paper back had a small ceremony to it. The image was not exactly what I planned, but it had pressure in it, and my mistakes were suddenly part of its surface.

What stayed with me was the slowness. Linocut makes you think backwards, work carefully, and accept that the hand will show. I found that frustrating. I also found it honest, which is why I want to try again.
