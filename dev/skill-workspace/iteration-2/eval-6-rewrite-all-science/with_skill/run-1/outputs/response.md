**Rewrite**

After rain, a forest can pull the eye upward: wet leaves, darkened bark, birds shifting in the canopy. Much of the work, though, is underfoot. Beneath the litter layer, roots run through soil threaded with fungal filaments so fine that a teaspoon of earth can hold a living mesh. Those fungi are part of the forest's metabolism. They help trees feed, recover after stress, compete for resources, and move carbon through the soil.

The relationship is called mycorrhiza, from the Greek for "fungus-root". In a mycorrhizal association, a fungus colonises a plant's root system and grows outward as microscopic filaments called hyphae. The plant pays in carbon-rich compounds made by photosynthesis. The fungus helps the plant reach phosphorus and nitrogen, along with water or trace elements beyond the root's own range. More than 80% of plant species form nutritional symbioses of this kind, and in many soils these fungi make up a large share of the living microbial biomass.

For a tree, that partnership changes the scale of its reach. Roots are costly, bulky structures compared with fungal hyphae. A root hair explores nearby soil; a fungal filament can enter smaller pores and work through leaf litter or mineral soil. The fungus gives the tree a finer absorbing surface, with chemistry the root cannot easily supply on its own.

Ectomycorrhizal fungi, common on many pines, birches, oaks, beeches, and eucalypts, wrap root tips in a sheath and grow between root cells. Arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi enter root cells and form tiny branched structures where exchange takes place. The architecture differs. The trade is similar: plant carbon moves to the fungus, while resources from the soil move back to the plant.

That trade changes with circumstance. Trees do not give sugar equally to every fungus nearby. Fungal partners differ in where they forage and what they can extract. Some are good at scavenging inorganic phosphorus. Others help mobilise nitrogen from organic matter. Reviews of ectomycorrhizal forests stress that nitrogen transfer varies with fungal identity and soil chemistry, as well as the form of nitrogen available. A tree in poor soil may spend heavily on fungal partners. A tree in fertilised soil may spend less because the return on each unit of carbon has changed.

This is where the phrase "wood-wide web" tends to take over. A common mycorrhizal network has a narrower meaning than the metaphor suggests: a physical fungal connection between the roots of two or more plants. Such a connection may move carbon, nutrients, water, or chemical signals between plants. Showing that it does so in an intact forest is hard. Researchers have to rule out root leakage, microbial recycling, dissolved chemicals in water, and airborne cues. A 2023 review in Nature Ecology & Evolution argued that several popular claims about these networks in forests have outrun the field evidence, especially claims about how widespread they are, how often they help seedlings, and whether mature trees preferentially feed offspring.

The networks are real. The stronger lesson is that they are difficult to measure cleanly. A classic 1997 experiment by Suzanne Simard and colleagues used carbon isotopes to trace movement between paper birch and Douglas-fir seedlings in British Columbia. The researchers found carbon moving in both directions between the species, with Douglas-fir gaining more under the conditions studied. The result mattered because it showed that forest plants could be linked through shared fungal systems, rather than behaving only as isolated competitors.

The careful conclusion is smaller than the folklore and more useful. Carbon can move between plants in some experimental settings, and fungal pathways may be involved. A measured transfer by itself does not prove that an old tree is feeding a seedling in any intentional sense, or that the transfer changes survival across forests. Later reviews have found mixed results: access to common networks can help seedlings in some cases, harm them in others, or make little measurable difference. A connected seedling may receive water or nutrients. It may also lose carbon to fungi or face sharper below-ground competition. Forest life is an economy with uneven terms.

Claims about "communication" need the same restraint. Plants detect chemical cues and respond to them. Attacked leaves can alter defensive chemistry. Nearby plants sometimes respond when neighbours are damaged. Some experiments suggest that mycorrhizal networks can carry stress-related signals. One study reported carbon transfer and defence-related signalling from damaged Douglas-fir to neighbouring ponderosa pine through ectomycorrhizal networks. That is still biochemistry rather than speech: changes in one organism can alter the chemical environment of connected organisms, which may then shift their physiology.

The fungus is more than a cable between plants. It is a living partner trying to obtain carbon and reproduce. It may send nutrients toward hosts that supply more carbon, grow into nutrient-rich patches, or reduce exchange when conditions change. Some mycorrhizal relationships are strongly mutualistic. Others tilt toward parasitism when one partner gives more than it receives. A fungus that helps one tree may compete with another for nitrogen. A network that appears to connect plants may also let fungi manage resources across several hosts.

These fungal systems change the soil itself. Hyphae bind particles into aggregates and move nutrients over short distances. When fungal tissue dies, its residues can become part of longer-lived soil organic matter, especially when microbial remains attach to minerals. Mycorrhizal fungi still do many things with carbon once they receive it. They build tissue. They respire carbon dioxide. They interact with decomposer microbes and sometimes stimulate decomposition while mining organic matter for nutrients. Recent forest-carbon research links fungal community composition with soil carbon patterns, but the direction is not simple. Different fungi can push carbon cycling in different ways.

In a nutrient-poor conifer forest, trees may send a large share of photosynthetic carbon below ground because nutrients, rather than light, limit growth. Ectomycorrhizal fungi wrap root tips, extend hyphae through the soil, and use enzymes plus organic acids to reach forms of nitrogen or phosphorus the tree cannot easily access alone. The mushrooms that appear after rain are fruiting bodies. Most of the organism is the mycelium in the roots and soil. When machinery compacts soil, topsoil is stripped away, or clear-felling changes moisture and temperature, the mycelium's living conditions can shift in a day.

Regeneration after disturbance begins in an already occupied underground world. Seedlings germinate among fungi, roots, bacteria, and decaying organic matter. In some places, established mycorrhizal fungi help young trees by colonising their roots quickly. In others, the existing fungal community favours some tree species over others, which changes which seedlings survive. Replanting a forest is therefore more than putting stems in the ground. The underground community helps decide whether those stems become a working forest.

The climate and conservation stakes follow from that. Mycorrhizal fungi are involved in plant productivity, nutrient cycling, and soil carbon dynamics at large scales. A 2025 Nature study mapping global mycorrhizal fungal richness reported extensive underground networks and found that many hotspots for these fungi lie outside protected areas. The policy implication is modest but important: forest protection that ignores below-ground biodiversity is leaving out part of the system.

The useful way to think about mycorrhizal networks is to drop the fairy tale without dropping the biology. They are not magical internet cables sending care packages through the woods. They are also not a debunked fantasy. Mycorrhizal fungi are ancient and ecologically important. They help trees acquire nutrients, alter competition, influence seedling establishment, and change how carbon moves through soil. Sometimes they connect plants in ways that allow matter or signals to pass between them.

The forest under the forest is infrastructure, digestive system, and battleground at once. Its exchanges are chemical rather than sentimental. Its networks are patchy and hard to measure. That makes them less tidy than the "wood-wide web" story, and more interesting. A forest is not just a collection of trunks and crowns. It is a layered community negotiating matter and energy above ground and below it, with fungi doing much of the work where light cannot reach.
