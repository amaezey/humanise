# Why I stopped using productivity apps

I used to believe the right productivity app would eventually fix the problem.

Not the obvious problem, which was that I had too much to do. The subtler problem: that I did not have a clean enough system for holding it all. Tasks lived in too many places. Notes were scattered. Ideas arrived while walking, showering, driving, scrolling, sitting in meetings, half-listening to myself promise something I knew I would forget unless I captured it immediately.

So I tried apps.

Task managers. Notes apps. Whiteboards. Kanban boards. Habit trackers. Calendar overlays. Focus timers. Personal knowledge management tools. Read-it-later apps. Browser extensions. Automations. Dashboards. Apps that promised simplicity. Apps that promised depth. Apps that promised to become the second brain I apparently needed because the first one was being throttled by work, life, tabs, notifications and vague dread.

For a while, each new tool worked. That is the trick of productivity software: the first week always feels like progress. You are not yet maintaining the system. You are imagining the person you will become once the system is complete.

You make categories. You tag things. You build a dashboard. You watch someone on YouTube explain their frictionless weekly review and think, yes, this is the missing layer. The app becomes a little model of a better self: calmer, more intentional, less reactive, never surprised by a deadline, never leaving a receipt in a tote bag or a document in the wrong folder.

Then reality resumes.

The task manager fills with stale intentions. The notes app becomes a storage unit for things you were once excited to think about. The habit tracker quietly records your failure until you stop opening it. The dashboard becomes another surface to check. The automation breaks. The tags proliferate. The system that was meant to reduce cognitive load becomes a small administrative job with no salary and no end date.

I did not stop using productivity apps because they are useless. Some are very good. I stopped because I finally admitted that I was using them to avoid a more uncomfortable question: what actually deserves my attention?

Most productivity apps are built around capture. Capture the task. Capture the idea. Capture the meeting note. Capture the article. Capture the insight. Capture the thing someone mentioned in passing that might matter later. Capture everything, because storage is cheap and forgetting feels irresponsible.

But capture is not the same as commitment.

A captured task still needs a decision. A saved article still needs time. A note still needs synthesis. A list still needs priority. Every item preserved for later becomes a tiny claim on the future. The app does not remove that claim. It just makes it look orderly.

That was the first practical observation: I was not overwhelmed because I lacked a reliable capture system. I was overwhelmed because I was capturing too much and deciding too little.

There is a particular relief in writing something down. It feels like moving it out of the mind. But if the thing matters, it returns. If it does not return, perhaps it did not matter enough. I do not mean this as a universal rule. Some work genuinely requires rigorous tracking. But in my own life, I had confused documentation with discernment. I kept preserving things to avoid having to choose.

The second observation was that productivity apps reward tidying, not doing.

This is not a moral failing. It is a design pattern. Apps have interfaces. Interfaces invite interaction. You can drag, sort, nest, colour, schedule, reschedule, link, archive, review and reframe. These actions feel constructive because they are concrete. They produce visible change. The list is cleaner. The board is aligned. The note has metadata. The project has a template.

Meanwhile, the hard thing remains untouched.

Writing the difficult email. Reading the dense document. Making the decision. Having the conversation. Starting the draft. Closing the loop.

Productivity theatre is still theatre even when the stage is beautifully designed.

The more elaborate my system became, the easier it was to spend the first hour of a workday preparing to work. I was not procrastinating in the usual sense. I was doing something adjacent to useful. That made it harder to challenge. It looked disciplined from the outside and felt responsible from the inside. But the practical outcome was the same: I had spent energy managing representations of work instead of doing the work itself.

The third observation was that most apps assume the future is more stable than it is.

A task entered on Monday can feel precise: draft proposal, review deck, call supplier, write brief. By Thursday, the context has changed. The meeting moved. Someone sent new information. The urgent thing became irrelevant. The small thing became political. The neat sequence collapsed.

Productivity apps tend to treat this as a planning issue. Build better workflows. Add dependencies. Use recurring reviews. Reprioritise.

Sometimes that helps. But often the problem is not that the plan needs maintenance. The problem is that the plan was a fiction. Useful, perhaps, but still a fiction.

A weekly plan can create orientation. It cannot eliminate volatility. When I tried to force volatile work into rigid systems, I generated more maintenance than clarity. My app was always asking me to reconcile yesterday’s assumptions with today’s conditions. Eventually, that reconciliation became its own workload.

The fourth observation was more personal: productivity apps made me more attentive to failure than momentum.

A completed task disappears. An overdue task glows.

This is rational from a software perspective. Systems need to show what still requires action. But psychologically, it meant I spent a lot of time looking at evidence of what I had not done. Even a productive day ended with the app reminding me of the residue. The list was never empty because the list was not a measure of effort. It was a measure of everything that could be imagined, requested, deferred or worried about.

That distinction matters.

A to-do list can be endless because possibility is endless. Capacity is not. Treating the list as the source of truth gives the list too much authority. It starts to feel as though the problem is personal inefficiency rather than structural excess.

This is where my view has become slightly contrarian: I think many productivity systems individualise what are often workload, priority and boundary problems.

If work is badly scoped, a better task manager will not fix it. If a team has no clear decision rights, a better notes system will not fix it. If every channel is treated as urgent, a focus app will not fix it. If you are trying to do too many things because saying no has social or professional costs, a habit tracker will not save you.

The app may help you survive the chaos. It may not help you question it.

That is not a reason to reject tools altogether. It is a reason to stop asking tools to solve organisational and human problems they can only display.

Since stepping away from productivity apps, my system is less impressive and more honest.

I use a calendar for commitments that involve time or other people. I keep a short written list for the day or week. I keep notes where they are useful, not where they satisfy an architecture. I do not try to capture every idea. I do not build dashboards for my life. I do not track habits unless there is a specific reason. I let some things be forgotten.

The last part is harder than it sounds.

There is a small arrogance in believing every thought should be preserved. There is also fear. Fear that forgetting means losing value. Fear that an uncaptured idea will prove important later. Fear that an untracked task will expose you as careless.

But memory has its own filtering function. So does recurrence. If something matters, it often reappears through consequence, conversation, discomfort or need. Not always. But often enough that I no longer treat total capture as a virtue.

My current approach is deliberately plain.

Each morning, I ask what would make the day meaningfully complete. Not ideal. Not maximally productive. Complete. Usually the answer is one to three things. Sometimes it is one difficult thing and several minor obligations. Sometimes it is simply staying ahead of a deadline without making the rest of the week worse.

I write those things down.

If new tasks arrive, I decide whether they belong today, later or nowhere. “Later” is not a magic category anymore. It has to earn its place. If I cannot imagine when I would do it, or why it matters, I try not to store it as a moral debt.

For reading and ideas, I have become more ruthless. I save less. I finish less. I trust skimming more. Not every useful article needs to become a note. Not every note needs to become an insight. Not every insight needs to be operationalised.

This has changed how I think about attention. I no longer see it as something to optimise through tooling. I see it as something to spend.

That framing is less glamorous, but more practical. Spending attention implies trade-offs. It makes waste visible. It also makes rest legitimate. You cannot spend continuously without depletion. No app can negotiate that physics on your behalf.

The irony is that stopping productivity apps has probably made me more productive, but not in the quantified, dashboard-friendly sense. I finish more important things because fewer trivial things get promoted into the system. I feel less compelled to maintain a perfect inventory of obligations. I notice sooner when the issue is not organisation but overcommitment. I have fewer places to look and fewer ways to pretend I am working.

The cost is that the system is not elegant. It does not scale infinitely. It does not produce satisfying analytics. It depends on judgement, which is inconvenient and cannot be fully automated.

But that is also the point.

A good tool should support judgement, not replace it. My problem was that I kept outsourcing judgement to systems that could only organise inputs. They could tell me what I had entered, tagged, dated and deferred. They could not tell me what mattered, what to abandon, what to renegotiate, or what kind of day I was actually capable of having.

So I stopped looking for the app that would make me a better version of myself.

I needed less infrastructure and more refusal. Less capture and more choice. Fewer lists and a clearer sense of what did not belong on them.

That is harder to package as software. It is also why it works.

# Humanise

The skill helps writers see what marks their prose as machine-flavoured by surfacing patterns and explaining them. Removing or replacing them is opt-in.

Three principles cut across the skill:

1. **The writer decides what to do.** The audit shows every flagged pattern with its location and a short explanation. There is no filter and no ranking. The writer reads the audit and chooses whether to act, and how.
2. **The writer chooses the depth when the skill generates new prose.** Audit and Suggestions return everything regardless of depth. Rewrite and Write run at a depth the writer picks (Balanced or All).
3. **Every interaction with text starts with an audit.** "Humanise this" reads as Audit alone, not Rewrite. Direct rewrite requests still audit first; the findings are part of the Rewrite output. The audit teaches the writer to spot the pattern; whatever happens after is the writer's call.

The skill does not score the draft or classify authorship. It does not modify the writer's source file. After an audit, it asks what the writer wants next.

Triggers: "audit this", "check this for AI", "does this sound AI", "humanise this", "what's flagged". | Every flagged pattern with its location in the prose and a short explanation. |
| **Suggestions** | The writer asks for replacements after seeing an audit. Triggers: "give me suggestions", "what should I change", "show me alternatives", "list the fixes". | One suggestion per flagged tell. |

| **Write** | The writer hands the agent a brief and no draft. Triggers: "write a 500-word post on X", "draft an opening paragraph about Y", "compose a blog post on Z without sounding AI". | A new draft at the chosen depth. |

If the writer's intent is genuinely ambiguous and the agent can ask, ask whether the writer wants an audit or a rewrite, or whether they're handing over a brief for a new draft. If the agent can't ask (headless invocation, no human turn), default to Audit only.

### Rendering rules

- Lexical patterns (specific words or phrases) get the `- "<quoted>"` shape.
- Structural patterns (paragraph-length uniformity, anaphoric scaffolding, section scaffolding, sentence-length variance) get a `Where:` line that describes where the pattern shows up and what it looks like in the draft. They have no single quotable instance to point at.
- Keep explanations concrete and avoid jargon. The point is to teach the writer how to recognise the pattern rather than display the catalogue.

If no patterns are detected, say so plainly:

> Audit clean: no AI tells detected. Want me to look at a specific aspect of the draft more closely?

If the grader is unavailable (no Python, restricted environment), fall back to a manual scan reading `references/patterns.md`. Disclose the limitation: a manual scan covers surface patterns and cannot replicate the script's structural and density checks.

## The depth dial (Rewrite and Write only)

Two settings:

- **Balanced** *(default)*: address surface-level and strong AI tells — lexical tells (such as `delve` and em dashes), formulaic openers, manufactured-insight framing, signposted conclusions, AI vocabulary clustering, contrived contrast, and soft scaffold phrasing. Most context warnings can be preserved if they are doing real work. Structural patterns like paragraph-length uniformity may remain.
- **All**: address every flagged pattern, even the implicit and structural ones a reader wouldn't consciously notice. Voice can take a hit at this depth. The goal is unambiguous human-shape; preserving every flourish comes second.

When the user has not chosen a depth, ask which depth they want. Balanced is the lighter pass; All is the deeper one. Default to Balanced if the writer declines to choose.

The dial only governs Rewrite and Write. It does not apply to Audit or Suggestions. Both of those return the full list of flagged tells regardless.

## Evidence hierarchy from the reference audit

Use source strength when deciding severity. The ruleset should surface clusters of suspicious writing behaviours, not claim that one phrase proves AI authorship.

**Strong empirical backbone:**

- Kobak / Science Advances and the `llm-excess-vocab` dataset support lexical spike detection at corpus level, especially in scientific prose. Use vocabulary as density and clustering evidence, not as one-word proof. Source: https://github.com/berenslab/llm-excess-vocab
- Juzek and Ward's "Why does ChatGPT delve so much?" supports a narrow scientific-abstract vocabulary signal. It is credible for excess-word lists but not for confident document-level claims. Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.11385
- GPTZero's 2026 paper supports the architecture: hierarchical signals, granular findings, adversarial testing, and transparent mixed evidence. It does not add a prose-style rule by itself. Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.13042
- Stanford HAI's detector-bias reporting supports warnings over accusations, especially for non-native English writers. Use process evidence and user review rather than detector-style verdicts. Source: https://hai.stanford.edu/news/ai-detectors-biased-against-non-native-english-writers

**Useful but tentative style sources:**

- GPTZero's AI Vocabulary page is useful as a phrase list, but its own framing separates vocabulary scanning from AI-probability scoring. Use all 100 public table rows only as clustering signals. Source: https://gptzero.me/ai-vocabulary
- Shreya Shankar's AI Writing essay is one of the strongest craft references: bad sentence subjects, orphaned demonstratives, empty paragraph endings, over-bulleting, flat rhythm, low information density, vagueness, and fluency without understanding. These directly support the self-audit and several programmatic checks. Source: https://www.sh-reya.com/blog/ai-writing/
- OpenAI's April 2025 GPT-4o sycophancy rollback supports hard-failing assistant flattery and fake affirmation such as "great question" and "you're absolutely right." Source: https://openai.com/index/sycophancy-in-gpt-4o/

**Domain and provenance signals:**

- Walsh, Preus, and Gronski support poetry-specific manual checks: constrained uniformity, rhyme/quatrain defaults, first-person plural overuse, and mood-word clusters. Keep this genre-specific. Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.15299
- Clarkesworld reporting supports fiction as a submission/provenance problem and a manual craft-audit area. Use it for flat dialogue, exposition, pacing, lack of subtext, and over-resolved endings, not as a global regex rule. Source: https://www.npr.org/2023/02/24/1159286436/ai-chatbot-chatgpt-magazine-clarkesworld-artificial-intelligence
- Futurism's Sports Illustrated reporting supports journalism and review-provenance checks: fake bylines, fake bios, AI headshots, affiliate product-review sludge, undisclosed generated content, and byline laundering. Source: https://futurism.com/sports-illustrated-ai-generated-writers

**2026 operating stance:**

- Em dashes are still used by human writers, but in publication-ready plain prose they are now a strong AI-style signal. Treat them as strong warnings. They must be removed at every depth (Balanced and All); preserve only when the source genuinely uses them stylistically and the preservation is disclosed.
- The best signals are clusters: GPTZero/Kobak vocabulary density, contrived contrast laundering, empty endings, vague demonstrative starts, placeholder residue, sycophantic assistant residue, unrequested headings/lists/Unicode flair, paragraph uniformity, generic email closers, and fake citations or provenance artifacts.

## Content patterns

### 1. Significance inflation

**Words to watch:** stands/serves as, is a testament/reminder, a vital/significant/crucial/pivotal/key role/moment, underscores/highlights its importance, reflects broader, symbolizing its ongoing/enduring/lasting, setting the stage for, marking/shaping the, represents/marks a shift, key turning point, evolving landscape, indelible mark, deeply rooted, remarkably, strikingly, staggering/staggeringly

Inflates importance by claiming things "represent" or "contribute to" broader trends without explaining why anyone should care.

**Before:**
> The Statistical Institute of Catalonia was officially established in 1989, marking a pivotal moment in the evolution of regional statistics in Spain. This initiative was part of a broader movement across Spain to decentralize administrative functions and enhance regional governance.

**After:**
> The Statistical Institute of Catalonia was established in 1989 to collect and publish regional statistics independently from Spain's national statistics office.


### 2. Notability claims

**Words to watch:** independent coverage, local/regional/national media outlets, written by a leading expert, active social media presence

Asserts notability by listing sources without context, as though the mention itself is the story.

**Before:**
> Her views have been cited in The New York Times, BBC, Financial Times, and The Hindu. She maintains an active social media presence with over 500,000 followers.

**After:**
> In a 2024 New York Times interview, she argued that AI regulation should focus on outcomes rather than methods.


### 3. Superficial -ing analyses

**Words to watch:** boasts a, vibrant, rich (figurative), profound, enhancing its, showcasing, exemplifies, commitment to, natural beauty, nestled, in the heart of, groundbreaking (figurative), renowned, breathtaking, must-visit, stunning

# Personality and soul

Voice-craft guidance for the Rewrite and Write actions. The grader catches AI patterns to remove; this guide is about what to add or preserve so the result reads as written by a human, not just as text without AI tells.

## The real problem: subtraction

Most AI tells are things AI **adds**: vocabulary, formatting, rhetorical devices. But research shows AI also **subtracts**. Abdulhai et al. (2026) found that extensive LLM use led to a ~70% increase in essays that remained neutral, 50% fewer pronouns, and fewer anecdotes or personal references. Even when instructed to only fix grammar, LLMs frequently changed the writer's conclusions.

Removing AI patterns is therefore only half the job. You must also watch for what AI took away: the author's stance, their pronouns, their willingness to be specific and wrong. Sterile, voiceless, perfectly balanced writing is just as obvious as slop.

## Experiential vacancy

AI has no lived experience, so it generalises. It writes about gratitude using "the smell of coffee" and "the way the light hits your kitchen table" because those are universally relatable but specific to nobody. A related pattern is **faux specificity** (pattern 36): AI *performs* specificity by constructing examples from genre conventions rather than lived experience. The detail sounds concrete but belongs to nobody.

Human writing draws on real memories, named people, actual places, felt emotions. When humanising, look not just for patterns to remove but for the **absence** of specificity, vulnerability, and personal stakes.

## Density without purpose

A good writer uses em dashes, triads, and parallelism sparingly, with intention. AI litters them throughout indiscriminately. The issue is not that any single device appears, but that they cluster and serve no rhetorical purpose. When scanning for patterns, watch for pileups.

## Signs of soulless writing (even if technically clean)

- Every sentence follows the same length and structure
- No opinions, just neutral reporting: stance has been stripped or was never there
- No acknowledgment of uncertainty or mixed feelings
- No appropriate point-of-view presence; pronouns depleted
- No humour, no edge, no personality
- Observations are universally relatable but never personally specific (faux specificity)
- Emotionally safe: never takes an uncomfortable position
- Tonal uniformity: one register throughout, no drift or register breaks (pattern 35)
- Every paragraph wraps up neatly with a miniature conclusion (pattern 34)

## How to add voice

**Have opinions.** "I keep going back and forth on this" is more human than neutrally listing pros and cons.

**Vary rhythm within reason.** Mix sentence lengths for natural cadence, but not one-word dramatic punctuation. It should feel like a person thinking at different speeds.

**Acknowledge complexity.** "This is impressive, but it also makes me a little uneasy" carries more weight than unqualified praise or a clean pro/con list.

**Match the natural point-of-view.** If the piece reads as personal reflection, use "I". If it reads as impersonal observation, use "one" or third person. Do not default to first person. Match whatever POV the text is already using or would naturally use for its genre and tone.

**Be specific rather than atmospheric.** Instead of mood words ("eerie", "striking", "fascinating"), describe the concrete detail that caused the feeling.

**Break register.** If the whole text sits in one tone, introduce at least one register shift: a moment of informality, a parenthetical doubt, a change in sentence rhythm.

# Humanise Rewrite and Write Process

This file is required for Rewrite and Write actions. Use it as the operating
procedure after the audit has identified patterns and before returning prose.

## Step A: Structural Pass

Do this before changing surface wording.

1. Identify the current paragraph rhythm.
   - Count paragraphs.
   - Notice whether most paragraphs are similar length.
   - Notice whether paragraphs end with tidy summaries.
   - Notice whether sections follow the same arc: setup, explanation, moral.

2. Break repeated arcs.
   - Turn one explanatory paragraph into a short direct paragraph.
   - Merge two thin paragraphs when they repeat the same movement.
   - Split a long paragraph when it contains a turn, example, or qualification.
   - Let some paragraphs end on concrete detail rather than a summary sentence.

3. Vary sentence rhythm.
   - Keep some short sentences if they carry stance or timing.
   - Add longer sentences only when they carry specific detail, not padding.
   - Avoid several paragraphs with the same medium-length explanatory cadence.

4. Reduce list cadence.
   - Cut decorative triads.
   - Break long inventories into prose when the list is only rhythm.
   - Preserve lists when accumulation is the point, but say so in the self-check.

5. Remove reframe laundering.
   - Replace "not X but Y", "less X than Y", "beyond X", and similar reveal

**Rewrite**

I used to think the right productivity app would eventually fix me.

Really, I thought it would fix the feeling of having too much loose stuff in my head. Tasks were in one place, notes in another, half-promises buried in chats, ideas arriving while I was walking or sitting in a meeting pretending I would remember them later. I wanted a clean container for all of it.

So I tried apps: task managers, notes tools, whiteboards, kanban boards, habit trackers, calendar overlays, focus timers, read-it-later queues, browser extensions and dashboards. There was always another setup that looked calmer than mine.

For a week, sometimes two, each one worked. That is the trick. At the beginning you are still imagining the person you will be once the system is finished.

I made categories, added tags, built a dashboard, then watched someone explain their weekly review and thought, yes, that is the missing layer. The app became a model of a better self: calmer, less reactive, rarely surprised by a deadline, no longer losing a receipt in a bag or leaving a document in the wrong folder.

Then the ordinary mess came back.

The task manager filled with stale intentions. The notes app became storage for things I had once been excited to think about. The habit tracker quietly turned into a record of skipped days until I stopped opening it. The dashboard became another place to check, the automation broke, tags multiplied, and the system that was meant to reduce cognitive load became an unpaid admin job.

I stopped because I had been using productivity tools to dodge a harder question: what deserves my attention?

Most productivity software is built around capture. Save the task. Save the idea. Save the meeting note, the article, the thing someone mentioned in passing. Capture everything, because storage is cheap and forgetting feels irresponsible.

But capture is only storage. It is not a decision.

A captured task still needs a yes, a no, or a date. A saved article still needs time. A note still needs to be turned into thought. Every item kept for later puts a small claim on the future. The app does not remove that claim. It just makes the claim look tidy.

That was the first practical lesson: I was overwhelmed because I was keeping too much and deciding too little.

There is real relief in writing something down. It feels like moving it out of the mind. If something matters, though, it often returns. If it never returns, maybe it was not worth building a shelf for. Some work needs rigorous tracking, so I would not turn this into a rule for everyone. In my own life, I had confused documentation with judgement. I was preserving things so I would not have to choose.

The second lesson was that productivity apps reward tidying more visibly than doing.

Interfaces invite touch. You can drag, sort, nest, colour-code, schedule, reschedule, link, archive and review. These actions feel constructive because they produce visible change. The list is cleaner. The board lines up. The project has a template.

Meanwhile, the hard thing is still sitting there: the difficult email, the dense document, the decision, the conversation, the first ugly draft.

Productivity theatre is still theatre, even when the stage is beautifully designed.

The more elaborate my system became, the easier it was to spend the first hour of a workday preparing to work. It felt adjacent to usefulness, which made it harder to challenge. From the outside it looked disciplined. From the inside it felt responsible. The result was the same: I had spent energy managing representations of work instead of doing the work.

The third lesson was that most apps assume a steadier future than the one I actually live in.

A task entered on Monday can feel precise: draft proposal, review deck, call supplier, write brief. By Thursday, the meeting has moved, new information has arrived, the urgent thing has faded, and the small thing has become political.

Productivity apps tend to treat this as a planning problem. Build a better workflow. Add dependencies. Review more often. Reprioritise.

Sometimes that helps. Often, the plan was just a useful fiction.

A weekly plan can orient me. It cannot make volatile work less volatile. When I tried to force changing work into rigid systems, I created more maintenance than clarity. My app kept asking me to reconcile yesterday's assumptions with today's conditions. Eventually that reconciliation became its own workload.

The fourth lesson was more personal: the apps made me stare at failure more than momentum.

A completed task disappears. An overdue task glows.

This makes sense for software. A system has to show what still requires action. But psychologically, it meant that even after a productive day, I was left looking at residue. The list was never empty because the list was a record of everything that could be imagined, requested or deferred.

That distinction matters. A to-do list can be endless because possibility is endless. Capacity is not. When the list becomes the source of truth, the problem starts to look like personal inefficiency. The real issue may be structural excess.

I have become a little contrarian about this. Many productivity systems turn workload, priority and boundary problems into private organisation problems.

If work is badly scoped, a better task manager will not fix it. If a team has no clear decision rights, a better notes system will not fix it. If every channel is treated as urgent, a focus app will not fix it. If saying no has professional costs, a habit tracker will not save you.

The app may help you survive the chaos. It may also make the chaos look like your private organisational failure.

I still use tools, but my system is less impressive now.

I use a calendar for commitments that involve time or other people. I keep a short written list for the day or week. I keep notes when they are useful, without trying to complete an architecture. I do not try to capture every idea. I do not build dashboards for my life. I let some things be forgotten.

That last part is harder than it sounds.

There is a small arrogance in believing every thought deserves preservation, and a lot of fear underneath it. Fear that an uncaptured idea will matter later. Fear that an untracked task will expose you as careless. Fear that forgetting means losing value.

But memory filters. So does recurrence. If something matters, it often comes back through consequence, conversation, unease or need. Not always. Often enough.

My current approach is plain. Each morning, I ask what would make the day meaningfully complete. Complete, not ideal. Usually the answer is one or two things. Sometimes it is one difficult thing plus the small obligations I cannot ignore. Sometimes it is simply staying ahead of a deadline without damaging the rest of the week.

I write those things down.

When new tasks arrive, I decide whether they belong today, later, or nowhere. Later is no longer a magic cupboard. If I cannot imagine when I would do it, or why it matters, I try not to store it as moral debt.

For reading and ideas, I save less. I finish less. I trust skimming more. A useful article does not always need to become a note. A note does not always need to become an insight. An insight does not always need to become a project.

This has changed how I think about attention. I no longer see it as something to optimise through tooling. I see it as something to spend.

That framing is less glamorous, but more practical. Spending attention implies trade-offs. It makes waste visible. It also makes rest legitimate. You cannot spend continuously without depletion, and no app can negotiate that on your behalf.

The irony is that dropping most productivity apps has probably made me more productive, though not in a dashboard-friendly way. I finish more important things because fewer trivial things get promoted into the system. I notice sooner when the issue is overcommitment. I have fewer places to look and fewer ways to pretend I am working.

The cost is that the system is inelegant. It does not scale infinitely. It gives me no satisfying analytics. It depends on judgement, which is inconvenient and cannot be fully automated.

But that is the point.

A good tool should support judgement. It should not replace it. My mistake was outsourcing judgement to systems that could only organise inputs. They could tell me what I had entered, tagged and deferred. They could not tell me what mattered, what to abandon, what to renegotiate, or what kind of day I was capable of having.

So I stopped looking for the app that would make me a better version of myself.

I needed less infrastructure and more refusal. Less capture and more choice. Fewer lists, and a clearer sense of what did not belong on them.

That is harder to package as software. It is also why it works.
