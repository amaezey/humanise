## Summary
Craft essay arguing that "AI slop" is not bad simply because AI made it, and human writing is not good simply because a human made it. Caroll's concrete distinction is that AI can produce readable, relatable, warm prose, but it lacks lived experience and therefore defaults to generic writing unless a human adds specific personal material.

## Main insights
- Strong craft support for #36 faux specificity and human-specific lived-experience checks.
- Useful false-positive caution: humans also produce slop, clichés, lectures, excess words, and moralizing.
- The essay is not empirical evidence for AI detection. It is best used for agent-judgement prompts about generic affect, lived specificity, and reader takeaway.

## Evidence and claims to extract
- Caroll says AI's real problem is that it is generic and lacks unique human perspective because a machine has no stories to tell.
- She gives a ChatGPT gratitude example that is simple, warm, relatable, and likely to receive approval, then contrasts it with a human example rooted in a specific family experience.
- She argues AI succeeds partly because it uses simple vocabulary, simple sentence structure, and relatable emotional framing.
- She says human writing can also be bad: lecturing, clichés, excess word count, tropes, assumptions, and rambling.
- She concludes the value of a piece of writing comes from what the reader can take away from it, not from whether a human or AI produced it.
