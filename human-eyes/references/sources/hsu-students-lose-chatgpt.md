## Summary
WNYC interview with Hua Hsu about what students lose when they outsource essay writing to ChatGPT and similar tools. The transcript is primarily about writing as thinking, education, attachment to one's own work, and the risk that AI shortcuts remove productive difficulty. It is not a source of surface prose tells.

## Main insights
- Useful for stance/voice-loss framing.
- Supports rewrite-process safeguards and the principle that writing is a way of discovering thought.
- Hsu says AI use among college students appears widespread, but he does not offer firm statistics.
- The interview gives concrete process concerns: students outsourcing unwanted work, retaining little, losing accidental learning, and seeing themselves as project managers/collaborators rather than attached authors.
- Best used as pedagogical/process context, not direct evidence for a visible neutrality-collapse pattern.

## Evidence and claims to extract
- Local clipping includes transcript and metadata for the Brian Lehrer Show segment.
- Lehrer frames writing difficulty as part of how students work something out and experience "eureka" moments.
- Hsu says student use seems widespread from talking to students, while acknowledging the lack of firm statistics.
- Hsu describes a student using Claude and ChatGPT for an estimated 90% of the work in a class he did not care about and retaining very little.
- Hsu says the college process is being short-circuited: pockets of potential magic are going away.
- The discussion emphasizes education as producing people/citizens, not only workers, and treating AI as a shortcut around difficult thought.
- Caller examples show a related risk on the grading side: AI feedback can make writing feel like busywork when no human reads it.
