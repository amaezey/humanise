The Reluctant Dragon, Maxfield Parrish, illustration, 1902

I read a post this morning by a man who said he came to eat his words out loud, in public. He said one year ago, he wrote a post saying AI will not kill writing because companies will always pay for good quality writing. It got a ton of views.

He said a year ago, he assumed companies will always want good writing. Except, he just lost his job to AI so he’s quitting writing and going back to programming. He said he was wrong. Companies don’t want good writing, they’re fine with slop.

This isn’t about AI. It’s about calling it slop, and it’s about human writing.

Slop was Merriam Webster’s [word of the year](https://www.merriam-webster.com/wordplay/word-of-the-year) last year.

Every time they pick a word of the year, they explain why they picked it. So they said “_amid all the talk about AI threats, slop set a tone that’s less fearful, more mocking._”

That’s it, isn’t it? That’s how we use the word. To mock AI.

But it’s a little deeper. It’s about mocking the people using it, too. Isn’t it? Like, we’re the “real” writers. And they’re just using AI. Churning out slop.

So we can roll our eyes at them and get on with our human writing.

Merriam Webster went on to say in the 1700s, slop used to mean soft mud. Then it meant food waste, like pig slop. And today it’s a generalization for rubbish. For something that’s not very good or has little or no value.

And then—the final blow.

They said when we use the word “slop” it’s like sending a little message to Ai that when it comes to replacing human creativity, it’s not that intelligent. Not good.

When I read that, I thought wow, don’t we just love our moral high ground.

The problem with calling AI slop is that it creates a subconscious divide that says AI is bad, and therefore human is good. But the truth is, a lot of human writing isn’t very good. And I’m sorry to say that, but it’s true. Every human writer has pieces they knocked out of the park and pieces that suck. The ratio is the only issue.

A skilled writer with years of experience might write 8 good pieces for every 2 that suck. A writer who hasn’t worked on their skillset is likely the other way around.

I have been that person with eight bad for every two good. Every writer has.

Know what the real problem with AI is?

It’s generic. It lacks unique human perspective.

A machine has no stories to tell, so it has to generalize. How ironic, considering that AI has a belly full of stories stolen from other writers. And yet, even with a belly full of peoples’ stories, it lacks what every writer has. One person’s lived experience.

Let me show you an example. I logged into ChatGPT and typed in a prompt. The prompt was “write up to 200 words on gratitude.”

Here’s what it wrote:

> _Sometimes gratitude arrives on a regular day, when nothing is happening. Like noticing how the light hits your kitchen table in the morning. Or the way your coffee smells before you even take a sip and savoring it. Like hearing someone say your name and realizing how much you like the way it sounds in their voice._
> 
> 
> _Maybe that’s all gratitude needs to be. Just paying attention. Letting yourself feel the small, ordinary pieces of life. And for a few seconds, letting yourself be glad for them. Sometimes, the best kind of gratitude arrives when you aren’t even looking for a reason to be grateful. —written by ChatGPT_

Huh!

If I posted that on Notes, we all know it would get a ton of hearts, right?

Do you know why it would get so many hearts?

There are three reasons.

See if you can guess them.

I’ll tell you in a bit, okay? Write your guesses down. Three reasons.

Now let’s talk about human writing.

If I asked a hundred people to write me a short excerpt on gratitude, a good chunk, maybe close to half, would write it using the word “you.” As in “you should” — like they’re preaching to the world about how we should collectively be.

It’s so common, even ChatGPT did that. Except there’s a difference in how humans do it. Humans use it more like pointing the finger. Lecturing. I see that a lot on Medium.

They write things like if you are unhappy, you need to realize. If you aren’t grateful, you should blah, blah, something… Which is ironic, because it’s just more moral high ground. ChatGPT gets away with it because it’s writing warm fuzzies not lectures.

I write back to those writers and say don’t lecture. Tell me your story. Some re-write. Others get huffy and withdraw the submission. Go publish it as-is elsewhere. They like their lecture. Don’t we just love to tell other people what they’re doing wrong?

Another chunk would use some kind of trope, like saying there are people who are worse off, or we should be grateful because it’s how god wants us to be or some other clichéd thinking. Problem is, cliché is the opposite of original thought.

Ninety of a hundred would use more words than they need.

I run four publications on Medium and I see that all the time, too. Human writers like to stretch out our story. I’d say I don’t know why, but I do. It’s just a stage writers go through. I suspect we learned that in school when we had to write to a wordcount.

I always think of the Dr. Seuss quote — “_the writer who breeds more words than he needs, is making a chore for the reader who reads._”

When William Zinsser taught writing at Yale, one of the first assignments he’d give his students was to write an essay on some topic. When they turned in the essay, he’d tell them to take it home and cut it in half without losing any meaning. He said it never failed, they’d complain, say it can’t be done and he’d say yes it can, go do it.

He’d tell them it’s hard. Absolutely. Because you have to examine every paragraph, every sentence. Ask yourself—can I say this shorter? Can I be clearer? Where am I repeating myself? He said the finished piece would always be so much stronger.

Did you guess three reasons people respond so favorably to AI?

1.   It uses a simple vocabulary. 

ChatGPT writes at a 5th to 6th grade comprehension unless you ask it to write at a higher level. That’s not a bad thing. Hemingway wrote at 5th grade level. He wanted his writing accessible to all audiences. Today, 54% of American read at or below 6th grade level. That doesn’t mean they don’t like to read. It just means they skip anything that’s hard to read or confusing. It’s why they like AI.

2.   It uses simple sentence structure. 

I read a study were college students were asked to read writing from a century ago. The vocabulary was simple, but they still couldn’t follow. Know why? Older writing uses complex sentences. More than one action per sentence and lots of commas and semi-colons. One “action” per sentence is easier to follow. Because easy to read isn’t just simple words. It’s simple sentence structure, too.

3.   They can relate. People will read along and think “omg! me, too” and if there’s one thing humans love, it’s to identify with something they read.

The third one is the win — but also the lose.

It’s the win because people can identify with it. But it’s also the lose because AI is a machine with no human stories to tell. It can only ever be generic, and anything that’s not generic has to be added in manually. Usually, manual additions break the flow.

To write something that’s simple to read, deeply personal, and yet evokes feeling in the reader is really hard.

But when it’s well done? It kicks AI to the curb.

On Sunday I posted on Notes and said want to play a game? I posed the exact same prompt I gave ChatGPT. Write up to 200 words on gratitude.

Here’s [a snippet](https://substack.com/profile/215936985-alice-wild/note/c-208640983) from [Alice Wild](https://substack.com/@alicewild)

> _Melody Beattie said, “Gratitude unlocks the fullness of life,” but I don’t believe that._
> 
> 
> _Maybe it’s because I grew up in a home where gratitude meant something else. Maybe it’s because my dad used to rattle off judgment and self-righteousness faster than the preacher that Sunday, but then always told me to “have some gratitude.”_
> 
> 
> _Stay quiet. That’s what he really meant. A thorough resection of my identity. Except for a small trembling flame left in that hollow place..._

Alice writes [Wildflowers Grow](https://alicewild.substack.com/), a healing journey for women.

Know what she said?

She said she was nervous sharing that.

I wanted to shout from the treetops because ChatGPT can never share a story like that. It doesn’t have any. It doesn’t have a dad. No one ever tried to silence it. Because the writing is easy to read and deeply personal and yet I feel seen.

Who hasn’t felt silenced?

That’s only part of her entry, but it was enough to illustrate what I’m trying to say.

If we’re going to call AI “slop” and if “slop” means “not that good,” then it’s on us to write stuff that’s not slop. We don’t get a free pass because we’re human.

When I say that, I’m not trying to be a judgy piece of crap. I’ve written more than my share of slop. I’ve leaned into tropes, and made assumptions and wagged the finger and rambled and done everything AI doesn’t because it’s not human so it can’t.

The value of a piece of writing doesn’t come from the fact that it came from a human. The value comes from the reader being able to take something away from it.

In 1902, Maxfield Parrish drew an illustration for a story called [The Reluctant Dragon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Reluctant_Dragon_(short_story)).

It’s a sweet little story about a boy who discovered a dragon who loved words and just wanted to write poetry. The boy makes friends with the dragon but soon enough, the people of the kingdom discover the dragon. Of course, all the people knew dragons are bad, so they quickly sent for St. George to come kill the dragon.

But the little boy ran to meet St. George first and told him all about the dragon.

St. George and the dragon made a plan. They’d have a fake fight, and when the fight was announced, the people came to watch. St. George sliced the dragon through a fold of skin that wouldn’t harm him, just as they’d planned. Then St George said the dragon had been changed by his victory and he was allowed to live in peace.

Sometimes, the hardest part of life is identifying the dragon. Sometimes, the hardest part of writing is exactly the same. As always, love to know what you think…
