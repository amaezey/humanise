# AI detection for peer reviewers: Look out for red flags

![Image 4: Moazzma Arshad](https://www.sagepub.com/global/styles/images/avatar.png)

[Alex Moersen](https://www.sagepub.com/explore-our-content/blogs/blog-authors/alex-moersen)

 June 11, 2025 

 Read Time - 9 minutes 

Detecting irresponsible use of AI in research articles can be tricky, so here are some red flags to look out for when peer reviewing a manuscript.

## Peer Review, Author support

* * *

Generative artificial intelligence (AI) usage is becoming more prevalent in the landscape of academic publishing. [AI can be a powerful tool for authors during the research and writing process and when it’s use is properly disclosed](https://www.sagepub.com/about/policies/ai-author-guidelines). However, the technology also poses challenges, particularly when it comes to detecting undisclosed AI-generated content.

As peer reviewers and journal editors, it's crucial to be aware of the signs that might indicate a manuscript has been generated by AI. Whilst authors are allowed to use AI, irresponsible and/or untransparent usage should be noted. As you review submitted manuscripts, there are a number of red flags you can look out for.

## **Writing Style Red Flags**

AI writing often has a distinct "texture" or pattern that can be a giveaway. Here are some common signs:

**Repetitive Phrasing or Over-Explanation**: AI tends to restate things, summarize what it's just said, or over-explain obvious points. For example, "It is important to note that peer review is important because it ensures quality, which is important." If you find yourself reading the same point multiple times, it might be AI at work.

**Generic or Vague Language**: AI sometimes avoids specifics and relies on filler phrases like, "This is an important area of research," "More research is needed," or "Studies have shown…" without citing real studies. This lack of detail can be a red flag.

**Flawless but Empty Prose**: AI-generated text may read smoothly but feel oddly superficial — like it’s saying a lot without really saying anything. It’s not always about poor grammar. Sometimes it’s about “lifeless prose,” which has words stitched together that have no meaning.

It is important to note that poor grammar or writing does not necessarily point to the use of AI.

## **Citation Red Flags**

AI often fabricates or mishandles citations. Here’s what to look out for:

**Non-existent References**: Often called “phantom references,” these look real at first glance but don't exist in databases like PubMed, Scopus, or Google Scholar. Always verify references to ensure they are legitimate.

**Incorrect DOIs or Journal Titles**: AI might assign the wrong journal to a given article or provide incorrect volume/issue/page numbers. Double-check these details to catch any discrepancies.

## **Content/Logic Red Flags**

AI can struggle with nuanced argumentation. Here are some signs:

**Contradictions Within the Text**: Saying one thing in one paragraph and the opposite later can indicate AI-generated content. Consistency is key in academic writing.

**Misuse of Technical Terms**: Using jargon incorrectly or awkwardly is a common AI mistake. If the technical language feels off, it might be worth a closer look.

**Overly Uniform Structure**: Every paragraph starts the same way or follows an oddly rigid pattern. This lack of variation can be a sign of AI involvement.

## **Visual or Data Red Flags**

If the manuscript includes figures or tables, be on the lookout for:

**Fabricated or Generic-looking Data**: Perfectly smooth trends where real-world data would have noise, or a lack of variability or error reporting, can indicate AI-generated data.

**Misleading Figure Captions**: Captions that describe a figure inaccurately or vaguely can be a sign of AI involvement.

## **Unusual Formatting or Meta Red Flags**

AI might accidentally leave behind weird signs such as:

**Repetitive Section Headers**: If section headers seem oddly repetitive or generic, the paper might be AI-generated.

**Placeholder Text**: Look out for text like "Insert Table 1 here" that hasn’t been replaced with actual content.

**Overuse of Keywords**: Keywords that are overused without integration into the narrative can be a sign of AI-generated content.

**Lack of Authorial Voice or Critical Analysis**: AI-generated text often lacks the unique voice and critical analysis that human authors provide.

## **What Can Editors Do if Suspicious?**

If you suspect a manuscript has been generated or heavily assisted by AI, here are some steps you can take:

*   **Verify References**: Check the legitimacy of all citations.
*   **Request Raw Data or Methodological Clarification**: Ask for additional details to ensure the research is sound.
*   **Recommend Careful Revision**: Suggest further clarification from authors.
*   **Ask for a Cover Letter**: Request a detailed cover letter outlining the research process.

## **Sage’s AI Guidance Resources**

For more detailed guidance, check out [Sage’s Artificial Intelligence Policy](https://www.sagepub.com/journals/editorial-policies/artificial-intelligence-policy) and [assistive and generative AI guidelines for authors](https://www.sagepub.com/about/policies/ai-author-guidelines). These resources can help you navigate the complexities of AI in academic publishing, and we recommend familiarizing yourself with them.

There is no single red flag that can definitively indicate AI-use, which is why human intervention through expert peer review remains the most effective way to monitor it. By staying vigilant and informed authors, peer reviewers, journal editors, and publishers can work together to help maintain the integrity of academic publishing in the age of AI.
